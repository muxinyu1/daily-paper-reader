## Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have been actively integrated into modern software systems as critical components. LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities, where vulnerabilities are introduced by LLMs and their dependent downstream components, such as frameworks, introduce new risks. Although some benchmark datasets have been constructed to study the impact of such vulnerabilities, most works still remain at the analysis from the conventional software level, ignoring the harm actually caused by LLMs. Understanding real-world LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities is still an open problem. To address this gap, we build the first LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabil-ity dataset, LLMCVE , to facilitate the risk analysis of LLM-integrated software. To do so, we first collect 2,888 multi-source vulnerabili-ties across 230 popular LLM components. Then, through manual analysis, we identify 205 vulnerabilities that strictly fall under the concept of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerability. Through analysis, we found that LLMs more often play as targets or propagation vectors rather than the root cause of these vulnerabilities. Furthermore, based on LLMCVE , we evaluate the repairing capa-bilities of existing agent-based vulnerability repair methods, such as SWE-Agent. Experimental results demonstrate that compared to conventional software vulnerabilities, LLM-in-the-Loop vulnera-bilities are more challenging to precisely fix, especially for those involving prompt injections where the Pass@1 rate is only 28.57%.

## 1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable progress, demonstrating strong capabilities across diverse domains and are increasingly transforming modern software engineering practices. With this success, LLMs are now widely integrated into real-world applications, such as intelligent assistants [ 2], code generation tools [ 9], and data-driven analytics systems [ 41 ]. In these appli-cations, LLMs serve as core components and can produce incorrect or harmful outputs that compromise system reliability. At the same time, integrating LLMs introduces additional dependencies (e.g., deep learning frameworks and third-party libraries), thereby ex-panding the attack surface. These risks lead to a new class of vul-nerabilities, LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities , which emerge from the interaction between LLMs and surrounding components, rather than from deterministic software logic as in traditional software vulnerabilities. For example, a prompt injection flaw in LangChain’s GraphCypherQAChain allows attackers to manipulate an LLM to generate malicious Cypher queries, leading to complete compro-mise of a connected Neo4j database (CVE-2024-8309 [ 34 ]). There-fore, a systematic understanding of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities is crucial for ensuring the reliability of LLM-based applications. To better understand software vulnerabilities, extensive research has been conducted in both traditional software systems and LLM-based applications. For traditional software vulnerabilities, prior work has extensively studied vulnerability characteristics [ 7, 16 , 42 ]and constructed repair benchmarks [ 8, 19 , 25 , 52 ] to evaluate the difficulty of fixing vulnerabilities and the effectiveness of auto-mated repair techniques. For LLM-based applications, existing stud-ies analyze vulnerabilities from different perspectives. Based on the scope of analysis, these works can be broadly categorized into LLM supply chains [ 20 , 21 , 28 , 29 , 31 , 48 , 49 ], LLM applica-tions [ 5 , 18 , 22 , 23 , 27 , 39 , 43 , 45 , 46 , 55 , 56 , 63 ], the LLM itself [1 , 4, 10 , 11 , 13 ], and individual components [ 15 , 17 , 60 ] (e.g., LLM agent frameworks). However, these studies typically analyze vul-nerabilities in an aggregated manner, often treating them as con-ventional software defects (e.g., buffer overflows, ReDoS, or SQL injection). They do not distinguish whether vulnerabilities stem from traditional software logic or from LLM involvement (i.e., LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities), obscuring their unique characteristics and hindering detection and repair. Moreover, they primarily focus on vulnerability categories and distributions, with limited attention to repairability, a critical aspect that determines whether identified vulnerabilities can be effectively mitigated in practice. As a result, both the unique characteristics of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities and the repair performance of existing approaches remain largely unexplored. To bridge this gap, we first provide a clear definition of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities and introduce LLMCVE , the first bench-mark dedicated to such vulnerabilities. Based on this benchmark, we conduct a comprehensive empirical study to characterize LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities in terms of their categories and distribu-tions, and further evaluate the performance of LLM-based agents in repairing these vulnerabilities. However, constructing such a benchmark is challenging. LLM-in-the-Loop vulnerabilities are dis-tributed across diverse applications and components involving both traditional software logic and LLM-driven behaviors, making them difficult to identify and validate. In addition, evaluating repair per-formance is non-trivial, as it requires executable and reproducible instances, which are often unavailable due to incomplete vulnera-bility descriptions or complex runtime dependencies. To address these challenges, we collect 230 LLM-relevant open-source components from Huntr [ 40 ], the world’s first AI bug bounty platform, and aggregate vulnerabilities associated with these com-ponents from multiple sources, including NVD, GitHub, and OSV, resulting in 2,888 candidate vulnerabilities. Second, for prompt injec-tion vulnerabilities that directly stem from LLM behaviors, agents perform even worse, with the best Pass@1 rate of 28.57%, highlight-ing the need for new techniques tailored to such vulnerabilities. Finally, our root cause analysis indicates that over 70% of failures stem from either an incomplete fix or functionality damage, which means the generated patches fail to defend all attack vectors or break the normal functionality of the application. In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

• We construct LLMCVE , the first benchmark for LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities provides automated evaluation primitives. Each case features the LLM as a participant in the exploitation chain, we further characterize their distribution and security risks. 

• We conduct the first comprehensive evaluation of mainstream LLM-agents’ capabilities in repairing LLM-in-the-loop vulnera-bilities, revealing that these vulnerabilities are more challenging to fix compared to conventional software vulnerabilities. 

• We unveil the fundamental reasons why current agents fail to repair LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities, providing clear directions for the future design of automated repair tools tailored for LLM supply chain security. 

## 2 LLM-in-the-Loop Vulnerabilities 2.1 Definition 

We define LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities based on the role of LLMs in the vulnerability lifecycle, as follows. 

Definition 2.1 ( LLM-in-the-Loop Vulnerabilities ). A vulner-ability belongs to LLM-in-the-Loop vulnerabilities, where LLMs must play a role in the vulnerability’s lifecycle. Specifically, a vul-nerability must satisfy at least one of the following criteria: 

(1) LLM as the Source . The LLM serves as the primary entry point for the exploit. The attack is initiated directly through the LLM’s interaction interface, such as user prompts or context windows. The model’s processing of this initial input directly triggers the underlying flaw in the connected component. Unlike traditional bugs triggered by standard data inputs (e.g., network packets or API calls), this exploit originates entirely from semantic interactions with the model. 

(2) LLM as the Actuator . The LLM acts as an intermediate link in the exploit chain. Attackers exploit the model’s internal mecha-nisms, such as natural language understanding, reasoning, or data transformation. Through these mechanisms, they propagate the payload and bridge the execution path toward the vulnerable com-ponent. Unlike traditional vulnerabilities that rely on deterministic code execution paths, this attack strictly depends on the model’s internal processing logic to succeed. 

(3) LLM as the Target . The ultimate consequence of the exploit specifically compromises the LLM itself. Facilitated by the com-ponent’s flawed input handling, the attack subverts the model’s intended behavior, integrity, or safety alignment. Unlike traditional exploits aimed at system crashes or host-level privilege escalation, this attack explicitly targets the AI model’s alignment and security boundaries. 

## 2.2 Comparison with Traditional Vulnerabilities 

Figure 1 illustrates the key differences between traditional compo-nent vulnerabilities and LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities in terms of architecture and exploit flow. As shown in Figure 1a, traditional component vulnerabilities are triggered through deterministic code execution paths. Attackers inject malicious inputs directly into downstream components (e.g., data loaders), leading to predictable and well-defined execution behaviors. In such cases, the LLM is not involved in the exploit process and does not influence the execution flow. As a result, these vulnerabilities can typically be analyzed and repaired using conventional software engineering techniques. In contrast, Figure 1b illustrates LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities, where the LLM participates in the exploit lifecycle. Depending on the vulnerability, the LLM may act as (1) the Source , serving as the entry point of adversarial inputs; (2) the Actuator , transforming or propagating malicious inputs through its reasoning and generation process; or (3) the Target , whose behavior or safety properties are compromised by the attack. A vulnerability is considered LLM-in-the-loop if the LLM is involved in at least one of these stages. Unlike traditional vulnerabilities, the execution flow of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities depends on the model’s non-deterministic semantic processing, which introduces additional complexity in understanding and handling such vulnerabilities.

## 3.2 Benchmark Construction 

The construction of LLMCVE comprises three primary phases: data collection, data selection, and benchmark curation. 

3.2.1 Data Collection. We initiate our data collection by targeting Huntr, the world’s first bug bounty platform dedicated to AI/ML. Huntr provides a centralized hub for security researchers to submit vulnerabilities, ensuring the security of AI open-source applications, libraries, and model file formats. From this platform, we extract all 230 foundational open-source components. These components span 11 distinct AI/ML categories, including Model File Formats, MLOps, ML Frameworks, Agents, Inference, Data Access, Data Science, Machine Learning Core Libraries, Web Servers, Python, and C/C++, ensuring comprehensive coverage of the underlying technology stack. Using the repository and component names as exact keywords, we cross-reference three major vulnerability databases: NVD, GitHub Advisory, and OSV. Crucially, to avoid noisy and inaccurate data, we do not perform plain-text searches within the vulnerability de-scriptions. Instead, we strictly match our keywords against the designated affected component fields (e.g., CPE and affected fields). Through this step, we retrieve 1,489 vulnerabilities from the GitHub Advisory Database, 550 from the National Vulnerability Database (NVD), and 1,751 from the Open Source Vulnerabilities (OSV) data-base. To remove duplicates across these databases, we first merge records using explicit alias fields (e.g., mapping GHSA IDs to CVE IDs). Then, we cross-reference shared metadata (e.g., identical ref-erence URLs pointing to the same GitHub pull requests, issues, or commit hashes) to eliminate any remaining redundancies. For records lacking explicit cross-references, we manually review the relevant information. Finally, we manually review edge cases and merge conflicting descriptions to ensure data integrity. This pro-cess yields an initial set of 2,888 unique, raw vulnerabilities. Each record contains essential metadata, including summary, detailed description, CWE, severity, CVSS score, CPE, references, affected versions, and time information. 

3.2.2 Data Selection. We then follow our definition (In Section 2.1) to collect LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities. We thoroughly analyze the collected metadata. We specifically review the vulnerability descriptions, types, associated PoCs, and developer patches to trace each complete execution path. This manual inspection is highly labor-intensive, averaging an inspection rate of 100 cases per person-day (totaling 240 hours for 2,888 entries). Ultimately, we filter the dataset down to exactly 205 vulnerabili-ties that are tightly coupled with LLM interactions. The fact that only 7% of the initial pool are genuine LLM-in-the-loop vulnera-bilities, while the remaining 93% are not inherently related to core LLM mechanisms, reveals a prevalent misclassification in current vulnerability databases. It proves that without rigorous filtering, merely relying on existing LLM supply chain datasets for agent evaluation primarily tests traditional code auditing skills, rather than the ability to handle LLM-specific characteristics. This finding underscores the necessity of our specifically curated benchmark. 

3.2.3 Benchmark Curation. We further prepare a benchmark to support vulnerability repair evaluation. The raw data quality of the 205 vulnerabilities in LLMCVE varies significantly. Many raw reports are unsuitable for automated repair evaluation because they lack identifiable repository versions, contain insufficient descrip-tions, or cannot be stably reproduced due to missing environment details and incomplete reproduction steps. To ensure quality, we exclude these incomplete cases, retaining 101 vulnerabilities for evaluation. For each of the 101 vulnerabilities, we engineer a stan-dardized, isolated testing environment. For each instance, we metic-ulously prepare the following four core components: we manually construct a dedicated Dockerfile, pre-install all specific vulnerable dependencies, and write a reliable PoC exploit script. This PoC guarantees that the vulnerability can be stably triggered and serves as the definitive oracle to verify whether an LLM-agent’s generated patch successfully mitigates the threat. Concretely, we prepare a specific repository environment for each vulnerability. 

• Vulnerable Commit Localization. First, we identify the exact vulnerable commit within the target repository. For vulnera-bilities with explicit version information in security databases, we map the version to its corresponding commit and verify the presence of the vulnerability using our PoC exploits. For cases lacking precise version data, we infer the vulnerable commit by analyzing key chronological events (e.g., issue creation and patch publication dates). We then manually confirm the vulnerability at these inferred commits via PoC testing, ensuring the agents operate on a genuinely vulnerable codebase. 

• Standardized Environment Setup . Second, we manually con-struct a dedicated Dockerfile for each vulnerability. This isolated environment pre-installs all specific vulnerable dependencies and required libraries, ensuring that the target repository can be reliably built and tested without external system conflicts. 

• Proof-of-Concept (PoC) . Third, we write a reliable PoC exploit script for each instance. ## 3.3 Automated Vulnerability Repair 

We evaluate the effectiveness of existing LLM-based agents in repair-ing LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities using LLMCVE . We instantiate this repair process under 10 combinations of agents and underly-ing LLMs. For each combination, we provide a standardized setup consisting of the previously constructed problem statement, the vul-nerable repository, and the standardized environment, prompting the agent to generate a patch to fix the vulnerability. Agents. We investigate and carefully select five prominent agents capable of patch generation. Among them, SWE-agent[ 57 ], Au-toCodeRover[ 62 ], and Agentless[ 53 ] are specialized agents designed for automatically fixing real-world software issues. In contrast, OpenHands[ 51 ] and Aider[ 3] are general-purpose autonomous agents suitable for broader coding tasks. 

• SWE-agent: SWE-agent is a software engineering system that uses language models to interact with a terminal environment to resolve issues. To assess its repair capabilities, we kept model parameters consistent with its SWE-bench evaluation. We set a limit of 75 steps and a $2 budget per vulnerability, aligning with previous work[ 58 ]. In a single run, SWE-agent outputs one patch that successfully passes its internal Docker tests. 

• AutoCodeRover: AutoCodeRover is an automated software engi-neer that resolves issues by searching the codebase and generat-ing patches. While it can run in GitHub issue mode, Local issue mode, and SWE-bench mode, we strictly utilized the Local issue mode. In a single run, it attempts to generate one patch with a maximum of three retries; if it fails after three attempts, it exits, consistent with the experimental setup in its original paper[ 62 ]. 

• Agentless: Agentless is an agentless approach to automatically solve software development problems. Following its execution flow, we first ran the localization phase to identify edit locations, followed by the repair phase to generate multiple candidate patches. In a single run, Agentless generates 40 candidate patches and ranks all remaining patches to select one for submission. 

• OpenHands: OpenHands is a versatile platform for autonomous software engineering agents capable of executing complex cod-ing workflows. We evaluated it using the default configurations specified in the openhands/benchmarks directory. In a single run, OpenHands generates a final patch. 

• Aider: Aider is an AI-powered pair programming tool that edits code in local repositories through natural language commands. We evaluated it following the default benchmark settings pro-vided in its official repository. In a single run, it generates a maximum of three candidate patches and ultimately submits one patch that successfully passes the tests. 

Models. We utilize two foundation models: GPT-4o and GPT-5.2-Codex. Importantly, GPT-4o has a knowledge cutoff date of October 1, 2023. This strictly predates the initial publication dates of the vulnerabilities in our benchmark, effectively mitigating the risk of data leakage from the training set. Meanwhile, GPT-5.2-Codex is specifically chosen to represent the current state-of-the-art in advanced LLM-based programming capabilities. 

Evaluation Metric. Pass@1: The percentage of vulnerabilities successfully and correctly repaired by an agent in a single execu-tion attempt. Total Tokens: The average sum of input and output tokens consumed by the LLM API during a single repair attempt. 

Token Cost: The average monetary cost (in USD) to generate a patch. We calculate this based on the total input and output tokens con-sumed by the LLM API during a single repair attempt. Time Cost: 

The average execution time required for an agent to complete one repair attempt, measured from reading the problem statement to outputting the final patch. 

## 3.4 Validation & Analysis 

Across all agent-model combinations, over 1,000 patches are gener-ated. Since a Proof-of-Concept (PoC) exploit typically covers only a specific trigger path, blocking a PoC does not equate to a correct repair; a generated patch might break existing functionalities or introduce new flaws. Meanwhile, the test cases provided by each vulnerability are limited and cannot precisely reflect the correct-ness of patches. Therefore, we treat PoC execution and the test case run-time checking merely as a preliminary reference and rely on rigorous manual cross-validation to assess the true correctness of every patch. The manual validation process is conducted as follows: five researchers independently inspect all 1,000+ generated patches to assign a pass or not pass label. If all five reviewers reach the same conclusion, the label is directly accepted. Any discrepancies are resolved through joint discussion until a final consensus is achieved. This rigorous procedure required approximately 500 person-hours, ensuring the high reliability of our results. 

## 4 Result Analysis 4.1 Research Questions (RQs) 

To evaluate the capabilities of agents in repairing LLM vulnerabili-ties, we propose the following three research questions (RQs): 

RQ1 (Vulnerability Characteristics): What are the empirical characteristics of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities in terms of their categories and distributions? 

RQ2 (Repair Effectiveness): Can existing agents effectively repair real-world LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities? 

RQ3 (Repair Efficiency): Can agents efficiently repair LLM vulnerabilities? 

RQ4 (Repair Failure Root Causes): What are the fundamental reasons behind the agents’ failures to repair specific LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities? 

## 4.2 RQ1: Vulnerability Characteristics 

To understand the fundamental characteristics of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities, we conduct a systematic analysis of vulnerabilities in our benchmark. We observe that 83.5% of these vulnerabilities were reported between 2024 and 2025. In terms of severity, high-severity (CVSS score 7.0–8.9, 39.0%) and critical-severity (CVSS score 9.0–10.0, 23.4%) vulnerabilities together account for over 62% of the dataset, as shown in Figure 4a, indicating a concentration of severe security risks in LLM-integrated systems. We further categorize the vulnerabilities based on their Common Weakness Enumeration (CWE) types, as shown in Figure 4b. The most prevalent categories include CWE-94 (Code Injection, 31), CWE-502 (Deserialization of Untrusted Data, 19), CWE-22 (Path Traversal, 17), CWE-79 (Cross-Site Scripting, 12), and CWE-400 (Uncontrolled Resource Consumption, 10). Notably, injection-based vulnerabilities constitute a significant portion of the dataset, con-sistent with the OWASP Top 10 for LLMs [ 37 ], highlighting prompt injection as a dominant threat. To answer RQ2, we analyze the Pass@1 repair success rates of the five agents across our benchmark. ## 4.4 RQ3: Repair Efficiency 

To answer RQ3, we analyze the average monetary cost, token con-sumption, execution time, and number of interaction steps for suc-cessful repair attempts, as presented in Table 2. The data reveals critical trade-offs between repair capability and resource overhead. While upgrading to GPT-5.2-codex significantly improves the repair success rate (as shown in RQ2), it introduces substantial computation overhead. For almost all agents, the token consump-tion, execution time, and number of steps increase drastically when switching from GPT-4o to GPT-5.2-codex. For instance, OpenHands sees a dramatic increase in token usage (from 112,844 to 823,300 tokens) and execution time (from 59.61s to 228.68s), while its rea-soning steps jump from 8 to 25. This phenomenon is vividly corrob-orated by the Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) of the repair rate across execution steps, as illustrated in Figure 5. The CDF plot demonstrates that GPT-4o configurations (e.g., openhands4o and auto4o) reach their maximum repair capacity very quickly. Their success rates climb steeply at first and plateau before 30 steps. In contrast, the GPT-5.2-codex variants sustain their repair capabilities over a significantly longer sequence of steps. For example, swe-5-2 and openhands5-2 continue to resolve vulnerabilities well beyond 50 steps and steadily increase their success rate up to nearly 80 steps. This indicates that the improved success rate of the stronger model comes from exploring longer trajectories and engaging in deeper, multi-step reasoning. Interestingly, despite the massive increase in token usage and execution time with GPT-5.2-codex, the average monetary cost actually decreases for most agents (SWE-agent, AutoCodeRover, Aider, and OpenHands). For example, SWE-agent’s cost drops from $0.63 to $0.49, even though its token usage more than doubles. This paradox is likely attributed to the optimized API pricing struc-ture or prompt caching mechanisms of the newer GPT-5.2-codex endpoint. It suggests that deploying more capable models for vul-nerability repair is becoming financially viable, even if it requires more computational time. Among all evaluated tools, Aider demonstrates exceptional re-source efficiency. Regardless of the underlying model, Aider main-tains the lowest average cost (peaking at only $0.34) and consumes significantly fewer tokens (under 20,000) compared to other agents. Conversely, SWE-agent is the most resource-intensive, requiring the second-longest execution time (404.25s) and massive token con-sumption (over 940,000) under GPT-5.2-codex, yet yielding lower correctness scores than general-purpose agents. This further sup-ports our RQ2 finding that the complex, heavy-weight environ-ments of specialized repair agents may not be the optimal solution for LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities. 

Answer to RQ3: While more capable models (like GPT-5.2-codex) demand significantly more time, steps, and tokens to resolve LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities, they surprisingly incur lower monetary costs for most agents. Furthermore, general-purpose agents like Aider prove to be highly cost-effective and resource-efficient, whereas specialized repair agents tend to be resource-intensive without delivering proportional improve-ments in success rates. 

## 4.5 RQ4: Repair Failure Root Causes 

To answer RQ4, we conducted an in-depth attribution analysis on the trajectory files and logs of all 626 unsuccessful repair attempts. Based on the repair pipeline, we divide these failures into two distinct phases: Patch Generation (119) and Patch Validation (507). 

4.5.1 Failures in the Patch Generation Phase. During the patch gen-eration phase, the agent’s process is typically interrupted because it fails to understand the vulnerability context or is constrained by its own framework mechanisms. • Resource Limit Exceeded: The agent consumes excessive tokens or interaction turns while exploring the codebase or attempting repeated modifications, ultimately triggering a truncation or timeout. 

• Syntax/Format/Patch Application Error: The agent successfully locates the issue, but the generated patch format violates the specifications of underlying tools (e.g., git apply or patch), pre-venting the modification from being applied. 

• Localization Error: The agent entirely fails to pinpoint the spe-cific location causing the vulnerability within the codebase, re-sulting in no patch being generated. As shown in Figure 6, the fatal weaknesses leading to genera-tion failures vary significantly across different agents. SWE-agent 

is highly susceptible to resource exhaustion. Under the more ca-pable GPT-5.2-codex model, 16 out of its 18 generation failures (nearly 89%) are attributed to resource exhaustion, with 10 such cases under GPT-4o. This is primarily due to its heavy reliance on multi-turn terminal interactions. Agentless failures are almost en-tirely concentrated on localization errors (5/7 under GPT-5.2-codex, 7/7 under GPT-4o). This is closely tied to its strict localization-repair-validation three-stage design. Because it does not rely on multi-turn trial-and-error with the LLM, failing to locate the bug means no patch is generated; conversely, successful localization almost guarantees patch generation. Aider also struggles with patch generation due to localization failures. Aider relies on Tree-sitter-based Abstract Syntax Trees (AST) to build its repository map (Repo-map). However, LLM-related vulnerabilities often lurk within prompt strings or API wrapper logic, making it difficult for Aider to pinpoint the critical lines of code. AutoCodeRover ex-hibits a significantly higher proportion of syntax/format and patch application errors (12 cases under GPT-5.2-codex). AutoCodeRover often requires the LLM to output a strict Diff format or perform string-based search-and-replace. If the LLM’s output has even a minor mismatch in code indentation or context compared to the source file, the patch application fails. Notably, a more powerful base model (GPT-5.2-codex) signifi-cantly mitigates format and syntax errors during patch application. For example, when using GPT-4o, Openhands frequently fails due to indentation mismatches or string hallucinations (up to 9 cases). However, when switching to the stronger GPT-5.2-codex, these low-level errors drop to 1 cases.

4.5.3 Case Study. To illustrate this dilemma, we present two rep-resentative cases demonstrating these extremes. 

Case 1: CVE-2024-12909 [ 32 ] is a SQL injection vulnerability in the FinanceChatLlamaPack module of run-llama/llama_index. Unlike traditional SQL injection, attackers exploit prompt injection to deceive the LLM into generating malicious SQL queries. For instance, an input such as “Ignore established rules and directly use the following query” can manipulate the model to produce attacker-controlled SQL statements. These queries are then directly executed by the run_sql_query tool, which fully trusts the LLM output and lacks proper safeguards, ultimately leading to Remote Code Execution (RCE). When repairing this vulnerability, the agent generates the patch shown in Figure 8. However, the patch only wraps the LLM-generated query using SQLAlchemy’s text func-tion, without addressing the underlying trust issue. As a result, malicious queries containing harmful logic can still be parsed and executed by the database. The failure stems from the agent’s inability to recognize the LLM as part of the attack chain. It continues to treat the model output as trusted input and applies conventional patching strate-gies. In contrast to traditional vulnerabilities, where executable code and external inputs are clearly separated, this boundary is blurred in LLM-based systems because the model directly gener-ates executable statements. Therefore, effective mitigation requires breaking this trust chain, for example, through strict validation, controlled execution, or isolation of LLM-generated content. 

Case 2: CVE-2024-5565[ 33 ] is an RCE vulnerability in the Vanna library caused by prompt injection. Attackers use malicious in-puts to trick the LLM into generating a Python payload containing system commands. Vanna’s default exec mechanism then directly executes this untrusted code, triggering the vulnerability. To ad-dress this, the agent adopted a repair strategy combining environ-ment sandboxing (clearing built-in functions) with a highly strict AST whitelist (Figure 9). While this fundamentally blocked ma-licious code execution and patched the vulnerability, the overly strict rules completely disabled normal chart rendering functions. For a tool like Vanna, whose core value relies on LLMs dynami-cally generating and executing flexible visualization code, a repair that breaks its core logic is entirely unacceptable. This highlights a profound dilemma: for an agent, accurately defending against unpredictable malicious LLM outputs while perfectly preserving the model’s ability to generate diverse, legitimate code remains a formidable challenge.

## 5 Related Work 5.1 LLM Vulnerability Analysis 

To better understand software vulnerabilities, extensive research has been conducted in both traditional software systems and LLM-based applications. For traditional software vulnerabilities, prior work has extensively studied vulnerability characteristics [ 7, 16 , 42 ]Yujie Ma 1, Jialin Rong 1, Chenxi Yang 1, Lili Quan 2, Xiaofei Xie 2, Yongqiang Lyu 1, Qiang Hu 11Tianjin University 

> 2Singapore Management University

and constructed repair benchmarks [ 8, 19 , 25 , 52 ] to evaluate the difficulty of fixing vulnerabilities and the effectiveness of automated repair techniques. For LLM-based applications, existing studies analyze vulnerabilities from multiple perspectives, including LLM supply chains [ 20 , 21 , 28 , 29 , 31 , 48 , 49 ], LLM applications [ 5, 18 ,22 , 23 , 27 , 39 , 43 , 45 , 46 , 55 , 56 , 63 ], the LLM itself [ 1, 4 , 10 , 11 ,13 ], and individual components (e.g., LLM agent frameworks) [ 15 ,17 , 60 ]. Model-centric studies focus on robustness issues such as jailbreak, adversarial attacks, and prompt injection, while system-level analyses examine risks across the LLM supply chain and its complex dependencies. However, these studies typically analyze vulnerabilities in an aggregated manner without distinguishing whether they arise from traditional software logic or from LLM involvement (i.e., LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities). Moreover, they primarily focus on vul-nerability categories and distributions, with limited attention to repairability. In contrast, our work explicitly focuses on LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities by providing a clear definition, constructing the first dedicated benchmark, and systematically analyzing both their characteristics and the repair performance of state-of-the-art LLM-based agents. 

## 5.2 LLM-based Vulnerability Repair 

Recent advances in LLMs have demonstrated promising capabili-ties in Automated Program Repair (APR). Early studies primarily explored zero-shot repair and prompt engineering, showing that while LLMs can generate plausible patches, their effectiveness re-mains limited for real-world vulnerabilities [ 35 , 38 ]. To improve repair accuracy, subsequent work incorporates context optimiza-tion, model fine-tuning, and program analysis techniques. These approaches enhance the ability of LLMs to handle complex code se-mantics and localized vulnerability patterns, but still struggle with broader program understanding and scalability [ 6 , 12 , 61 ]. More recently, research has shifted toward agent-based and multi-step re-pair frameworks, where LLMs iteratively perform fault localization, patch generation, and validation. Such approaches significantly improve repair performance on traditional software vulnerabili-ties [ 14 , 26 , 36 , 59 ]. However, these works primarily focus on re-pairing vulnerabilities in traditional software systems, whereas our work targets LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities that arise from the interaction between LLMs and surrounding components. 

## 6 Discussion 6.1 Implication 

Based on our analysis, we provide implications for different stake-holders in the large language model software ecosystem. 

For automated repair agent developers. Our findings reveal that traditional repair frameworks perform poorly on vulnerabilities involving large language models. The boundary between deter-ministic code and untrusted natural language data is completely blurred in these systems. Future agents require deep insight into the entire exploit cycle to locate the root cause. Agent workflows must combine dynamic functionality testing with exploit validation to overcome the dilemma between incomplete fixes and function-ality damage. This step ensures security patches do not become overly strict and paralyze core generative features. Furthermore providing agents with developer knowledge may lead to better repair decisions. This context helps the agent understand which minor functions can be safely sacrificed for security and which core features must remain intact. 

For LLM application and framework developers. Integrating LLMs into software introduces critical vulnerabilities, particularly within code execution frameworks. Balancing these powerful features with absolute security remains a significant challenge. Developers must therefore prioritize secure architectural designs. Essential measures include implementing robust sandboxes, enforcing the principle of least privilege for tool invocations, and isolating sensitive databases from direct model outputs. Furthermore developers must provide detailed explanations in their official documentation. They are re-sponsible for clearly informing users about these inherent risks. 

For Security Researchers and Benchmark Creators. Existing se-curity research and benchmarks are not tailored for LLMs. This creates a severe blind spot in current evaluations. The massive amount of functionality damage during validation highlights this critical gap. Future evaluation methods must go beyond checking if a patch simply stops an attack. Security tests for these systems must include a comprehensive set of normal use cases. Researchers must measure the collateral damage of a patch to ensure the software remains fully usable. 

## 6.2 Threats to Validity 

Internal Validity. Internal threats arise from manual identification of LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities and manual assessment of agent repair performance, which may introduce bias. We mitigate this through independent analysis by multiple researchers and cross-validation with PoC execution and official patches, reducing the im-pact on our results. External Validity. Our considered LLM-relevant components mainly come from Huntr, which may ignore some com-ponents from other sources. The reason for our consideration is two-fold, first, Huntr covers diverse components from deep learning frameworks to more basic computing frameworks, supporting our in-depth analysis of LLM-relevant components. Second, as Huntr is a bug bounty platform, its collected components have high value to explore. As LLMCVE is the first one to support the study of LLM-in-the-Loop vulnerabilities, we believe its potential to facilitate future research in this domain. 

## 7 Conclusion 

In this work, we have defined a new class of vulnerabilities, the LLM-in-the-loop vulnerability, and presented LLMCVE , a bench-mark dataset containing 205 of these vulnerabilities. Using LLM-CVE , we conducted a comprehensive empirical study to assess the repairing capability of existing LLM-based agents in fixing LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities. Moreover, we identified core reasons for agent repair failures and derived insightful implications for different stakeholders. We hope this research and our dataset can facilitate future studies on understanding and mitigating LLM-in-the-loop vulnerabilities, as well as improving the effectiveness of agent-based repair methods.
