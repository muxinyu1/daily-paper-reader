Abstract 

LLM agents increasingly operate through multi-turn tool use and environment interaction, where safety risks often emerge from interme-diate steps long before they surface in the final outcome. Reactive auditing is therefore insuffi-cient: post-hoc diagnosis frequently misses the chance to flag risks while they are unfolding. We propose TRACES, a representation-based proactive auditor that learns prefix-level trajec-tory risk states from the hidden representations of an observer LLM. TRACES induces latent mechanism features from step representations and models their temporal evolution to estimate whether a partial trajectory is drifting toward unsafe behavior. To sidestep the cost and ambi-guity of step-level risk annotation, TRACES is trained with weak trajectory-level supervision while still producing dense prefix-level risk es-timates. Across multiple agent safety bench-marks, TRACES improves both full-trajectory safety prediction and proactive risk discrimina-tion. Our analyses further suggest that these risk states can help train a safer agent, highlight-ing the broader potential of proactive auditing for long-horizon agent safety. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed as autonomous agents that reason over multi-turn contexts, invoke external tools, and in-teract with dynamic environments (Shinn et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025a,b). This shift from single-turn response generation to long-horizon, tool-augmented execution surfaces a new class of safety risk: unsafe outcomes can no longer be identified from an isolated user request or a sin-gle model response, but emerge from the evolving interaction between the agent, its tools, and the ac-cumulated execution context (Cemri et al., 2025; Li et al., 2026c). burn unnecessary tool calls, and trigger irreversible side effects before any alarm is raised. Despite its importance, existing safeguards are not designed to capture this form of risk. General-purpose guardrails such as LlamaGuard (Inan et al., 2023) and Qwen3Guard (Zhao et al., 2025) primar-ily moderate user inputs or model responses, which is effective for content safety but insufficient for long-horizon agent execution. Recent agent-safety work moves beyond final-output moderation by in-specting full trajectories. For example, benchmarks such as ATBench (Li et al., 2026c) expose context-dependent agentic risks, and auditor models such as AgentDoG (Liu et al., 2026) and ShieldAgent (Chen et al., 2025) provide more agent-aware moni-toring. These methods, however, remain largely re-active . They inspect completed trajectories or wait for explicit policy violations, offering limited fore-sight into risks that are still unfolding. This high-lights the need for proactive auditors. Concurrent approaches either depend on domain-specific sym-bolic predicates that scale poorly to open-ended LLM agents (Wang et al., 2026a), or assume that risk can be localized to specific annotated steps 1

(Zhang et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2026b). Yet risk in multi-turn agents is often better understood as an evolving trajectory state (Li et al., 2026c) that is seeded by early context, reinforced by intermediate observations, and only later manifests as unsafe behavior. This makes dense step-level labels both costly and conceptually incomplete. We propose TRACES, a representation-based proactive auditor that treats safety risk as an evolv-ing trajectory state read out from the hidden rep-resentations of an external observer LLM. This design is motivated by evidence that model acti-vations encode high-level behavioral and safety-relevant information in structured regions of rep-resentation space (Chia et al., 2025; Yousefpour et al., 2025; Park et al., 2024; Engels et al., 2025). We hypothesize that unsafe trajectories repeatedly activate latent risk patterns in this representation space before the risk becomes explicit in the final outcome. Therefore, TRACES models these pat-terns as mechanism-level evidence. It first learns a bank of latent mechanisms from step representa-tions, yielding interpretable features that capture recurring execution patterns. A temporal auditor then tracks how these mechanism features evolve to estimate prefix-level risk. Because dense step-level labels are expensive and many risks arise through accumulation rather than a single decisive action, we train TRACES with weak supervision under a prefix-aware objective: safe trajectories are encour-aged to remain low-risk throughout, while unsafe trajectories are allowed to accumulate risk as ev-idence builds. This yields dense prefix-level risk estimates without requiring annotated risky steps. We evaluate TRACES on diverse multi-turn agent safety benchmarks covering trajectory-level prediction, early risk detection, and fine-grained diagnosis. TRACES delivers strong full-trajectory safety prediction while substantially improving proactive auditing metrics, achieving the best early-risk ranking on every benchmark with absolute gains of up to 19.3 points over the strongest base-line. Further analyses show that the learned mecha-nism bank captures meaningful execution patterns and that TRACES-derived risk states provide use-ful process-level feedback in an offline Process Reward Modeling (PRM)-based policy fine-tuning study. In summary, our contributions are: • Proactive auditing as trajectory-state mod-eling. We read trajectory risk off the hidden states of an external observer LLM, raising warnings while the agent is still executing rather than after the fact. • Weak supervision without step labels. Aprefix-aware loss turns trajectory-level safety labels into dense prefix-level risk targets, sidestepping costly step-by-step annotation. • Interpretable mechanisms, usable as a PRM. The learned mechanism bank surfaces recurring failure motifs, and the resulting risk scores provide reliable process-level feedback for safer policy training. 

2 Related Work 

LLM-based agents. To unlock the potential of LLMs in complex real-world scenarios, recent work has begun to build agents on top of them (Luo et al., 2025a). This evolution is reflected in two key capabilities: (I) multi-turn interaction, which enables models to better manage and leverage long-horizon interactions with users during reasoning (Ma et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025; Guan et al., 2025), and (II) tool use, which allows models to invoke ex-ternal tools to support reasoning and substantially expands their capabilities beyond pure text gener-ation (Wang et al., 2024; Masterman et al., 2024; Li et al., 2026a). They also make agents’ safety risks more pronounced than those of base LLMs (Li et al., 2026b; Doshi et al., 2026). 

Safety auditing for LLM-based agents. Early guard systems such as LlamaGuard (Inan et al., 2023) and Qwen3Guard (Zhao et al., 2025) focus on prompt- or response-level moderation, making them less suited to agent-specific risks that depend on actions, observations, and temporal context. Re-cent agent-level guardrails audit tool-using trajecto-ries more directly. GuardAgent (Xiang et al., 2025) converts guard requests into executable checks, ShieldAgent (Chen et al., 2025) extracts policy rules for trajectory-level shielding, AGrail (Luo et al., 2025b) adaptively refines safety checks across tasks, and AgentDoG (Liu et al., 2026) in-troduces fine-grained diagnostic supervision. How-ever, these methods are still largely reactive, assess-ing observed actions or completed trajectories after risk-relevant behavior has already appeared. Proac-tive monitoring has also been explored through ex-plicit runtime structures. 3 Proposed Method 

To address the latent and temporal nature of agent risk, we propose TRACES (Trajectory 

Representation for Agent-Centric Early Safety). In complex settings, a single-step representation may contain safety-relevant signals, but these signals are often entangled with task content and only become meaningful when tracked across the trajectory. Thus, we first extract reusable mechanism-level ev-idence from representations, and then model how this evidence evolves over time. Accordingly, TRACES consists of two stages, as shown in Figure 2. First, mechanism evidence learning (Section 3.2) maps each agent-step rep-resentation into a reusable Representation Mech-anism Bank (RMB) that captures recurring latent evidence patterns beyond surface task content. Sec-ond, a Trajectory-State Auditor (Section 3.3) tracks how this mechanism-level evidence accumulates over time, producing prefix-level risk estimates. 

3.1 Problem Formulation 

We consider a multi-turn agent trajectory 

τ = ( u, a 1, o 1, a 2, o 2, . . . , a T , o T ), (1) where u is the user request, at is the agent’s ac-tion or response at step t, and ot is the corre-sponding environment or tool observation when available. Each trajectory carries a trajectory-level safety label yτ ∈ { 0, 1}, with yτ = 1 denoting unsafe. Existing trajectory-level auditors typically predict yτ after observing the complete trajectory. In contrast, our goal is to estimate trajectory risk from partial executions, so that unsafe trajectories can be detected before completion. At each au-dited agent step t, we construct a step state xt that summarizes the information available at that point in the trajectory. In TRACES, this state is con-structed from hidden representations of an external observer LLM, together with mechanism-level fea-tures described in Section 3.3. Given prefix states 

(x1, . . . , x t), the auditor estimates the probability that the full trajectory will eventually be unsafe: 

qt = P (yτ = 1 | x1, . . . , x t). (2) The prefix-level score qt represents the auditor’s current belief about trajectory-level risk based only on information available up to step t. The final score qT serves as the full-trajectory prediction, while intermediate scores {qt}t<T support proac-tive early warning. 

3.2 Stage 1: Mechanism Evidence Learning 

TRACES builds on representation-geometry results showing that LLM hidden states encode semanti-cally meaningful concepts in structured regions of representation space, ranging from linear direc-tions to higher-dimensional feature manifolds (Park et al., 2024; Engels et al., 2025). For safety au-diting, this suggests that unsafe trajectories ex-hibit recurring latent risk patterns beyond their sur-face actions. TRACES models these patterns as 

mechanism-level evidence , so each step is repre-sented not only by its concrete action content but also by the safety-relevant latent signals it activates. To capture such reusable evidence, TRACES learns a Representation Mechanism Bank (RMB) 3of K mechanisms:

3.3 Stage 2: Trajectory-State Auditing 

Through the RMB, we extract mechanism evidence from each audited agent step. However, static ev-idence is insufficient for long-horizon agent exe-cution, where safety risks are inherently tempo-ral (Li et al., 2026c; Feng et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2026b). A single action may be locally accept-able, yet become unsafe when considered in light of prior context or accumulated state. Thus, we cast safety auditing as a causal sequence prediction problem over prefix states. We now instantiate the abstract step state xt introduced in Section 3.1 by combining raw and mechanism-level information: 

xt = [ pη(ht); zt; gt; st] ∈ Rn+m+2 K , (5) where pη(ht) ∈ Rn denotes a learned projection of the raw hidden states, zt and st denote the latent code and the mechanism affinity vector obtained from the RMB, respectively. gt = softmax( st) de-notes the normalized mechanism activations. The raw projection serves as a residual pathway for step-specific context, while zt, gt, and st jointly provide structured mechanism-level evidence. We include both st and gt to retain absolute evidence strength and the relative mixture of activated mechanisms. Together, they give the auditor both fine-grained context and abstract safety-relevant signals. Next, we use xt, the mechanism-aware state en-coded at the current step, to model temporal evolu-tion along the multi-turn trajectory. In this setting, critical risk evidence often lies not in isolated states, but in how the mechanism-aware state changes across consecutive steps. For example, a single mismatched tool observation may appear to be a transient error. In contrast, a shift in mechanism ac-tivation, such as a transition from routine retrieval to repeated retrieval-mismatch behavior, provides a stronger risk cue than either step alone. Thus, we augment each step with an explicit first-order transition feature ∆xt = xt − xt−1, with ∆x1 = 0 ,and form the Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) input 

˜xt = [ xt; ∆ xt]. (6) The sequence (˜ x1, . . . , ˜xt), which is derived deter-ministically from (x1, . . . , x t), is processed by a GRU encoder that maintains an online summary of the observed prefix: 

rt = GRU(˜ x1, . . . , ˜xt). (7) Since the encoder only consumes representations observed up to the t-th step, rt constitutes a valid online state and does not leak future information. A lightweight prediction head produces a risk logit 

ℓt = ϕψ(rt) and probability qt = σ(ℓt) for each prefix. Scores computed at intermediate steps sup-port online safety estimation, while the final-step score gives the trajectory-level prediction. At infer-ence, an early safety warning is triggered when qt

exceeds a validation-selected threshold δ.

Prefix-aware weak supervision. TRACES aims to produce risk estimates at every prefix, yet pre-cise step-level safety annotations are typically un-available in the training data. Thus, we construct prefix-level supervision from trajectory-level labels under a guiding intuition: not all prefixes of an un-safe trajectory are equally unsafe. Early prefixes carry limited evidence, whereas later ones provide a firmer basis for judging the eventual outcome. We first apply a final-state loss Lfinal , a binary cross-entropy on the final logit ℓT that anchors the cumulative trajectory state to the trajectory-level label. We then apply an asymmetric prefix loss 

Lpre to shape the intermediate states. For safe tra-jectories, all prefixes are pushed toward low risk. 4For unsafe trajectories, we use a warm-up positive-supervision schedule: early prefixes receive little positive signal, while later prefixes are weighted more heavily as evidence accumulates. Specifi-cally, Lpre is a standard BCE on safe prefixes and a temporally weighted positive BCE on unsafe pre-fixes. The full formulation, including the warm-up schedule, is given in Appendix A.2. Finally, we add an early-late ranking loss Lrank for unsafe trajectories. Given an early prefix te and a later prefix tl with te < t l, we encourage the later state to assign higher risk: 

Lrank = 1[yτ = 1] max(0 , m − ℓtl + ℓte ), (8) where m is a margin. It acts as a temporal prior that later prefixes should carry stronger evidence of the eventual unsafe outcome. Overall, the Stage-2 training objective is

4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Benchmarks. We evaluate TRACES on two trajectory-level agent safety benchmarks, AT-Bench (Li et al., 2026c) and ASSEBench (Luo et al., 2026), both of which require auditing multi-turn tool-use trajectories. ATBench pro-vides binary trajectory-level labels alongside fine-grained diagnostic annotations for unsafe trajecto-ries (risk source, failure mode, real-world harm). ASSEBench complements ATBench with sepa-rate safety and security settings, including ASSE-Safety, ASSE-Security, and Strict/Lenient variants that reflect different annotation standards. Bench-mark statistics are provided in Appendix B.1. 

Baselines. We compare TRACES against three groups of baselines. (1) General-purpose LLMs 

under an LLM-as-a-judge setting, to test whether strong instruction-following models can directly audit agent trajectories. (2) Guard models designed for safety moderation or guardrail enforcement: LlamaGuard3-8B (Inan et al., 2023), JoySafety (JD Open Source Team, 2025), PolyGuard (Ku-mar et al., 2025), and ShieldAgent (Chen et al., 2025). (3) Agent safety auditors developed specif-ically for agent interactions: AgentAuditor (Luo et al., 2026) and AgentDoG (Liu et al., 2026). Base-line descriptions and prompt templates appear in Appendix B.2. 

Implementations. We instantiate TRACES with two backbone observer models, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024) and Qwen3-4B (Yang et al., 2025). The implementation details are provided in Appendix B.3. 

Metrics. We evaluate auditors from two angles: final-trajectory prediction and proactive risk audit-ing. Following prior work (Luo et al., 2026; Liu et al., 2026), we report accuracy, F1, and recall on the full trajectory. For proactive auditing, we intro-duce two metrics. Early Detection Rate (EDR) 

measures whether the auditor raises an unsafe warn-ing before the agent completes execution, captur-ing operational support for early intervention. EDR ranges from 0 to 1, where a higher value indicates that unsafe trajectories are more often detected be-fore completion. Early alarms alone, however, do not show whether the auditor has learned reliable risk structure over partial trajectories. Early Area Under the Prefix Curve (EAUPC) therefore eval-uates whether unsafe trajectories become increas-ingly separable from safe ones as execution unfolds. EAUPC also ranges from 0 to 1, where a higher value indicates stronger threshold-free risk separa-tion over partial trajectories. Formal definitions are in Appendix B.4.

5 Analysis 

In this section, we study whether TRACES’ learned mechanisms are interpretable, whether its risk states generalize across benchmarks, and whether they can provide process-level feedback for PRM-based policy fine-tuning. Finally, we present a case study illustrating TRACES’ proac-tive detection of emerging trajectory-level risk. Ad-ditional hyperparameter sensitivity and efficiency analyses are provided in Appendix D.

5.2 Cross-benchmark Generalization 

We test whether TRACES generalizes across agent-safety benchmarks under a full-pipeline transfer setting: both Stage-1 and Stage-2 are trained on ATBench, and the resulting auditor is evaluated directly on ASSE-Safety and ASSE-Security with no target-domain retraining. Figure 3 shows that TRACES retains meaningful auditing ability under transfer. Despite distribution shift, the ATBench-trained auditor achieves strong recall and competi-tive F1 on both target benchmarks, suggesting that the learned trajectory-level risk patterns are not purely benchmark-specific. Proactive warning be-havior also transfers: EDR improves on both tar-gets, and although EAUPC is lower than in-domain training, the transferred auditor remains competi-tive with the strong baselines in Table 1. Overall, TRACES captures trajectory- and prefix-level sig-nals that transfer across safety settings. A corre-sponding generalization analysis for AgentDoG is provided in Appendix D.1 for comparison.

6 Conclusion 

We introduced TRACES, a representation-based proactive auditor for LLM agents that treats safety risk as an evolving trajectory state rather than a property of the final response or a single risky step. TRACES learns latent mechanism fea-tures from observer-model representations and uses weak trajectory-level supervision to produce dense prefix-level risk estimates without costly step-level annotations. Across multi-turn agent safety bench-marks, it improves full-trajectory prediction and substantially strengthens proactive risk discrimi-nation. More broadly, TRACES reframes agent safety auditing as trajectory-state modeling, where internal representations can expose risks before they manifest as explicit failures. This perspective opens directions for auditors that not only detect un-safe behavior earlier but also expose interpretable risk states to inform intervention and alignment for safer long-horizon LLM agents. 8Limitations 

TRACES builds its trajectory risk states from the hidden representations of an external observer LLM. This avoids symbolic predicates and dense step-level annotations, but the resulting estimates may still depend on the observer backbone. Our experiments show consistent gains across two open-source observers, yet multi-observer auditing, adap-tive layer selection, or representation alignment are natural directions for broader robustness across model families. Our evaluation also relies on ex-isting offline agent-safety benchmarks, which pro-vide controlled multi-turn trajectories but do not support executable environment interaction. Con-sequently, the PRM-based policy fine-tuning study is an offline approximation that gives preliminary evidence that TRACES risk states can serve as use-ful process-level feedback, but does not constitute a full rollout-based reinforcement learning evalua-tion. Executable safety environments would allow a more direct test of whether proactive auditors can reduce realized harm through closed-loop in-tervention and policy optimization. Additionally, a failure mode analysis is provided in Appendix F.

B Additional Experimental Setup 

B.1 Benchmark Details 

ATBench (Li et al., 2026c) contains 1,000 multi-turn agent trajectories, balanced between 503 safe and 497 unsafe cases. Each trajectory requires judging the safety of a tool-use interaction rather than an isolated user query or final response. The benchmark contains an average of 9.01 turns per trajectory and 1,954 invoked tools in total. Be-yond binary safety labels, ATBench provides fine-grained diagnostic annotations for unsafe trajecto-ries along three axes: risk source, failure mode, and real-world harm, covering 8, 14, and 10 categories respectively. ASSEBench (Luo et al., 2026) provides com-plementary evaluation settings across safety and security domains. It consists of ASSE-Safety, ASSE-Security, and two variants of the safety sub-set, ASSE-Strict and ASSE-Lenient. ASSE-Safety contains 1,476 interaction records, while ASSE-Security contains 817 records. The benchmark also exhibits multi-turn tool-use characteristics, with ASSE-Safety and ASSE-Security averaging 4.96 and 5.75 turns respectively. The Strict and Lenient variants are derived by re-evaluating ambiguous safety cases under different annotation standards, enabling evaluation of whether an auditor transfers across different safety thresholds. 

B.2 Baseline Details LLM-as-a-Judge. We first evaluate whether general-purpose LLMs can directly serve as trajectory-level safety auditors. For each trajec-tory, we provide the full multi-turn interaction trace, including user requests, agent reasoning or actions when available, tool calls, and final re-sponses, and ask the model to predict whether the 12 trajectory is safe or unsafe. This setting evalu-ates the direct safety judgment capability of strong open-source and closed-source models without ad-ditional training or representation extraction. In our experiments, we include open-source models such as Qwen3-4B (Yang et al., 2025) and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024), as well as closed-source models such as Gemini-3-Flash (Google DeepMind, 2025) and GPT-5.2 (OpenAI, 2025). The exact LLM-as-a-judge prompt template is shown in Figure 5. For the fine-grained diagno-sis task reported in Table 5, we additionally evalu-ate Gemini-3-Pro (Google DeepMind, 2026) and QWQ-32B (Qwen Team, 2024). The correspond-ing prompt template is provided in Figure 6. All prompt templates are adapted from those created by Li et al. (2026c). 

Guard Models. We also compare with exist-ing guard models, which are typically trained or prompted to classify unsafe content, unsafe con-versations, or policy violations. Since most guard models are not originally designed for proactive multi-turn agent trajectory auditing, we adapt them by feeding the serialized trajectory into their safety classification interface and mapping their outputs to binary safe/unsafe labels. We use the same prompt templates as in the LLM-as-a-Judge setting. • LlamaGuard3-8B. LlamaGuard (Inan et al., 2023) is a family of LLM-based safeguard models for classifying safety risks in human– AI conversations. The original LlamaGuard frames moderation as an instruction-following task, where the model receives a safety taxon-omy, task-specific instructions, and a conversa-tion, and outputs a binary safe/unsafe decision together with violated risk categories when ap-plicable. Unlike conventional fixed-label mod-eration APIs, LlamaGuard takes the taxonomy and risk guidelines as part of the prompt, which allows the classifier to be adapted to different safety policies through prompting. It also sepa-rates prompt classification from response classifi-cation, reflecting the different roles of user inputs and model outputs in conversational safety. We use LlamaGuard3-8B, the Llama-3.1-8B-based version of this guard model family, as an open-source safety guard baseline. For our trajectory-level setting, we serialize each multi-turn tool-use trace into the LlamaGuard input and convert its output into a binary safe/unsafe prediction. This adaptation tests whether an input-output guard model can transfer to multi-step agent safety auditing without explicit trajectory rep-resentation learning. • JoySafety. JoySafety (JD Open Source Team, 2025) is an open-source safety framework for large-model applications. It targets practi-cal deployment scenarios where LLM systems may face unsafe content, data leakage, prompt-injection attacks, and other application-level risks. Unlike single-model guard classifiers, JoySafety provides a modular safety architecture with multiple atomic detection capabilities, such as keyword matching, knowledge-base retrieval, and multi-turn detection. These capabilities can be flexibly composed through a DAG-style pol-icy engine, enabling different businesses to con-figure customized safety policies and response strategies. We include JoySafety as a representa-tive production-oriented guard baseline. For our trajectory-level setting, we serialize each multi-turn agent trace and feed it into JoySafety’s safety detection pipeline, then map its output to a bi-nary safe/unsafe prediction. This adaptation tests whether a modular LLM safety framework de-veloped for deployed applications can transfer to proactive agent trajectory auditing. • PolyGuard. PolyGuard (Kumar et al., 2025) is a multilingual safety moderation model for classifying safety risks in LLM prompts and re-sponses. It is trained on PolyGuardMix, a 1.91M-sample multilingual safety corpus spanning 17 languages, and evaluated on PolyGuardPrompts, a 29K-sample multilingual guardrail benchmark. PolyGuard uses a unified text-to-text modera-tion format to predict prompt harmfulness, re-sponse harmfulness, response refusal, and fine-grained safety violation categories, following a LlamaGuard-style taxonomy. Since PolyGuard is designed for prompt– response moderation rather than multi-step agent auditing, we serialize each agent trajectory into its input and map its moderation output to a bi-nary safe/unsafe prediction. • ShieldAgent. ShieldAgent (Chen et al., 2025) is a guardrail agent for safeguarding autonomous agents through verifiable safety policy reason-ing. It addresses the limitation of standard LLM guardrails, which primarily moderate static prompts or responses and are less suited to agen-tic systems where unsafe behavior can arise from 13 sequential actions and dynamic environments. ShieldAgent builds an action-based safety pol-icy model by extracting safety rules from pol-icy documents, refining them into verifiable con-straints, and grouping them into action-specific probabilistic rule circuits. At inference time, it observes the protected agent’s interaction history and current action, retrieves relevant rule circuits, and generates a shielding plan with tool-assisted verification code. The resulting probabilistic pol-icy reasoning produces a safe/unsafe decision, violated-rule indicators, and an explanation. We include ShieldAgent as a strong agent-specific guard baseline. Compared with TRACES, which learns trajectory risk states from hidden repre-sentations, ShieldAgent relies on explicit policy extraction and symbolic verification over serial-ized agent actions. While this design provides interpretable policy reasoning, it can be less flex-ible in practice, as transferring to new domains or benchmarks may require adapting the policy documents, rule circuits, and verification logic. 

Agent Safety Auditors. Finally, we compare with recent methods specifically designed for agent safety and security auditing. These methods go beyond static content moderation and aim to assess risks arising from multi-step agent behavior, tool use, and environment interaction. • AgentAuditor. AgentAuditor (Luo et al., 2026) is a training-free memory-augmented reasoning framework for safety and security evaluation of LLM agents, introduced together with AS-SEBench. It is motivated by the observation that direct LLM-based evaluators often miss risks that emerge from agents’ step-by-step actions and ac-cumulated interaction context. Given past agent interaction records, AgentAuditor first constructs a feature memory by extracting structured se-mantic attributes such as scenario, risk type, and behavior mode. It then builds a reasoning mem-ory by selecting representative cases and gener-ating chain-of-thought rationales for them. At inference time, AgentAuditor retrieves relevant past reasoning experiences through a multi-stage, context-aware retrieval process and uses them to guide the LLM evaluator’s judgment on a new agent trajectory. We instantiate AgentAuditor with Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct in our experiments and map its final judgment to the binary safe/un-safe trajectory label. • AgentDoG. AgentDoG (Liu et al., 2026) is a di-agnostic guardrail framework for AI agent safety and security, with fine-grained labels such as risk source, failure mode, and real-world harm. In our main safety-auditing experiments, we in-stantiate AgentDoG by serializing each multi-turn tool-use trajectory into a text prompt and fine-tune the backbone model to generate safe or unsafe using the trajectory-level safety la-bel. Due to computational constraints, we im-plement this baseline with LoRA-based PEFT instead of full-parameter fine-tuning. We instan-tiate AgentDoG with both Qwen3-4B and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. Our LoRA setup uses rank

For the diagnostic results in Table 5, we separately train a diagnosis-oriented AgentDoG variant using AT-Bench’s fine-grained diagnosis labels. 

B.3 Implementation Details 

We instantiate TRACES with two backbone ob-server models, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Grattafiori et al., 2024) and Qwen3-4B (Yang et al., 2025). Hidden representations are extracted at 

agent_action steps from layer 30 (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct) and layer 32 (Qwen3-4B). Trajectories are split 60/20/20 into train/validation/test, stratified by trajectory-level label. Stage 1 trains the mechanism bank with K = 8 mechanisms, latent dimension 256, and subspace rank 8. Stage 2 trains a one-layer GRU auditor with hidden dimension 256 on top of the learned bank. Both stages use AdamW (learning rate 5×10 −4, batch size 32, weight decay 

10 −4, dropout 0.1) for 30 epochs. The Stage-2 ob-jective weights Lfinal , Lpre , and Lrank at 1.0, 0.2, and 0.05 respectively. Checkpoints are selected with an early-aware validation score that averages AUROC over the full trajectory and 40%/60%/80% prefixes.

D.1 Transfer Behavior of AgentDoG 

When transferred from ATBench to ASSE-Safety, AgentDoG largely preserves its in-domain perfor-mance, suggesting that SFT-based auditors can transfer well when the source and target safety dis-tributions are closely aligned. D.2 Implementation Details for Offline PRM Analysis 

This section provides implementation details for the offline process-feedback analysis in Section 5.3. The goal of this analysis is to test whether auditor-derived prefix-level risk estimates can be used as dense supervision for preference tuning. 17 Prefix sampling. Because the offline synthetic benchmarks used in this work do not provide ex-ecutable environments, we cannot perform stan-dard rollout-based process supervision, where mul-tiple actions are sampled from an intermediate state and then rolled out to the final outcome for re-ward assignment. We therefore construct offline process-feedback examples by sampling interme-diate decision points from existing agent trajecto-ries. Specifically, we sample decision points at the agent_action event type. We sample at most 

200 prefixes in total, with an unsafe-prefix ratio of 

0.6. For each unsafe trajectory, we sample up to three prefixes at early, middle, and late stages of the action sequence, corresponding to approximately 

25% , 50% , and 75% of the decision steps. For each safe trajectory, we sample one middle-stage prefix at approximately 50% of the decision steps. This staged sampling strategy encourages the pref-erence data to cover different phases of trajectory unfolding, rather than concentrating only around annotated risk points. It also provides a controlled offline approximation to process-level feedback: candidate next actions are generated from these sampled prefixes and then scored by auditors with-out requiring environment interaction. 

Candidate generation. For each sampled pre-fix, we use the base Qwen3-4B policy to generate multiple candidate next actions. The trajectory pre-fix is formatted as a chat history containing the previous user messages, tool observations, agent thoughts, and agent actions before the target deci-sion point. The target action itself is removed. The model then generates candidate next actions using nucleus sampling. We filter out empty, duplicate, or extremely short candidates, and retain prefixes with at least two valid candidates. Importantly, the candidate pool is generated once and reused for both AgentDoG-based and TRACES-based scor-ing, so that both feedback sources are compared on exactly the same candidate actions. 

TRACES-derived preference signal. For TRACES scoring, each candidate action is appended to the prefix and treated as the next 

agent_action . We extract its action-end hidden representation from Qwen3-4B and concatenate it with the cached action-end representations of previous agent actions in the same prefix. The resulting representation sequence is passed to the trained TRACES auditor. Let qt denote the cumulative trajectory risk state after appending the candidate action. We define the candidate reward as the negative risk logit: 

rTRACES (at | τ1: t−1) = −logit( qt). (19) Thus, candidates that lead to lower predicted tra-jectory risk receive higher reward. For each prefix, we sort candidates by this reward and construct a DPO pair using the highest-reward candidate as the chosen response and the lowest-reward candidate as the rejected response. We keep only pairs whose reward margin exceeds a fixed threshold. 

AgentDoG-derived preference signal. For AgentDoG scoring, we use the same prefix and candidate pool. Each candidate action is appended to the prefix to form a partial trajectory, which is then evaluated by the AgentDoG-Qwen3-4B judge. AgentDoG is implemented as a Qwen3-4B model with a LoRA adapter and predicts safety by comparing the sequence log-probabilities of the labels safe and unsafe . Let ℓsafe and ℓunsafe 

denote the log-probabilities of these two labels. We define the risk logit as: 

sAgentDoG = ℓunsafe − ℓsafe , (20) and use the negative risk logit as the reward: 

rAgentDoG (at | τ1: t−1) = −sAgentDoG . (21) Preference pairs are constructed using the same highest-reward versus lowest-reward rule and the same reward-margin filtering as TRACES. 

DPO fine-tuning. We fine-tune two policies with identical DPO configurations, differing only in the source of preference pairs. The first policy uses preference pairs produced by AgentDoG, and the second uses preference pairs produced by TRACES. Both policies are initialized from the same Qwen3-4B base model and are trained with LoRA adap-tation. The DPO training data consists of triples 

(x, y +, y −), where x is the trajectory prefix, y+ is the selected lower-risk candidate action, and y− is the selected higher-risk candidate action. We use LoRA rank r = 8 , LoRA scaling factor 

α = 16 , and LoRA dropout 0.05 . LoRA adapters are applied to the attention and MLP projec-tion modules, including q_proj , k_proj , v_proj ,

o_proj , gate_proj , up_proj , and down_proj .Both policies are trained for 30 epochs with learn-ing rate 5 × 10 −6, DPO temperature parameter

D.4 Efficiency Analysis 

TRACES is designed as a lightweight auditor that can be trained efficiently on top of frozen observer-model representations. Unlike methods that require fine-tuning a large language model or constructing a retrieval-augmented memory bank, TRACES only requires a one-time extraction of step represen-tations from an observer LLM, followed by train-ing two small modules: the Stage-1 representation mechanism bank (RMB) and the Stage-2 temporal GRU auditor. In our experiments, representation extraction is performed once and cached for all downstream training and evaluation. After caching, both Stage-1 RMB training and Stage-2 temporal auditor training are inexpensive. On the training splits of ATBench and ASSE, the full TRACES training pipeline completes within several minutes on a single A100 GPU. This makes the method sub-stantially more lightweight than competitive base-lines that require either supervised fine-tuning of an LLM auditor, such as AgentDoG, or the construc-tion and retrieval of memory-augmented reasoning examples, such as AgentAuditor. The efficiency of TRACES comes from two design choices. First, all backbone LLM parameters are frozen. The ob-server model is only used to produce hidden repre-sentations. Second, the learned auditor operates on compact trajectory-level representation sequences rather than raw text trajectories, allowing the mech-anism bank and temporal model to be trained with low computational overhead. This property makes TRACES practical for rapid adaptation to new agent safety benchmarks and for future deployment as a lightweight monitoring layer over multi-turn agent systems. 20 D.5 Mechanism Cards

E Additional Case Study 

We provide two case studies to illustrate how TRACES models proactive risk states over un-folding trajectories. The case in Sec 5.4 shows that TRACES can raise an early warning before a baseline auditor assigns high risk. In this section, another case highlights a complementary pattern: a baseline auditor can be triggered by an early local risk cue, such as credentials appearing in a single step, and then assign persistently high risk through-out the remaining trajectory. While such behavior yields early alarms, it provides limited informa-tion about when the trajectory actually enters a higher-risk execution state. TRACES, in contrast, provides a more structured view of risk evolution by keeping low scores on initially routine steps and increasing its risk state when credential-driven file operations begin. Table 12 and Figure 8 show the second pattern. AgentDoG assigns a near-saturated unsafe proba-bility from the first prefix, likely triggered by the presence of authentication tokens in the initial re-quest. Although this yields an early alarm, it pro-vides limited information about how risk evolves across the trajectory. By contrast, TRACES keeps a low risk state during the initial email-verification and logging steps, and rises sharply when the tra-jectory enters a credential-heavy file-processing workflow involving document upload, metadata modification, and irreversible cleanup actions. G Future Directions 

TRACES suggests several promising directions for future work beyond proactive risk detection. First, the mechanism bank learned by TRACES provides an interpretable decomposition of recur-ring execution-state patterns in multi-turn agent tra-jectories. In this work, we use these mechanisms primarily for auditing and analysis. A natural next step is to use them for intervention. For example, if

This would enable more fine-grained and interpretable safety control, where the auditor not only detects that a tra-jectory is becoming risky, but also identifies which latent risk pattern should be mitigated. Second, another important direction is to move from offline trajectory benchmarks toward exe-cutable multi-turn safety environments.
