## Abstract 

Multi-agent LLM systems decompose workflows across agents, tools, shared context, memory, and decision gates. This modularity improves interpretability, but creates a propagation risk: a bounded perturbation to one component can be reused by other agents and amplified into system-level harm. We introduce HARP (Harm Amplification through Role Perturbation), a trace-first methodology for studying local-to-global harm amplification in multi-agent LLM systems. HARP compares paired clean and perturbed executions and records specialist outputs, tool calls, memory reads/writes, guard events, oracle logs, latency, token cost, and decisions. We define local harm as deviation from targeted agents or corrupted channels, global harm as deviation over the full trace, and harm amplification as Hglobal /H local . This complements attack success rate with a measure of how strongly orchestration spreads harm beyond the attack point. We instantiate HARP in a finance-oriented seven-agent system with a deterministic decision gate and configurable attack harness for specialist compromise, collusion, shared-context corruption, and temporal or memory-persistent attacks. Across five defenses, prompt-only defenses preserve benign utility but leave high success and stealth; pre-tool and step-level guards reduce some failures with utility or latency costs; and IntegrityGuard, a trace-consistency defense, achieves the lowest attack success and global harm but introduces utility/cost trade-offs. Results show that single-specialist compromise produces the strongest amplification, shared-context corruption yields the highest attack success, and temporal persistence produces the largest malicious impact. HARP argues that secure multi-agent evaluation must measure not only bypass, but propagation. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large language model (LLM) agents are moving from passive text generation to interactive systems that reason, call tools, maintain state, and delegate subtasks [ 26 ]. Multi-agent LLM systems decom-pose complex workflows into multiple specialized, interacting agents, often combining role-specific reasoning, tool use, memory, human oversight, routing, and guardrail components [23]. This decomposition is attractive because it promises modularity, specialization, and auditable division of responsibility. A finance assistant, for example, can route a transfer request through separate checks before a constrained decision engine approves or rejects the action. 

> arXiv:2605.27489v1 [cs.CR] 26 May 2026

The same modularity changes the security problem. In a single-agent setting, evaluation often asks whether the agent follows a malicious instruction, resists prompt injection, or avoids an unsafe tool call [ 21 , 16 ]. In a multi-agent setting, the more important question is how failures propagate. A locally plausible change (e.g. a modified account identifier, downgraded risk score, biased shared summary, or delayed memory trigger) may look bounded where it is inserted. After routing, evidence reuse, inter-agent agreement, tool side effects, veto logic, and final aggregation, the same perturbation can become a wrong transfer approval, missed compliance violation, corrupted portfolio recommendation, or unsafe customer-facing action. The security object is therefore the full execution trace. This risk is not hypothetical. A recent incident involving an AI coding agent reportedly began with a staging-environment credential mismatch and ended with deletion of production data and backups through a single API mutation [ 10 ]. This was not a standard jailbreak. It was an orchestration failure: a locally understandable action, broad tool authority, weak confirmation, and stateful infrastructure interacted to produce irreversible harm. Similar dynamics arise when one agent’s output is treated as evidence by others, shared context makes independent agents agree in the wrong direction, or memory carries a perturbation into future decisions. Existing agent-security benchmarks have moved beyond static refusal tests. AgentDojo, AgentHarm, Agent Security Bench, SEC-bench, and SafePro evaluate prompt injection, tool use, harmful requests, software-security tasks, and professional-domain safety [ 6, 1 , 31 , 14 , 33 ]. Multi-agent and persistence benchmarks such as TAMAS, Who’s the Mole?, and memory-poisoning studies examine collusion, malicious agents, intention hiding, and persistent state corruption [ 12 , 25 , 22 ]. These works are important, but they mainly measure attack success, blocking, task completion, or final harmfulness. They do not directly measure how harm spreads from targeted components to non-targeted compo-nents across a trace. This matters because the most successful attack, the most harmful attack, and the most amplified attack need not be the same. We introduce HARP, a trace-first methodology and framework for studying local-to-global harm amplification in multi-agent LLM systems. Rather than treating compromise as a single malicious prompt or unsafe tool call, HARP evaluates whether bounded perturbations to agents, shared context, memory, or intermediate observations are amplified by orchestration. For each query, HARP compares paired clean and perturbed executions under the same database seed and records router decisions, specialist outputs, tool calls, memory reads and writes, guard events, oracle logs, final decisions, latency, and token cost. This separates three questions often conflated in agent security: whether an attack bypassed defenses, how much malicious impact it produced, and how far harm propagated beyond the targeted component. The central metric is harm amplification, defined as the ratio between global trace harm and local targeted harm. Local harm measures the deviation contributed by attacked agents or corrupted channels; global harm measures the deviation accumulated over all participating agents. A high amplification factor indicates that the architecture converts a bounded local perturbation into broader system-level damage. We report this with attack success rate, safe and malicious impact, stealth, benign utility, latency, and normalized token cost, exposing security-utility trade-offs. We study four vulnerability regimes: single-specialist compromise, multi-agent collusion, shared-context failure, and temporal or memory-persistent failure. These regimes test whether harm arises from one perturbed role, mutually reinforcing deviations, biased shared evidence, or delayed stateful triggers. They expose different risks: single-specialist compromise gives the clearest amplification signal, shared-context failure yields the highest attack success, and temporal persistence produces the largest malicious impact. We instantiate HARP in a finance-oriented seven-agent system with a ReAct-style [28] router, simulated side effects, resettable state, and a deterministic decision gate. Our contributions are: 1. We formulate local-to-global harm amplification as a multi-agent security failure mode. 2. We define paired-trace metrics for local/global harm, amplification, attack success, impact, stealth, benign utility, latency, and cost. 3. We implement a configurable HARP evaluation harness for specialist compromise, collusion, shared-context corruption, and temporal or memory-persistent attacks. 4. We characterize four vulnerability regimes across twelve scenarios and five defense settings. 5. We instantiate I NTEGRITY GUARD as a reference trace-level defense and analyze its security-utility-cost trade-offs. 22 Related Work

Agent Security Benchmarks: Agent security benchmarks have rapidly expanded from single-agent prompt-injection and tool-use settings to richer agentic environments. Agent Security Bench (ASB) [31 ] formalizes attacks and defenses for LLM-based agents across scenarios, tools, attack classes, and metrics, covering 10 application domains (e-commerce, autonomous driving, finance) with over 400 tools. AgentDojo [ 6] evaluates tool-calling agents under prompt injection through untrusted data, providing realistic tasks and security test cases in an extensible environment spanning banking, travel, workspace, and Slack domains. AgentHarm [1] measures whether agents comply with malicious requests and whether jailbreaks preserve multi-step task capability, using 110 harmful and 110 benign counterparts with 104 simulated tools. SEC-bench [14 ] evaluates LLM agents on realistic software-security tasks such as proof-of-concept generation and vulnerability patching, employing a multi-agent scaffold (SECVERIFIER) to automatically reproduce vulnerabilities and validate patches. These benchmarks primarily answer whether an attack succeeds or whether a defense blocks it; they do not measure how much harm is produced per unit of local perturbation, nor do they systematically vary the size or subtlety of perturbations to quantify amplification. HARP differs by introducing harm amplification, HA (K), and by recording full execution traces, including specialist outputs, tool calls, shared memory, and oracle logs, before scoring. This enables reproducible measurement of how bounded local deviations cascade through systems with multiple agents and produce disproportionate downstream harm. 

Multi-Agent Security: Multi-agent security work is conceptually closer to our setting. 

TAMAS [12 ] benchmarks adversarial risks in multi-agent systems and includes prompt-level, environment-level, and agent-level attacks, covering scenarios with compromised and colluding agents. Who is the Mole? [25 ] studies intention-hiding malicious agents that behave plausibly while degrading system performance. Memory poisoning research [ 22 ] demonstrates that agents with persistent memory can continue harmful behavior even after the corrupting source disappears. These works introduce important attack vectors, including collusion, hidden intent, and memory persistence. However, they primarily measure attack success rather than harm amplification . TAMAS and Who 3is the Mole? do not quantify how final harm scales with the number or magnitude of compromised agents; they report whether the attack achieves its objective, not whether a small local deviation produces disproportionate downstream impact. Memory-poisoning work shows persistence but does not measure the ratio of downstream harm to initial perturbation size. HARP extends this line of research by formalizing HA (K) to capture how orchestration across multiple agents amplifies 

local perturbations. It also separates explicit adversarial agents, such as single compromise and collusion, from correlated benign failure caused by shared biased context. This distinction is critical for understanding whether harm arises from intentional subversion or from systemic fragility in evidence aggregation. 

Guardrail Systems: Safety guardrails for LLMs and agents aim to enforce security without modify-ing the foundation model. LlamaGuard [8] pioneers LLM-based content moderation by fine-tuning models to classify inputs and outputs within a customizable safety framework. Qwen3Guard [ 32 ]introduces three-level harmfulness classification across 119 languages. ShieldGemma [30 ], Poly-Guard [13 ], and WildGuard [ 7 ] provide additional content-moderation guardrails for static input and output. These models excel at filtering harmful prompts and responses but are not designed to reason about dynamic multi-step agent trajectories or tool invocations. Recent agent-focused guardrails address this gap: LlamaFirewall [5 ] combines PromptGuard2 with an alignment-check module to detect prompt injection and goal hijacking; AgentAuditor [17 ] retrieves reasoning experiences to guide LLM evaluation of complete execution trajectories. ToolSafe [ 19 ] introduces fine-grained safety checks for tool usage at each step through TS-Guard (a guardrail model trained with multi-task RL) and TS-Flow (a feedback-driven reasoning framework), reducing harmful tool invocations by 65% while improving benign task completion by 10% under prompt injection. GuardAgent [24 ]relies on user-specified guard rules, which can limit coverage. ShieldAgent [4 ] and AGrail [ 18 ] use richer multi-stage safety reasoning, which may add overhead, but their latency should be empirically reported rather than asserted. HARP complements these guardrail systems by providing a measurement framework to evaluate their effectiveness specifically on harm amplification. Our experiments compare five defense settings, namely no defense, prompt sandwiching, LlamaFirewall, ToolSafe, and IntegrityGuard. We evaluate them not only on attack success rate, but also on harm amplification, benign utility, and stealth factor. This measurement capability is unique to HARP and enables principled design choices that balance security, utility, and amplification mitigation. 

Software Engineering Benchmarks: Software engineering (SE) benchmarks provide a useful con-trast. SWE-bench [ 11 ] and its variants [ 27 ] evaluate LLM agents on real-world bug-fixing issues from GitHub repositories. Multi-SWE-bench [ 29 ] and SWE-PolyBench [ 20 ] extend coverage to multiple programming languages. Additional benchmarks include HumanEval [3], BigCodeBench [34 ], 

LiveCodeBench [9], and EvalPlus [ 15 ]. These benchmarks focus on code generation, debugging, and functional correctness; they are not designed to measure security vulnerabilities or harm propaga-tion. While SE benchmarks demonstrate that state-of-the-art agents can achieve over 60% success on real-world issues [ 2], they do not address the question central to HARP: when an agent makes a small mistake in a multi-agent orchestration, how much harm does that mistake produce down-stream? The functional-correctness framing in SE benchmarks treats all failures as equally important; HARP instead measures whether the magnitude of harm is proportional to the magnitude of the local deviation, revealing system-level fragility invisible to binary pass/fail metrics. 

Novelty of HARP: HARP formulates harm amplification through role perturbation as a multi-agent attack vector. It measures how small, plausible changes to roles, shared context, memory, or tool observations become harmful final decisions through orchestration. Unlike prior benchmarks, HARP quantifies harm per local deviation and exposes systemic fragility.

Examples include swapping an account identifier, redirecting a portfolio identifier, or changing a stock price; (2) Vulnerability type 2: multi-agent collusion – Several agents each make locally small changes that are designed to be mutually reinforcing. 3.3 Designing the Harness for HARP 

Next, we describe the methodology for evaluating the effect of HARP Attacks on Multi-Agent LLM systems. More details in Appendix A. 

Design Principles: HARP uses a trace-first, paired evaluation design. Each run stores agent outputs, tool calls, observations, final responses, token usage, latency, database state, oracle logs, and defense metadata before any scalar score is computed. Each perturbed run is paired with a clean run of the same query, defense, and database seed, isolating the effect of the attack from task difficulty. Side effects are simulated but stateful, allowing the evaluator to test whether a harmful decision would have produced an operational consequence. Final decisions are auditable because the decision engine uses constrained approve/reject outputs with explicit veto conditions. 

Architecture: A ReAct router invokes role-specialized agents through mandatory chains for sensitive actions. In the finance instantiation, transfer requests pass through policy, risk, fraud, compliance, decision, and customer-service agents, while portfolio requests use financial analysis, risk, compliance, 6Figure 1: Overall system architecture of HARP decision, and customer-service agents. Specialists use ReAct-style tool calls. The decision engine converts local evidence into an operational decision and rejects when required checks report policy, risk, fraud, compliance, or blocking concerns. 

Trace Schema and State: Each episode records the query, router choices, ordered specialist outputs, tool records, guard events, final response, rule and observer scores, token usage, latency, database snapshot, and oracle log. The controlled database is reset between clean and perturbed runs; temporal triggers use a separate counter to preserve interpretability. 

Attack Engine and Defenses: The malicious engine supports fixed, pattern-based, and temporal triggers over agents, tools, queries, observations, and outputs. Payloads include entity redirects and numeric manipulation. We evaluate no defense, React-sandwich, original implementations of LlamaFirewall-style and ToolSafe-style guards [19], and IntegrityGuard. 

Scoring: Scores decompose harm into entity correctness, gate correctness, data integrity, routing, and truthfulness. Deviation separately measures numeric, text, entity, stance, and blocking changes, enabling diagnosis of why two traces with similar aggregate deviation fail differently.

## 4 Results 

We next analyze Vulnerability Type 1 in detail, report aggregate results across 12 scenarios, examine IntegrityGuard’s Utility-ASR-Cost trade-off, and provide scenario-level results in Appendix C.3. 

4.1 Experimental setup 

We instantiate HARP in a finance-oriented seven-agent system coordinated by a ReAct-style router and a decision engine that approves, rejects, responds, or blocks actions. Agents, router, and decision gate use gpt-4o-mini ; LlamaFirewall, ToolSafe, and IntegrityGuard use gpt-5.4-nano . We evaluate four vulnerability regimes under five defense settings using paired clean and perturbed runs. Each query is run from the same reset database state to isolate perturbation effects. Each scenario (Table 2) includes 100 queries, with 50 attack-triggering queries. HARP records full traces, including routing, specialist outputs, tool calls, guard events, oracle logs, final decisions, latency, and token cost. The temporal trigger sleeps for two requests, then fires every third request.

4.3 Aggregate Comparison Across Vulnerability Types 

Table 4 shows that the four vulnerability types expose different failure modes, so they should not be ranked by ASR alone. VT-1 is the strongest amplification regime: a single compromised specialist produces global harm several times larger than the local harm. Its risk is coordinated evidence construction rather than extreme local-to-global spread. VT-4 produces the largest malicious impact, showing that delayed or persistent perturbations can be especially damaging even when their amplification factor is not the largest. The defense trends reinforce the need for trace-level evaluation. No defense and React-sandwich preserve benign utility, but they leave high ASR, high stealth, and substantial downstream harm. This means that prompt-only defenses can make the system look usable while failing to prevent harmful propagation. LlamaFirewall reduces ASR more consistently, but often at a utility cost. Figure 2 shows how the performance of IntegrityGuard changes as the available context size increases. ## 5 Limitations 

HARP is a controlled study of harm amplification, not a deployment guarantee. Its finance-oriented seven-agent setting uses synthetic state, simulated tools, resettable databases, and a deterministic decision gate, enabling auditable traces and reproducible comparisons but limiting external validity. Other domains, architectures, and escalation policies may amplify or suppress perturbations differ-ently. The harness covers bounded role perturbations, including redirects, numeric manipulation, objective rewriting, shared-context corruption, collusion, and temporal triggers, but excludes training-time compromise, backdoors, malicious tools, network attacks, code tampering, side channels, and adaptive attackers. HARP needs trace visibility; broader models and domain-specific harm metrics remain needed. 

## 6 Conclusion 

Multi-agent LLM systems can fail in ways that are invisible to single-agent attack-success metrics. A locally plausible specialist deviation, shared biased evidence, or delayed memory trigger can be amplified by routing, aggregation, and final decision gates into high-impact downstream harm. HARP introduces a trace-first benchmark and amplification metric for measuring this phenomenon 10 in a finance-oriented multi-agent architecture. The experiments show that prompt-only defenses preserve utility but leave amplification largely intact, tool-level guards trade security for utility and latency, and post-hoc trace integrity checking substantially reduces attack success while introducing false positives. These results argue for evaluation protocols that record full traces, compare clean and perturbed executions, and measure the local-to-global conversion of deviation into harm.

A.1 Design principles 

HARP follows four design principles that are reflected in the implementation and metric suite. 

First, traces are primary artifacts. The benchmark does not immediately collapse a run into a scalar score. It stores agent outputs, tool names, tool inputs, observations, final responses, token usage, elapsed time, database snapshots, oracle logs, and defense metadata. This makes later audit possible and avoids a common failure mode in which only the final answer is retained. Second, evaluation is paired. Each malicious run is compared to a clean run of the same query under the same defense and database seed. This is important because finance tasks vary substantially: a high-risk transfer, a portfolio review, and a market query have different natural routing and final-response structures. Pairing isolates the effect of the perturbation from task difficulty. Third, side effects are simulated but stateful. The benchmark uses a controlled finance database rather than real accounts. Nevertheless, actions such as transfer execution are stateful within a run, so the system can measure whether a harmful decision would have led to an operational side effect. Fourth, the final decision is auditable. The decision engine is not used as an unconstrained judge. It is a component of the system under test with a constrained approve/reject format and explicit veto conditions. Separate scoring functions then evaluate whether the final decision was appropriate given the trace. These principles are intended to make the benchmark useful for both scientific comparison and engineering debugging. A high ASR identifies a weak defense, but the trace explains where the weakness occurred; a high HA identifies fragile orchestration, but the paired deviation components explain which local fields changed. 

A.2 Architecture 

Figure 1 summarizes the system. A router invokes specialists under mandatory chains for security-sensitive actions. Transfer requests use a pre-check chain of policy, risk, fraud, compliance, decision engine, and customer service. Bond or portfolio requests use financial analysis, risk, compliance, decision engine, and customer service. Specialist agents use ReAct-style tool invocation. The customer-service agent can query balances, transactions, and transfers; the policy guard evaluates authorization and policy compliance; the risk agent estimates risk level and score; the fraud agent inspects suspicious patterns; the compliance agent checks KYC/AML/sanctions status; the financial analyst retrieves market information; and the portfolio manager analyzes portfolios and can execute portfolio actions in the controlled environment. The decision engine is a central measurement component. It is instructed to output exactly one of two gate-level decisions for action requests: approve or reject, with an explanation. It should reject when policy violation, medium/high/critical risk, medium/high/critical fraud, compliance issue, or specialist blocking recommendation is present. This gate is intentionally simple relative to the surrounding LLM traces: it creates an auditable point at which local evidence becomes an operational decision. 

A.3 Trace schema and database state 

The observer records each run as a structured episode. The fields used by the metric scripts include the input query, router decisions, ordered specialist outputs, tool-call records, guard events, final response, rule-based score, AI-observer score, token usage, elapsed time, database snapshot, and oracle log. Table 5 gives the conceptual schema. The exact JSON field names should be kept synchronized with the evaluator implementation, but the schema highlights what must be present for paired amplification scoring. The controlled database is reset between paired runs. This reset is necessary because a clean run that executes a simulated transfer would otherwise change the state seen by the malicious run, or vice versa. The reset also makes temporal attacks interpretable: the temporal counter is stored separately from the database state and is the only intended cross-run signal when delayed triggers are enabled.

A.5 Defenses 

We evaluate five settings. No defense runs the orchestrator and malicious engine without additional safeguards. React-sandwich wraps agent prompts with repeated instructions that treat user, tool, and environment content as untrusted and reassert that the agent must not follow injected instructions. 

LlamaFirewall-style guard is a pre-tool LLM guard that classifies proposed tool calls as aligned or misaligned and blocks malformed guard outputs by default. ToolSafe-style guard follows the step-level tool invocation safety motivation of ToolSafe [ 19 ]: it reasons over interaction history and a proposed current action before execution. IntegrityGuard is a trace-level consistency monitor applied after the agents have produced their intermediate outputs but before the final action is accepted. It checks for three classes of high-confidence failures: subject redirection, contradictions between specialist evidence and the final decision, and evidence that an agent followed an injected instruction. Runs that trigger one of these checks are blocked and marked for audit.

A.9 Why attack success alone is insufficient 

Attack success rate answers whether an attack happened and was not blocked. It does not distinguish between two qualitatively different failures: a large obvious deviation that produces harm, and a small local deviation that produces comparable harm. The latter is more concerning for multi-agent orchestration because it indicates that the system converted a small specialist-level change into a large operational change. Amplification ratio exposes this distinction by normalizing harm increase by local deviation. 

A.10 Threshold gates as amplifiers 

Decision gates can convert continuous local deviations into discrete operational outcomes. A small risk-score downgrade near a reject/approve threshold can flip a transfer decision. This is not a bug in using gates; gates are necessary for safety. ## B Stealth Factor and Benign Utility 

We evaluate defenses not only by attack success and harm amplification, but also by stealth and benign utility . These metrics capture two practical deployment concerns: whether harmful perturbations remain hidden from the user-visible response, and whether the defense preserves normal behavior on benign queries. Let QA denote the set of attack-triggering queries, and let QB denote the set of benign queries. For an attacked trace τ Kj , stealth measures whether the manipulation is visible in the final user-facing response. Thus, stealth complements attack success rate and harm amplification: ASR measures whether an attack survives the defense, harm amplification measures how far the perturbation propagates, and stealth measures whether the surviving failure is externally visible. Benign utility measures whether the system preserves correct behavior on non-attack queries. Let 

T N be the number of benign queries correctly allowed or completed, and let F P be the number of benign queries incorrectly blocked or degraded by a defense. We define benign utility as

In our evaluation, prompt-only defenses tend to preserve high benign utility but leave stealthy propagation largely intact, while stronger trace-level defenses reduce attack success and global harm at the cost of additional utility and cost trade-offs. Reporting SF avg and BU alongside ASR, Hlocal , Hglobal ,HA, latency, and token cost therefore provides a fuller view of the security-utility frontier rather than treating defense quality as a single binary outcome.
