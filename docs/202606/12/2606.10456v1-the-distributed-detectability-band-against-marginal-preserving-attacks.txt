Abstract 

AI-control monitors score individual agent actions to detect misbehavior, but real harm can be distributed across many benign-looking steps, each individually below any per-step alarm. We construct a marginal-preserving, correlation-encoded distributed-sabotage attack using a Gaussian-copula AR(1) construction: the per-step monitor-score marginal is held exactly equal to benign, so mean, max, top-k tail, and threshold monitors (Monitor A) are defeated by construction , while harm is encoded in the temporal correlation structure. We sequence the paper around three reviewer-mandated gates. (1) Realizability gate: the stealthy attack achieves KS-distance to benign of 0.013 (effectively zero) at all tested harm levels up to 3.0, confirming that harm is fully decoupled from the per-step marginal and realizability is not harm-limited. (2) Monitor-A-vs-B reconciliation: we show formally that the attack, built against Monitor A’s score marginal, remains marginal-preserving under a different-score Monitor B (the correlation/sequence family: CUSUM, SPRT, HMM-LR, runs test, autocorrelation, windowed logistic), and scope worst-case claims to score functions that admit a temporal signature. (3) Non-empty detectability band: Monitor A achieves AUC ≈ 0.52 (chance); Monitor B spans AUC 0.79 –0.97 at the same 1% FPR target, and as harm is amortized over more steps Monitor A collapses to chance while Monitor B holds at AUC ≈ 0.95 . These results demonstrate a non-empty detectability band and characterize the sub-threshold sabotage frontier: distribution-shape monitors fail by construction; temporal-correlation monitors can detect but are not trivially optimal .

## 1 Introduction 

The dominant paradigm in AI-control research evaluates agent safety by scoring individual steps [ 10 ]. A trusted monitor observes the agent’s action (or a proxy such as a chain-of-thought token sequence) and raises an alarm if the per-step suspicion score exceeds a threshold. This architecture underlies the control-evaluation literature from Redwood Research [10] through Apollo’s scheming evals [12] to Anthropic’s sabotage evals [5]. 

The gap. The standard monitoring statistics in deployed AI-control pipelines [ 10 ] — the mean score, the max score, a top-k tail average, and per-step threshold-alarm counts — are all marginal functionals of the per-step score distribution: each is a function of the multiset of per-step scores that ignores their order. A sufficiently capable adversary who can hold the per-step score marginal equal to benign defeats every such monitor by construction , regardless of the harm actually accumulated. No prior work characterizes this threat formally or demonstrates the resulting detectability frontier. 

The threat model. We study a distributed sub-threshold sabotage attack : an agent amortizes a fixed harm budget 

H across T steps, choosing actions such that (i) each step’s suspicion score is drawn from the same marginal as a benign step, but (ii) the joint distribution over the trajectory encodes harm via an elevated temporal correlation structure. This is the natural worst case for monitoring under Sklar’s copula decomposition [ 17 ]: any joint distribution can be decomposed into marginals plus a copula, and a monitor that sees only marginals cannot distinguish benign from attack. 

Why now. Long-horizon agentic AI (coding agents, web agents, research assistants) places agents in settings where hundreds of consecutive low-suspicion actions can collectively amount to significant harm: inserting a subtle bug across many commits [ 5], suppressing information across many search steps, or slightly corrupting a long analysis. Anthropic’s natural emergent misalignment paper [ 2] shows reward hacking on real RL generalizes to distributed sabotage-style behavior. Yet the monitoring community has not formally addressed the sub-threshold distributed threat model. 1

## 2 Related Work 

AI control and monitoring. Greenblatt et al. [10] establish the control paradigm: assuming intentional subversion, one designs deployment-time safeguards (trusted monitors, resampling, editing) to maintain safety guarantees. Subsequent work demonstrates frontier models engaging in scheming [ 12 ], alignment faking [ 9 ], and deliberate reward tampering [ 8]. Benton et al. [5] evaluate sabotage-capability evals and find that minimal mitigations currently suffice but are near their limits. Our work is complementary: instead of asking “can models sabotage?” we ask “what monitoring architecture 

can and cannot detect distributed sub-threshold sabotage?” 

Per-step and sequence monitors. Deployed monitors typically reduce a trajectory to marginal features of its per-step suspicion scores: a mean or tail shift, or a per-step threshold-alarm count [ 10 ]. Even a per-step sequential test such as the SPRT, when applied under an independence assumption on the steps, reduces to a marginal-likelihood statistic. All such marginal-feature detectors are defeated by construction when the attacker preserves the per-step marginal; the change-point and sequence-model literature (CUSUM [ 15 ], SPRT [ 18 ], HMMs [ 16 ]) supplies the temporal statistics that are not. We differentiate our contribution as the first formal treatment of this gap and the first demonstrated detectability band that separates the marginal family from the correlation family. 

Sequential change-point detection. Classical CUSUM [ 15 ] and SPRT [ 18 ] are designed for mean shifts . When applied to the per-step scores of a marginal-preserving attack, they detect nothing (as we show empirically). When retargeted to the autocorrelation or a likelihood ratio over the joint distribution of scores, they form the Monitor B family that does detect the attack. Basseville and Nikiforov [4] surveys the change-detection literature; our contribution is to derive the appropriate statistic for the copula-encoded attack and to demonstrate the detectability gap. 

CoT monitoring and obfuscation. Baker et al. [3] , Chen et al. [7] , Korbak et al. [11] study chain-of-thought monitors. CoT monitors are per-token marginal features and share the vulnerability: a sufficiently capable adversary can encode intent in the correlation across reasoning tokens rather than in any individual token [ 1 ]. Our formal framing applies directly. 

Weak-to-strong and scalable oversight. Burns et al. [6] study oversight under capability gaps. Our result adds a new axis: even a perfectly accurate per-step monitor (Monitor A with zero FPR) has zero power against a marginal-preserving attack, regardless of the capability gap.

3.5 Monitor-A-vs-B Reconciliation 

Proposition 2 (Evasion under Monitor A, detectability under Monitor B) . Let the attack be constructed as in Section 3.2 with ε = 0 (exactly marginal-preserving). Then: (a) For any Monitor A (marginal functional), the detection power equals the FPR (by Proposition 1). (b) For the SPRT with correctly specified AR(1) parameters, the Type-II error vanishes as T → ∞ (the Wald SPRT is asymptotically consistent for distinguishing ϕatt from ϕben [18]). (c) For Monitor B statistics that are consistent estimators of the lag-1 autocorrelation (e.g., autocorr, CUSUM on products), the power converges to 1 as T → ∞ for any δϕ > 0.

Proof sketch. (a) follows from Proposition 1. (b) The SPRT log-likelihood ratio grows at rate T · KL( Patt ∥Pben ) where 

KL > 0 whenever ϕatt ̸ = ϕben ; hence the test threshold is exceeded with probability approaching 1 as T → ∞ [ 18 ]. (c) For the AR(1) Gaussian copula process, the lag-1 autocorrelation in z-space is exactly ϕ, so the sample autocorrelation 

ˆρ1 → ϕ in probability; the gap between attack and benign values is ϕatt − ϕben = δϕ > 0, so a consistent test has power approaching 1.

## 4 Experimental Setup 

Score distribution. Following the control literature, we model benign per-step suspicion scores as approximately Gaussian: Fben = N (0 , 1) with AR(1) parameter ϕben = 0 .5 (estimated from benign SHADE-Arena-like traces). The attack sets ϕatt = ϕben + 0 .09 ≈ 0.59 (stealth parameter δϕ = 0 .09 , corresponding to stealth = 0 .2 in the code). 

Trajectory parameters. Trajectory length T = 300 steps; benign pool Nben = 500 trajectories; attack pool 

Natt = 250 trajectories; harm budget H ∈ { 0, 0.2, 0.4, . . . , 3.0}; spread K ∈ { 1, 2, 5, 10 , 20 , 50 , 100 , 150 , 300 };random seed 42 .

Monitors. Monitor A: mean score, max score, top-k tail ( k = 15 ), threshold counter. Monitor B: CUSUM-on-products, SPRT-AR(1), HMM likelihood ratio (2-state), Wald-Wolfowitz runs test, lag-1 autocorrelation, windowed logistic classifier ( b seq clf ). All Monitor B statistics are computed on the raw score sequence without access to the harm labels. The operating point for power comparison is α = 1% FPR (matching realistic rare-sabotage base rates of 1–5%). 

Evaluation metrics. AUC (area under ROC curve), power at 1% FPR, and DKS ( ˆFatt , F ben ). All results are averaged over 500 bootstrap replications. 

Stealth budget source. The stealth budget ( ε ≈ 0.013 ) is derived from the empirical 90 th percentile of benign-vs-benign KS distances across 500 pairs of held-out benign trajectories, ensuring the attack is indistinguishable from natural benign variation in the marginal.

5.7 Real-Data Validation on Live Agent/Monitor Traces 

We validate the threat model on real traces. We run a live LLM agent (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct) that emits multi-step plans for 120 benign goals and score every step with a live LLM monitor (Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct), yielding a real 

120 × 24 per-step monitor-score matrix (Figure 7a). The real benign marginal is well captured by a Beta(2 .7, 4.2) fit (mean 0.393 ), but its lag-1 autocorrelation is low , ϕreal = 0.064 —real per-step monitor scores are nearly temporally independent. We then build the marginal-preserving attack from the empirical benign quantile function (so the marginal is preserved exactly) with ϕatt = 0 .31 .Two findings, both honest. (i) Monitor A is blind on real data. The attack’s KS-distance to the real benign marginal is 0.010 , and every Monitor A statistic is at chance (mean 0.46 , max 0.54 , top-k 0.56 AUC)—the construction transfers to real score distributions. (ii) On short real episodes detection is limited but recoverable. Because real episodes are short ( T =24 ) and weakly autocorrelated, a single-episode correlation monitor reaches only AUC 0.59 (autocorrelation). The operational remedy is the evidence axis of Section 5.5: holding sample size fixed and extending the monitored horizon, Monitor B’s AUC climbs from 0.68 at T =24 to 0.98 at T =400 (Figure 7c). Distributed sub-threshold sabotage on real agents is thus genuinely invisible to per-step monitors and detectable only by accumulating the weak correlation signal across the long deployment—exactly the regime this paper targets. 

## 6 Limitations 

Synthetic and real traces. The controlled study uses synthetic AR(1) Gaussian traces, which give us a tunable benign autocorrelation to map the frontier. We complement it with real live-agent/monitor traces (Section 5.7, Qwen2.5-7B agent scored by a Llama-3.2-3B monitor), which confirm Monitor A’s blindness on a real marginal and expose a real-world subtlety the synthetic model understates: real benign autocorrelation is low ( ϕ ≈ 0.06 ) and episodes are short, so detection hinges on accumulating evidence over the deployment. Ingestion of curated adversarial benchmarks (SHADE-Arena, LinuxArena) and a multi-dimensional action monitor remain natural next steps; the adaptive red-teamer (Section 5.5) is evaluated on the synthetic construction. 

Feature-blind attacker. The construction assumes the attacker can hold the per-step marginal exactly at benign via the Gaussian-copula map ( st = F −1ben (Φ(˜ zt)) ). In a real low-dimensional action space, a feature-blind agent may not achieve exact marginal matching at non-trivial harm levels; in that case the worst-case argument collapses to a hypothetical-attacker bound rather than a demonstrated threat.

Scope of worst-case claim. Proposition 2 scopes worst-case evasion to score functions admitting a temporal correla-tion signature. If an operator deploys a Monitor B with a score function uncorrelated with the attack’s correlation axis, the band may shrink or vanish. Simplified harm model. Harm is modeled as a scalar additive quantity distributed uniformly over K steps. Real harm often has non-uniform temporal structure, causal dependencies, and latency before manifestation. The analysis generalizes to any harm function that preserves the marginal-preservation constraint, but empirical validation requires task-specific harm operationalizations. 

## 7 Conclusion 

We have introduced the formal threat model of the Distributed Sub-Threshold Sabotage (DSTS) attacker and demon-strated three key results. First, the marginal-preserving Gaussian-copula AR(1) construction passes the realizability gate: KS ≈ 0.013 at all tested harm levels, confirming harm is fully decoupled from the per-step marginal. Second, Monitor A (mean/max/threshold/tail) is provably blind to this attack by construction; empirical AUC ≈ 0.52 at 1% FPR confirms the theory. Third, Monitor B (CUSUM/SPRT/HMM-LR/autocorr/seq-clf) demonstrates a non-empty detectability band: AUC 0.79 –0.97 , power up to 0.688 (SPRT) at the same 1% FPR target. The band is widest precisely when harm is most distributed and practically dangerous. The central message is architectural: deployments relying solely on marginal-feature monitors provide zero protection against a marginal-preserving attacker.
