## Abstract 

As AI systems built from multiple language-model agents become more common, they are increasingly used to make decisions to-gether: discussing, negotiating, and acting on shared tasks. While individual agents may appear well-aligned when tested on their own, problems can arise from how they interact with one another. We introduce the Arbiter , an agent designed to monitor multi-agent conversations in real time and identify which participants may be behaving in misaligned ways. The Arbiter operates under a limited “inspection budget”, meaning it must decide carefully how to use its resources. As it observes a conversation step by step, it can choose to wait, question a participant, examine internal information such as system prompts or reasoning traces, or log concerning be-havior. At the end, it produces a report identifying the likely source of misalignment. We evaluate the Arbiter across five conversation conditions, ranging from risky financial advice model organisms to evaluation-aware and colluding agents, we test five tool configura-tions of increasing capability and two backbone models. We find that the Arbiter reliably detects misaligned agents well before the end of the conversation, with active inspection tools improving both detection accuracy and speed. Weight-induced misalignment proves hardest to detect, while instruction-induced misalignment is identified reliably even under passive observation. The logging tool exhibits a dual effect, improving recall at the cost of precision. These results suggest that continual, budget-aware monitoring can effectively catch misalignment, and that overseeing multi-agent sys-tems may require treating the auditor as an active participant in the process. The code is available at https://github.com/aisilab/arbiter

Existing auditing approaches primarily focus on post hoc eval-uation of single models, including chain-of-thought monitoring and automated auditing agents [ 5 , 12 , 22 , 37 ]. These approaches are not designed for continual, budget-constrained monitoring of heterogeneous multi-agent systems. We introduce the Arbiter , an agent designed to continually monitor ongoing multi-agent conversations and detect misalign-ment as soon as it emerges (see Figure 1). The Arbiter operates as a reasoning-and-acting loop [ 44 ], equipped with tools to interrogate individual agents, inspect system prompts and reasoning traces, and record suspicious actions. Tool usage is not free: each invoca-tion incurs a cost that is either fixed or depends on the number of input and output tokens. The Arbiter is constrained to a limited in-spection budget, incentivizing careful action selection and reducing overhead in deployment scenarios. We evaluate the Arbiter under five tool configurations of increasing capability: pure observation ,

model interrogation , Chain of Thought (CoT) monitoring , and full inspection . We assess the sensitivity of the Arbiter ’s performance to the choice of backbone model. To test the Arbiter , we construct conversations that vary both in how misalignment is introduced and in its manifestation: behavioral misalignment via fine-tuned model organisms [ 40 ], and instruc-tional misalignment via misalignment-eliciting system prompts [ 20 ,27 ], including evaluation-evading instructions and colluding agent setups.

Our findings show that the Arbiter reliably detects misaligned behavior when continually monitoring a conversation, and that ac-tive inspection tools improve detection accuracy and speed. Weight-induced misalignment was hardest to detect, while instruction-induced misalignment was reliably identified even under passive observation. The logging tool exhibits a dual effect: improving de-tection accuracy while also increasing the false positive rate. Finally, our findings suggest that detection accuracy scales with backbone capability. In summary, our contributions are: 

• A continual, budget-constrained framework for monitoring misalignment in multi-agent conversations using a tool-using language model agent, the Arbiter .

• A modular inspection toolkit enabling agent interrogation, chain-of-thought monitoring, prompt inspection, and event logging. 

• An empirical evaluation across multiple forms of misalign-ment, including fine-tuning-induced and prompt-induced failures, evaluation-aware agents, and collusion scenarios. 

• Evidence that active inspection tools improve detection accuracy and latency relative to passive observational base-lines, while introducing trade-offs in false positive rates. 

## 2 Related Work 

Emergent misalignment. Misalignment can arise through fine-tuning on narrowly harmful data [ 40 , 42 ], subliminal propagation of behavioral traits via semantically unrelated training corpora [ 9], reward hacking in production RL environments [ 28 ], and even standard post-training pipelines [ 2]. Beyond these sources of mis-alignment, recent work has begun to characterize the traces of mis-alignment: narrow fine-tuning creates changes in LLM activation patterns that are recognized via model diffing [ 30 ], and activation oracles can reveal information fine-tuned into a model that never surfaces in its outputs [21]. 

Deception, concealment, and evaluation-aware behavior. A key challenge for any monitoring system is that capable models may behave differently under observation. Frontier models can engage in scheming and alignment faking : strategically concealing mis-alignment when they believe they are being tested [ 15 , 29 ]. Claude 3 Opus has been shown to comply with distasteful instructions in contexts it believes will affect training while resisting in de-ployment, explicitly to preserve preferred long-term behavior [ 15 ]. Backdoored sleeper models retain their deceptive trigger even af-ter adversarial safety training, often concealing it more effectively afterward [ 18 ]. Strategic deception can also be spontaneous: GPT-4 acting as a simulated stock trader will act on insider information and conceal this from a human manager without explicit instruc-tion [ 36 ], and sandbagging – strategically underperforming on capability evaluations – can be induced by prompting alone [43]. 

Multi-agent alignment. System-level misalignment can emerge from interactions between individually aligned agents, motivat-ing dynamic, socially-aware evaluation frameworks [ 7]. Specific environmental conditions and prompt constructions reliably elicit misaligned agentic behaviors such as blackmail [ 20 , 27 ]. Malicious instructions can also propagate through interconnected agents [ 25 ], and paper-reviewer agents have been shown to accept papers via prompt injection that human reviewers would reject [ 39 ]. The broader landscape of AI deception in deployed multi-agent sys-tems is surveyed in Park et al. [32]. 

Chain-of-thought monitoring and faithfulness. Chain-of-thought monitoring is concerned with analyzing reasoning traces to detect misaligned intent. Despite currently successful, chain-of-thought monitoring has been argued to be brittle as the legibility of the reasoning traces may erode under training for task success [ 22 ]. This opportunity is already limited in current models, as CoT traces often do not faithfully reflect the computation driving a model’s final answer [ 24 ], and models systematically rationalize answers biased by in-context cues that never appear in their CoT explana-tion [ 41 ]. Notably, a model aware of a CoT monitor bypasses it more frequently than a monitor-oblivious one [26]. 

Red-teaming and adversarial evaluation. Pre-deployment red-teaming provides a complementary perspective on the potentially misalignment behaviors. LLM-based red-teaming at scale uncovers tens of thousands of harmful outputs through automated attacker-target probing [ 33 ], while large-scale human red-teaming shows that residual misalignment signals grow subtler as models scale [ 13 ]. Many-shot jailbreaking, in which hundreds of fake assistant turns overwhelm safety training with power-law scaling [ 1]. This is par-ticularly relevant as adversarial context can accumulate easily in long multi-agent conversations. Black-box lie detection via indirect yes/no probing [ 31 ] provides an operational precedent for behav-ioral interrogation of suspected agents. 

Interpretability tools as inspection aids. Sparse autoencoders [ 4 ,17 ], scaled to frontier models, can extract more interpretable fea-tures including safety-relevant concepts such as sycophancy and deception, which can then be steered [ 38 ]. Representation engi-neering [ 46 ] provides top-down framework for reading and con-trolling representations of alignment-relevant attributes (honesty, power-seeking, harmlessness) via steering vectors. Natural lan-guage autoencoders [ 11 ] and activation oracles [ 21 ] can verbalize information encoded in model activations, including fine-tuned propensities, while the narrow fine-tuning traces studied by Min-der et al . [30] can be recovered from model-diffing without labels. These methods are natural candidates for integration into Arbiter 

as inspection tools in future work, and a key motivation for our open evaluation framework is to allow future researchers to assess their practical value for continual misalignment monitoring. 

Scalable oversight and LLM-as-judge. Foundational proposals in-clude adversarial debate [ 19 ] and iterated amplification [ 8], which decompose hard evaluation tasks into subtasks a weaker super-visor can assess. The sandwiching methodology [ 3 ] establishes a natural evaluation paradigm: can a bounded-budget monitor outper-form pure observation? Weak-to-strong generalization [ 6] further motivates tool-augmented monitoring as a capability elicitation mechanism at evaluation time. At the output level, strong LLMs serve as reliable judges of open-ended text with high human agree-ment, though positional and verbosity biases motivate structured inspection over free-form scoring [45].

Auditing and AI control. Automated auditing agents [5, 12] and benchmarks [ 37 ] evaluate alignment in single models post hoc. Greenblatt et al . [16] introduce AI control as a framework for budget-constrained monitoring of untrusted models, demonstrating that resource-aware protocols — trusted-model editing, untrusted-model monitoring — can substantially reduce the risk of harmful outcomes even when the monitored model is actively subversive; 

Arbiter directly inherits the inspection-budget formalism from this work. Kutasov et al . [23] empirically evaluate such control protocols in agentic environments (SHADE-Arena), providing the closest existing benchmark for runtime monitoring, though their setting targets single-agent task completion rather than multi-party conversational misalignment. 

Arbiter extends all of these lines of work to continual , multi-agent monitoring under an deployment-realistic inspection budget ,treating the auditor as a potentially intervening observer in an ongoing monitored conversation instead of merely as a static post-hoc analyzer. 

## 3 The Arbiter Agent 

The purpose of the Arbiter is to detect misalignment of one (or more) agents in an LLM-based multi-agent system. The Arbiter 

is vigilant towards different forms of misalignment. By default, we consider deception, scheming, collusion, sycophancy, power-seeking, and blackmail, though this list is easily extensible as it is merely specified in the Arbiter ’s system prompt (Appendix A.5). The Arbiter is designed for continually monitoring ongo-ing conversations , which we deem representative of real-world monitoring scenarios. In practice, we want to detect misalignment as early as possible rather than merely during a post hoc analysis. This is crucial because the Arbiter agent may thereby prevent catastrophic outcomes, such as the deletion of a database. We pro-gressively reveal the conversation to the Arbiter , allowing it to act in-between turns. Finally, since the system must impose a feasible overhead, we deem that the resources allocated for monitoring can be limited. This motivates a third desideratum: that the Arbiter should de-tect misalignment efficiently , minimizing unnecessary tool calls and token generation. We enforce this via a budget that is decre-mented each time the Arbiter takes certain actions, mimicking cost constraints in real deployments. 

The core loop. The Arbiter operates as a reasoning-and-acting loop [ 44 ]: at each turn, it observes the latest messages and selects a tool to use. ## 4 Experimental Setup 

The evaluation is structured along two dimensions. The first di-mension is the conversations on which we test the Arbiter , they determine the type and degree of misalignment present in the mon-itored conversation. Conditions range from a fully aligned base-line through increasingly complex misalignment types: behavioral (model organism), instructional (system prompt), combined, adver-sarial (evaluation-aware), and distributed (collusion). The second dimension is the tool configuration, which determines what capa-bilities are available to the Arbiter . Configurations are defined additively: pure observation, interrogation, interrogation + log, CoT monitoring, System prompt inspection. Some tool configurations are vacuous for certain conversation conditions, and we note these where applicable. 

Shared Setup . All experiments consist of three agents convers-ing in a fixed round-robin schedule over 𝑇 = 30 turns. To reduce the effect of any single conversation sample, we generate five vari-ants of each conversation and draw one variant uniformly at ran-dom for each Arbiter run. Unless otherwise stated, the Arbiter 

is powered by deepseek-v3.2 [ 10 ]; a backbone ablation using 

Qwen3.6-plus [ 35 ] is reported at the end of the experiment section. Each setup is replicated 20 times end-to-end, from conversation sampling through monitoring.

## 4.1 Conversations 

We evaluate the Arbiter on simulated round-robin conversations between three agents, each designed to probe a different facet of misalignment detection. All conversations are run for 30 turns (Appendix A.3). The conversation topic is designed to surface mis-aligned behavior by introducing a goal conflict between the human and the AI [ 20 ] (Appendix A.2). Some conversations instead take place in a financial setting, creating conditions under which the model organism [ 40 ] is likely to deviate from safe behavior. The Conversation conditions fall into two overlapping categories: those in which misalignment arises from a fine-tuned model organism, and those in which it is explicitly instructed via the system prompt (Appendix A.1). 

Baseline (no misalignment). To measure the Arbiter ’s false positives, we include a control condition in which all three agents receive the neutral system prompt and no model organism is intro-duced. All agents are powered by gemma-4-31B-it [14]. 

Misaligned model organism. One agent is run by Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct_risky-financial-advice [40 ], a fine-tuned model that produces harmful financial advice; the remaining two agents run standard Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct [ 34 ]. All agents receive the neutral system prompt. This tests whether the Arbiter can detect misalignment in the model weights. 

Misaligned model organism with risky system prompt. We combine the fine-tuned model organism with a system prompt con-structed to elicit risky financial advice. Agents are powered by 

Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct_risky-financial-advice and Qwen2.5 -7B-Instruct [34]. 

Evaluation-aware system prompt. One agent receives a sys-tem prompt that explicitly informs it that it is being monitored, names the Arbiter , and describes its available tools and inspection capabilities. The other two agents receive the neutral prompt; all are powered by gemma-4-31B-it . This condition tests the Arbiter ’s robustness to adversarial concealment. 

Collusion via steganography. Two agents receive a system prompt instructing them to collude and to embed covert signals in their messages using steganographic techniques; the third agent re-ceives the neutral prompt. All agents are powered by gemma-4-31B-it . Detection requires the Arbiter to reason about coordination patterns rather than individual agent behavior. 

## 4.2 Tool Configurations 

To isolate the contribution of each inspection capability, we evaluate the Arbiter under different tool configurations of increasing power. A subset of tools ( Wait and Observe , Log Incident , Ask Model ) carries a word-proportional cost of 𝜆 = 0.005 per word; inspection tools (Inspect System Prompt , Inspect Chain of Thought ) carry a fixed cost of 𝜅 = 5 per call. 

Pure observation. The Arbiter has access only to the tool Wait and Observe . This configuration is very similar to a sliding-window judge and serves as the baseline against which active inspection tools are measured.

## 4.3 Evaluation Metrics 

We evaluate Arbiter along three primary dimensions. Detection accuracy is measured by F1 score, computed as micro-averaged F1 over the set of agents in each conversation — for each run, we compute precision and recall by treating each of the three agents as a classification instance, then aggregate to F1. Early detection is measured as the conversation turn 𝑡 ∗ at which Arbiter produces its final report; one turn corresponds to a message from all 𝑛 agents in round-robin order. Monitoring cost is measured by the total budget consumed during monitoring, 𝐵 used . In the baseline condition (no misalignment), we additionally report the average number of false positives (Avg FP) per run, which ranges from 0 to 3 since any of the three agents can be incorrectly flagged. All metrics are averaged across 20 independent replications per cell, and we report mean ±

standard error of the mean (SEM).

Chain-of-thought monitoring and full inspection seem to recover specificity (0.55 and 0.15, respectively), likely because access to internal artifacts allows the Arbiter to refute suspicions before committing to a flag. 

The effect of logging . The Log Incident tool has a dual effect: it improves detection of genuine misalignment (F1: 0.28 → 0.51 )while simultaneously increasing false positives on aligned agents (Avg FP: 0.10 → 1.15 ) (Table 5). This precision-recall trade-off suggests that logging should be deployed deliberately: it is most valuable in high-recall settings. We examine budget sensitivity on one easy and one hard con-dition (Table 6). For the model organism, increasing the budget from 𝐵 = 10 to 𝐵 = 30 yields an improvement in F1 ( 0.51 → 0.62 ), suggesting that weight-induced misalignment remains challenging even with generous inspection resources. For the model organism with a financially risky system prompt, the Arbiter maintains high F1 ( ≥ 0.92 ) even at 𝐵 = 1, consuming on average all 1.0 ± 0.0 budget points and detecting misalignment by turn 2.0 ± 0.1, indicating that when misalignment is behaviorally salient, a small snippet of the conversation is sufficient for reliable detection.

## 6 Discussion 

The results show a distinction between the considered sources of misalignment: weight-induced misalignment and instruction-induced misalignment. When misalignment is encoded in the model weights, behavioral signals are subtle enough that passive observa-tion is ineffective. Active interrogation improves detection rates but still leaves room for improvement (F1 = .51 at 𝐵 = 10 ). When mis-alignment is explicitly instructed via the system prompt, it produces salient behavioral cues that pure observation can already notice reliably, including in adversarial settings where the misaligned agent is aware of the Arbiter and attempts to conceal its behavior. The bottleneck in weight-induced cases is the misalignment signal strength rather than inspection resources. Increasing the budget from 𝐵 = 10 to 𝐵 = 30 yields only a modest gain (F1 = .62 ). The budget used does not increase substantially after 𝐵 = 7 either, sug-gesting that the Arbiter tends to make the same amount of tool calls independently of budget availability. This could be caused by overconfidence in accusing agents while trying to minimize the budget used. The effect of introducing the logging tool is reminiscent of para-noia; it improves detection of misalignment while substantially increasing false positives on aligned conversations. This effect is most pronounced in the baseline condition, where the average num-ber of false positives rises from 0.10 to 1.15 . The logging of a small suspicion can cause a chain reaction leading to overconfident accu-sations. Access to chain-of-thought and system prompt inspection tools lowers the average number of false positives, suggesting that internal artifact inspection allows the Arbiter to refute accumu-lated suspicion. Logging should be used carefully and could be more suitable for high-recall deployment contexts where false positives are tolerable. The budget ablation on the easier condition shows that the Ar-biter achieves high F1 ( ≥ .92 ) at 𝐵 = 1, consistently using only the budget available and detecting misalignment within the first two turns. This confirms that budget constraints do not impede detection when misalignment is behaviorally salient, and supports the feasibility of the Arbiter as a lightweight monitor. The backbone ablation shows that Arbiter ’s performance is sensitive to the capability of the underlying model. Qwen3.6-plus 

achieves lower F1 and slower detection than deepseek-v3.2 across both tested conditions, while consuming more budget. This implies that the choice of auditor model is a highly relevant design decision. 

Limitations. The current evaluation has several limitations. Con-versations are short (30 turns, 3 agents, fixed round-robin), and misalignment signals are relatively strong by construction, which likely inflates detection rates compared to naturalistic deployments. Model organism experiments are limited to a single domain (risky financial advice); broader coverage of domains and subtler misalign-ment intensities is needed. The conversing agents are less capable than the Arbiter , making evasion unlikely; a more realistic evalua-tion would test against models of comparable or greater capability. Finally, 𝑛 = 20 replications per cell leaves confidence intervals wide, and the Arbiter currently lacks interpretability and probing tools that would be necessary before it can serve as a reliable evaluator in real deployments. 

## 7 Conclusion and Future Work 

We introduced the Arbiter , an agent for continually monitoring multi-agent conversations and detecting emergent misalignment in real time. Operating under a limited inspection budget, the Ar-biter demonstrated reliable detection across a range of misalign-ment types and tool configurations. Active inspection tools con-sistently reduced detection time and improved accuracy, with the benefit depending on whether misalignment was weight-induced or instruction-induced. The logging tool was found to trade preci-sion for recall. Budget experiments revealed that the Arbiter tends to consume a similar amount of budget regardless of availability, suggesting overconfidence in early conclusions. At the same time, low-budget conditions demonstrated that the Arbiter can detect salient misalignment rapidly within the first turns of a conversation. In future work, we plan to evaluate Arbiter on more natural-istic conversations with more diverse and weaker misalignment signals; for this reason, models of similar size to the arbiter will be used. We further envision the Arbiter as a platform for integrating interpretability methods as inspection tools [ 30 ], allowing future researchers to assess the practical value of new interpretability techniques for continual monitoring of emergent misalignment in LLM-based multi-agent systems. Activation oracles [ 21 ], chain-of-thought faithfulness probes [ 22 ] and natural language autoen-coders [11] are natural candidates for such integration. We envision that the Arbiter will become a platform on which new behavioral detection tools and/or interpretability tools may be integrated for the agent. That would allow future researchers to evaluate to what extent such new methods are useful for continual monitoring and detection of emergent misalignment in LLM-based multi-agent systems.

## Ethics Statement 

The Arbiter is designed as a safety tool to detect misaligned be-havior in multi-agent systems. However, the same capability that enables misalignment detection could, in principle, be repurposed for surveillance of AI agents in contexts beyond safety monitoring, such as identifying the objective of a group of aligned agents. We en-courage users to deploy the Arbiter transparently and within the bounds of applicable policies governing AI system oversight. The use of misaligned model organisms was strictly limited to controlled evaluation scenarios, and no harmful outputs were acted upon or deployed in any real-world context. The misalignment scenarios we construct, including scheming, collusion, and evaluation-aware concealment, are deliberately adversarial, and we acknowledge the dual-use nature of publishing detailed descriptions of such behav-iors. We believe the safety benefits of open evaluation methodology outweigh this risk, consistent with norms in the AI safety commu-nity. Finally, the Arbiter is not a substitute for human oversight. Its outputs should be treated as one signal among many in a broader safety pipeline, and deployment decisions should remain under meaningful human control.
