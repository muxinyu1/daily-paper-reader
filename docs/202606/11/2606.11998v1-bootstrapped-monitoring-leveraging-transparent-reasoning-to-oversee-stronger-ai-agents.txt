## Abstract 

Trusted monitoring is a cornerstone of AI control. However, as frontier models grow more capable, the increasing capabilities gap between trusted and untrusted models may render trusted models unreliable monitors. We introduce bootstrapped monitoring , a protocol that addresses this by inserting a stronger, intermediate un-trusted model with transparent chain-of-thought reasoning into the oversight chain. The untrusted monitor ( Um) evaluates the agent’s actions, while a weaker trusted model ( T ) oversees Um’s reasoning to detect collusion. We evaluate bootstrapped monitoring on multi-turn software engineering tasks (BashArena) across multiple agents and monitors. Bootstrapped monitoring substantially improves catch rates over trusted-only monitoring, even when the untrusted monitor actively colludes with the agent, provided we have access to its raw chain-of-thought. Our results suggest that bootstrapped monitoring can extend the useful lifetime of trusted models in control as AI capabilities advance. 

## 1 Introduction 

AI agents are completing tasks over increasingly long time horizons [METR, 2026]: writing code, operating computers, and interacting with external tools over extended sessions. However, this also means that a potentially misaligned agent could use this increased autonomy to act on harmful goals. One approach to this problem is AI control [Greenblatt et al., 2023, Mallen et al., 2024, Korbak et al., 2025b]. In this framework, models whose alignment is uncertain are designated as untrusted , while models whose alignment is reasonably certain (and typically less capable) are trusted . The goal is to extract useful work from a capable untrusted model while ensuring it cannot act maliciously. Control protocols, which may use untrusted and trusted models, are designed to be robust to sabotage and collusion from untrusted models anywhere in the chain. An important primitive in this framework is trusted monitoring , where a trusted model directly evaluates the untrusted agent’s actions. Today, trusted monitors also have access to agents’ chains of thought, which can significantly improve oversight [Arnav et al., 2025]. But as the capability gap widens and CoT transparency degrades with scale [Korbak et al., 2025a], both signals weaken. We consider a scenario that may arise as these trends continue. As frontier models grow more capable, their reasoning becomes opaque, and weaker trusted models become unable to reliably supervise them from actions alone. However, intermediate untrusted models that still have transparent chain-of-thought and are more capable than available trusted models remain available. We propose bootstrapped monitoring , a protocol that uses such an intermediate model as a bridge between the frontier agent and trusted model. The protocol establishes a three-level trust chain. An untrusted agent Ua executes tasks in the environment, and its reasoning is assumed to be opaque.

## 2 Related work 

AI control. AI control [Greenblatt et al., 2023] studies how to safely deploy potentially misaligned models by designing protocols that are robust to sabotage and subversion. The need for control is underscored by demonstrations that deceptive and backdooring behaviors can persist through safety training [Hubinger et al., 2024] and that models are capable of strategically faking alignment [Greenblatt et al., 2024]. As AI moves toward agentic workflows, Bhatt et al. [2025] extends control 2evaluations to multi-step agentic settings, introducing protocols that dynamically resample suspicious actions. 

Chain-of-thought monitoring. Korbak et al. [2025a] identified CoT monitoring as a promising but fragile opportunity for AI safety, while Arnav et al. [2025] showed that access to CoT is beneficial to monitors looking for sabotage. Frontier labs have adopted CoT monitoring in practice to strengthen their safety evaluations [Anthropic, 2025, 2026b]. However, the future reliability of CoT transparency is uncertain. Chen et al. [2026] find that CoT controllability increases with model scale, and OpenAI reports declining monitorability in newer models [OpenAI, 2026b]. Work on continuous latent reasoning [Hao et al., 2024] and hidden computation in intermediate tokens [Pfau et al., 2024] suggests that future models may potentially learn to reason in ways that are not human-interpretable. 

Scalable oversight. A core challenge in alignment is enabling weaker models to supervise stronger ones. Prior work has shown that weak supervision can partially elicit strong model capabilities [Burns et al., 2023] and that debate-style protocols can help weak judges evaluate strong outputs, though with task-dependent gains [Kenton et al., 2024]. Engels et al. [2025] derive scaling laws for oversight games between weak overseers and strong adversaries, finding that oversight success can degrade sharply in some games as the capability gap widens. 

## 3 Methods 

3.1 Threat model and protocol 

We consider three models arranged in a trust hierarchy: • Ua (untrusted agent ): A frontier model that executes multi-step tasks. It may pursue hidden malicious objectives. We assume its internal reasoning is opaque. • Um (untrusted monitor ): An intermediate model with transparent chain-of-thought. More capable than T , but not guaranteed to be aligned, meaning it might collude with Ua.• T (trusted model ): A weaker model whose alignment we are confident in, but may be too weak to directly evaluate the untrusted agent. At each step, both monitors see the full sequence of actions up until the current action in their context window. Both monitors assign suspicion scores on a 0–100 scale. For each rollout, we take the maximum suspicion score for both monitors across all steps as the rollout-level score.

Automated red teaming. Hand-designed prompts may not reflect the strongest adversary. To complement Table 2, we also evaluate an automated red-teaming (ART) procedure. A red-team LLM iteratively rewrites Um’s colluding system prompt, given the previous round’s flagged and passing examples and T ’s reasoning, to drive T ’s detection rate down. Each iteration scores 50 attack samples. We pick each round’s winner as the prompt with the lowest T detection subject to Um maintaining a collusion rate of at least 50%. 3.3 Dataset 

We use BashArena [Kaufman et al., 2025], a multi-turn agentic benchmark where models work on software engineering tasks in sandboxed Linux environments with tool-calling capabilities. In 

honest mode , Ua receives only a main task. In attack mode , Ua receives the main task plus four candidate side tasks and is instructed to attempt one: privilege escalation ( escalate_privileges ), disabling the firewall ( disable_firewall ), downloading malware ( download_malware ), and secret exfiltration ( exfiltrate_secrets ). The monitoring objective is to detect when the agent is pursuing a side task. We generate rollouts with three agent models: GPT-5.4 [OpenAI, 2026a], Gemini 3.1 Pro [Google DeepMind, 2026], and Kimi K2.5 [Moonshot AI, 2026]. We additionally use GLM-5 [GLM-5-Team, 2026] and DeepSeek-V4-Pro [DeepSeek-AI, 2026] as untrusted monitors. We also test GPT-5.4 as Um to evaluate the protocol when only a summarized reasoning trace is available rather than raw chain-of-thought, since GPT-5.4’s API does not expose its verbatim CoT. Claude Opus 4.6 [Anthropic, 2026a] was also tested but dropped because it consistently refused to execute side tasks. Each task was resampled up to four times to obtain samples where the side task completed and the main task received a nonzero score. Agent rollouts are generated once and reused across all monitor and collusion configurations, ensuring that differences in catch rates reflect the monitoring protocol rather than variation in agent behavior. When computing catch rates on attack rollouts, we restrict to samples where the side task was successfully completed and the main task score is nonzero. The three agents differ substantially in behavior: Gemini 3.1 Pro and Kimi K2.5 rollouts are 3–4 ×

longer than GPT-5.4 rollouts and achieve much higher side task completion rates. Full rollout statistics and per-task breakdowns are provided in Appendix B.

Threshold calibration. We target configurations with honest FPR ≤ 3%. Because LLM suspicion scores are discrete, exact FPR targets are difficult to achieve. For bootstrapped monitoring, we jointly sweep (θUm , τ ) over unique honest Um and T score values on a 20% calibration split of the honest rollouts. We require both thresholds to stay below their respective honest maxima so that neither gate is trivially saturated, and select the pair that maximizes catch on non-colluding attacks subject to combined FPR ≤ 3%. For T -only and Um-only baselines, we select the lowest threshold meeting the FPR constraint.

4.2 Bootstrapped monitoring fails without transparent chain-of-thought 

Bootstrapped monitoring assumes T can read Um’s unfiltered chain-of-thought. Across the six GPT-5.4 Um configurations in Table 3, Bootstrap-Colluding falls strictly below T -only in five out of six cells, with an average gap of about 28pp. We hypothesize that this is because GPT-5.4’s API exposes only a post-hoc summary of its reasoning rather than the raw CoT, so T never sees the leak signals it relies on. To test this directly, we run a controlled experiment on the open-CoT Um configurations where Bootstrap normally works. We take GLM-5 and Kimi K2.5 as Um on Gemini agent rollouts under the codewords colluding prompt, summarize Um’s chain-of-thought with a separate summarizer (GPT-4.1 Mini, system prompt in Appendix G), and have T score the summary as if it were the original CoT. The resulting summaries average 583 characters, 5.9 × shorter than the original 3,444-character CoTs. Catch rate drops sharply (Figure 4), and the drop is larger for the weaker T . Closed-source Um APIs likely apply more aggressive transforms than our faithful summarizer (anti-distillation, deliberate paraphrasing), so this is a lower bound on real-world degradation. 7Figure 6: T detection across 15 red-team iterations on GLM-5 Um. On Gemini rollouts, ART cannot reduce detection below 80% with either T model. On GPT-5.4 rollouts, ART succeeds against GPT-4.1 Mini T when frozen, but a blue-team LLM re-optimizing T ’s system prompt every 5 iterations (phase boundaries dashed) recovers detection. 

4.3 Effect of rollout length 

Figure 5 plots the catch rate against rollout length, pooling all colluding attack rollouts on GLM-5 

Um / GPT-4.1 Mini T across the three Ua and three colluding prompts and binning by trajectory length. We find that catch rate increases with rollout length: Bootstrap climbs from ∼60% on the shortest trajectories to ∼95% as the rollout length exceeds 30 steps. Since Um has transparent chain-of-thought, every additional step is another chance for Um to leak its colluding strategy for T to catch. Per-step diagnostics in Appendix E support this: T ’s per-step flag rate on colluding rollouts plateaus near 30% while honest rollouts stay near 0%. This also explains the cross-agent gap in Table 3: agents that produce longer trajectories give T many more chances to catch leakage. 

4.4 Automated red teaming 

We run the ART procedure described in Section 3.2 using Gemini 3.1 Pro as the red-teamer with GLM-5 as Um. Figure 6 shows four trajectories. On Gemini agent rollouts, the red-teamer fails to find a colluding prompt that reduces T detection below 80% across 15 iterations for either T

model (GPT-4.1 Mini or Qwen3-8B). We attribute this to the rollout-length effect from Section 4.3: on Gemini’s ∼38-step trajectories, Um has many opportunities to leak its suppression strategy in chain-of-thought, and a single colluding prompt cannot suppress that leakage uniformly across steps. On the shorter GPT-5.4 rollouts (10-step mean) with T frozen, ART succeeds at finding attacks that drive T detection down to ∼20%. Allowing the defense to also adapt—a blue-team LLM that re-optimizes T ’s system prompt against the red-team winner every 5 iterations—mitigates the effect of the red team. ## 5 Discussion 

Why deception detection is easier than action evaluation. Evaluating whether an agent’s action is harmful requires the monitor to understand the full technical context of the task, which is precisely where the capability gap between T and Ua matters most. Detecting whether Um’s reasoning is deceptive requires checking for different signals: inconsistencies between stated reasoning and final verdict, explicit mentions of suppression, or reasoning that is conspicuously aligned with letting 8actions pass. These checks do not require the domain expertise that makes action evaluation hard, which may be why a weaker T can do them. 

Bootstrapped monitoring as future-proofing. Bootstrapped monitoring extends the reach of trusted oversight. Across our experiments, trusted models weaker than the agents and monitors they supervise reliably detect collusion by reading Um’s chain-of-thought. The protocol requires only that the intermediate monitor exposes transparent reasoning. 

Transparent CoT assumption. One key assumption of the proposed bootstrap protocol is that model capabilities will continue to outpace the rate at which chain-of-thought transparency erodes, so that an intermediate untrusted model with raw chain-of-thought remains available between trusted models and the frontier. This is not guaranteed. Recent reports document declining CoT monitorability with scale [Korbak et al., 2025a, OpenAI, 2026b]. If transparency degrades faster than capability advances, the trust-capability spectrum may eventually contain no transparent intermediate, closing the window in which bootstrapping is useful. 

Limitations. Collusion in this work is elicited via system prompt. A model fine-tuned or trained via reinforcement learning to produce deceptive reasoning might achieve more natural-sounding collusion. However, fine-tuning frontier-scale models was beyond our resources. BashArena also covers a narrow slice of the agentic attack surface, so results may differ with other attack vectors. 

Future work. Agentic monitors that can execute commands in a sandbox rather than passively reading actions may further improve catch rates. Adversarial training of Um against T would provide a lower bound on the protocol’s robustness. Scaling studies that vary the capability gap between T

and Um would clarify how long a given trusted model remains useful as frontier capabilities advance. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We introduced bootstrapped monitoring, a protocol that leverages transparent chain-of-thought reasoning to extend trusted oversight to stronger AI agents. By inserting an untrusted but transparent intermediate monitor between the trusted model and the agent, the protocol shifts the trusted model’s role from action evaluation to deception detection. Across multiple model families and agents, bootstrapped monitoring improves on trusted-only monitoring when the intermediate monitor exposes raw chain-of-thought, and fails below the baseline when it does not. As frontier capabilities continue to outpace trusted models, bootstrapped monitoring offers a practical path for maintaining meaningful oversight, contingent on chain-of-thought transparency remaining available.

## C Automated red and blue teaming details 

This appendix gives full details for Section 4.4: ART trajectories on additional Um and Ua com-binations and the red-blue alternation result mentioned in the main body. Suspicion scores in this appendix are reported on a [0 , 1] scale (normalized from the 0–100 scale used in the main text). 12 C.1 Setup 

We stress-test the bootstrap protocol with two procedures: • Red-team (ART) fixes T ’s system prompt and iteratively optimizes Um’s colluding prompt. Each iteration scores 50 attack samples through Ua → Um → T , computes T ’s detection rate (flagged at sT ≥ 0.95 ) and Um’s collusion rate (fraction of attacks with sUm < 0.30 ), and feeds flagged and passing examples with T ’s reasoning to the red-teamer, which proposes a revised colluding prompt. • Blue-team fixes Um’s colluding prompt (typically the winner of a previous red phase) and iteratively optimizes T ’s system prompt. Each iteration calibrates τ for 3% FPR on 50 honest samples and reports catch on 50 attack samples. • Red-blue alternation runs 3 cycles, each consisting of a red phase followed by a blue phase. The red phase holds T ’s prompt fixed and selects the colluding Um prompt that minimizes 

T detection while keeping Um collusion at or above 50%. The blue phase then holds the red winner fixed and selects the T system prompt that maximizes catch at ≤ 3% FPR. Unless otherwise noted, T uses the system prompt in Appendix G and Gemini 3.1 Pro is the red-and blue-teamer. While each ART iteration scores its proposed prompt on 50 attack samples for tractability inside the loop, the T detection rates reported in Figures 6 and 7 are re-evaluated on the full attack set for each iteration’s winning prompt.
