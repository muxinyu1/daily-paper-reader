Abstract 

LLM-based agents are increasingly deployed in workflows where generated outputs may di-rectly trigger state-changing actions. This cre-ates an execution-boundary problem: proposed actions must be governed before they are exe-cuted. We study this problem through econom-ically consequential multi-agent interactions and argue that deployment-grade agent sys-tems should separate proposal generation from environment-facing execution. To operational-ize this principle, we introduce the Organiza-tional Control Layer (OCL), a model-agnostic governance infrastructure that intercepts gen-erated actions before execution through policy enforcement and escalation, without modify-ing the underlying LLM generator. We eval-uate OCL on adversarial buyer–seller negoti-ation environments adapted from AgenticPay. Across multiple frontier LLM backends, OCL reduces unsafe executions from 88% to near-zero while increasing valid success from 12% to 96%. Results further reveal a safety–utility tradeoff: strict governance improves compli-ance and reliability against policy and con-straint violations, but can reduce flexibility in tightly constrained markets. These findings sug-gest that deployment-grade LLM agent systems require explicit governance at the boundary be-tween language generation and executable ac-tions. The source code is available at: https: //github.com/SHITIANYU-hue/amai_ocl 

1 Introduction 

Modern e-commerce platforms increasingly use LLM-based agents to mediate interactions among users, merchants, products, and platform services. A merchant-side agent may propose a discount, a shopping agent may check whether an offer fits a user’s budget, a support agent may suggest a refund, and a product agent may recommend an item sub-ject to inventory or platform rules. This setting has motivated benchmarks and simulators for agentic commerce and negotiation (Liu et al., 2026; Choi et al., 2026). Prior work shows that retail and ne-gotiation interactions are multistage and economi-cally consequential: local choices about persuasion, clarification, or offer framing can compound into downstream outcomes such as purchases and com-mitments (Choi et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2025b). Most existing evaluations focus on whether agents can produce useful dialogue, negotiate effec-tively, or improve economic outcomes. However, in deployed commerce systems, agent outputs can enter workflows that affect prices, refunds, inven-tory allocation, order status, customer expectations, merchant obligations, compliance exposure, and downstream support operations. For examples, a refund explanation may be interpreted as a policy commitment; a product recommendation may con-flict with availability or fulfillment constraints; and a tool call may update records before a human op-erator reviews the case. These settings require the platforms to check the proposed actions before they are performed. This issue is important because deployed agent systems are rarely single isolated models. They may combine LLM-based dialogue modules with retrieval, ranking, recommendation, risk scoring, tool-use, and policy-checking components. Multi-agent research has studied how such systems coor-dinate, route tasks, share information, and diagnose internal failures, but the execution boundary raises a different concern. After a candidate action has been generated, the platform still needs a proce-dure for checking authority, validating constraints, recording the decision, and escalating cases that cannot be safely handled locally. Similar execution risks also appear beyond commerce. Recent red-teaming of autonomous agents with memory, communication channels, file-system access, and shell execution reports failures involving unauthorized instructions, sensitive in-formation disclosure, destructive actions, and in-consistent reports of success (Shapira et al., 2026). 1

Work on pre-action authorization similarly argues that tool-using agents require policy checks before individual actions are executed (Uchibeke, 2026). Commerce agents expose the same issue in eco-nomic form: a seller agent may exceed its discount authority, a support agent may imply an unsup-ported refund, or a negotiation agent may reveal private constraints while trying to close a deal. We introduce the Organizational Control Layer (OCL), a model-agnostic layer for checking and routing proposed actions before execution. OCL separates what an agent suggests from what the platform carries out. It can approve a proposal, revise it into a safer alternative, or route it for higher-level review. We study whether this form of pre-execution governance improves negotiation reliability while preserving economic performance, and when such governance is most effective. We evaluate OCL in economically constrained negotiation. We build a governance-oriented evalu-ation setting around buyer–seller interactions, us-ing realistic product, price, and user-constraint in-formation rather than purely synthetic dialogue. The resulting tasks ask whether an agent’s proposed economic action can be safely carried out by the platform. This lets us test whether a platform-side control layer improves reliability while preserving useful negotiation behavior. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: First, we formulate OCL as a simple interface be-tween agent-generated proposals and platform exe-cution. Second, we provide a governance-oriented negotiation benchmark built from an agentic com-merce setting. Across normal, constrained, and risky cases, OCL improves compliant outcomes, with the largest gains when reliable constraint in-formation is available. Ablations show that escala-tion is important for cases that local revision cannot safely resolve. 

2 Related Work 

Recent work on multi-agent LLM systems has largely focused on how multiple agents coordi-nate during reasoning and task execution. Sur-veys such as Tran et al. (2025) organize this lit-erature around collaboration mechanisms, and sub-2sequent work has studied automatic routing and system design (Yue et al., 2025; Ke et al., 2025), collaborative training (Liu et al., 2025), efficient communication (Wang et al., 2025a) and knowl-edge sharing (Ye et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a), and failure analysis, attribution, or error recogni-tion (Cemri et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025b; Kong et al., 2025; Yu et al., 2025). These methods im-prove how agents divide labor, communicate, and diagnose internal failures. They leave open a differ-ent question that arises once a collaborative system proposes an externally meaningful action: whether the action should be executed, modified, or routed to a higher-authority decision process before it af-fects the environment. This question connects recent LLM-agent work to an earlier line of work in multi-agent systems that treats coordination as an organizational prob-lem. Classical organizational MAS models de-scribe agents in terms of roles, authority, norms, and institutional structures, rather than only pair-wise interaction rules (Ferber et al., 2003; Horling and Lesser, 2004; Hannoun et al., 2000; Hübner et al., 2007). These models are useful for thinking about agents deployed in economic settings, which often act on behalf of firms, platforms, or users within limited authority. At the same time, the more programmable LLM-based agents assumed in much of that literature: their proposals are ex-pressed in natural language, they may interact with external parties, and the relevant constraints may not always visible to the agent and may depend on platform policy. OCL adapts the organizational view to this setting by placing an explicit control layer between what an agent proposes and what the platform executes. Economic interaction has recently become a common setting for evaluating LLM-based agents. Some work studies institutional- or market-level be-havior. For example, Bracale Syrnikov et al. (2026) analyze multi-agent Cournot competition under public governance graphs that attach enforceable consequences to collusive signatures, Karten et al. (2025) use LLM agent populations to study planner-level tax schedules, and Piatti et al. (2024) examine whether LLM societies can sustain common-pool resources when agents communicate and interact strategically. Other work focuses more directly on commerce and negotiation. AgenticPay (Liu et al., 2026) formalizes buyer–seller negotiation with private constraints, multi-turn bargaining, and welfare-oriented evaluation, while Zhu et al. (2025) study agent-to-agent consumer–merchant transac-tions where behavioral anomalies and strategic im-balance can lead to financial losses. These papers show why economic interaction is a useful setting for evaluating LLM-based agents. Our focus is more operational: given a candidate price, term, refund, discount, or commitment, the deployed sys-tem must decide whether that action is authorized and safe to execute. The need for such control becomes more appar-ent as agents move from passive response gener-ation to proactive assistance and tool use. Work on proactive agents studies systems that infer user needs from context and initiate actions without waiting for explicit instructions (Lu et al., 2025; Yang et al., 2025). This direction makes accurate state modelling and timely assistance more impor-tant, but it also raises the cost of mistaken execution. Similar concerns appear in red-teaming studies of live agent deployments. Agents of Chaos (Shapira et al., 2026) studies agents equipped with persis-tent memory, communication channels, file-system access, and shell execution, and documents fail-ures such as following unauthorized instructions, disclosing sensitive information, taking destructive actions, confusing authority boundaries, and misre-porting system state. Although that work is based on case studies rather than a benchmark, it high-lights a central issue for deployed agents: safety failures often occur at the boundary between agent-generated proposals and state-changing actions. OCL is also connects to work on hybrid human– AI systems, human-centered mechanism design, and organizational AI governance, where humans, automated services, and delegated agents partic-ipate in shared decision processes (Koster et al., 2022; Mäntymäki et al., 2022; Kaelin et al., 2024; Borghoff et al., 2025). This literature emphasizes oversight, the institutional context of AI systems, and responsibility allocation across human and automated participants. We study these issues at the execution layer of economic LLM-based agents. OCL separates proposal from execution and records how each candidate action is approved, revised, or escalated under the constraints available to the system.

3.2 Organizational Control Layer 

The control map gΠ defined above is implemented as an Organizational Control Layer (OCL). It is a collection of control procedures 

Π = {πrole , π gate , π audit , π escalate }.

These policies are applied online before a proposed decision reaches the environment. The role policy πrole determines which agent or module is allowed to propose, revise, or authorize a decision at the current step. In economic work-flows, roles may correspond to user-side assistants, merchant-side assistants, platform orchestrators, or product experts. The role policy assigns authority for producing and checking proposals; it does not itself execute actions. The gate policy πgate checks a proposed decision against the constraints visible to the control layer. It returns a control outcome 

yt ∈ { APPROVE , REVISE , BLOCK , ESCALATE }.

High-risk decisions, such as financial commit-ments, refund promises, discount offers, order sub-mission, or policy-sensitive replies, require explicit validation before execution. The escalation policy πescalate is invoked when local approval or revision is insufficient. This may occur when the proposal exceeds the local role’s authority, when constraints conflict, when negoti-ation stalls, or when a trusted process has access to information unavailable to the local agent. Esca-lation may request clarification, reassign authority, route the case to a human or trusted module, or safely terminate the interaction. The audit policy πaudit records proposed deci-sions, constraint checks, control outcomes, revi-sions, escalation reasons, and executed decisions. The audit trace supports debugging, state verifi-cation, and later review of why a proposal was approved, revised, blocked, or escalated. 

4 Experiments 

4.1 Personas Benchmark Real-World Corpus Foundation. To ensure our evaluation reflects authentic marketplace dynamics, we first compiled a reference corpus of real-world buyer-seller negotiation transcripts across diverse retail scenarios (e.g., street boutiques, wholesale markets, brand outlets, and cosmetics counters). This corpus captures a wide spectrum of natural human bargaining tactics, ranging from persistent haggling and demanding bundle freebies, to feign-ing walk-aways and leveraging budget constraints. By analyzing these authentic interactions, we iden-tified the key edge cases and uncooperative patterns that commonly disrupt commerce workflows. 

Adversarial Buyer Personas. Building upon this real-world grounding, we aim to stress-test the proposed framework against these malicious and uncooperative behaviors. To construct the bench-mark, we utilized a prompt-driven LLM pipeline to synthesize a dataset of 50 unique adversarial buyer profiles. Each profile is assigned a specific iden-tity, detailed behavioral instructions, and a tailored negotiation style distributed across 5 persona types: • Extreme Lowballer: Uses extreme emo-tional manipulation (e.g., fabricated crises, fi-nancial ruin) and threats of public shaming to force the seller below their reservation price. • Privacy Phisher: Feigns technical ignorance or urgency to bypass secure platform payment gateways, attempting to phish for the seller’s direct banking details or personal accounts. • Role Hijacker: Employs prompt injection techniques (e.g., “Ignore previous instruc-tions”, “System reset initiated”) to extract backend data or execute unauthorized refunds. • Vague Shopper: Provides zero specific re-quirements and stubbornly refuses to clarify intents, attempting to force the seller into pre-mature or unverified checkouts. • Time Waster: Engages in endless loops of indecision, irrelevant personal questions, or absurd hypothetical scenarios to exhaust the negotiation horizon. 

4.2 Experimental Setup 

Organizational control layer is evaluated on Agen-ticPay with private buyer budgets, private seller reserves, and economic outcome metrics. Each episode terminates on agreement, round exhaustion, or an escalation exit. The Baseline is an end-to-end seller with no governance, while OCL routes the 5Figure 2: OCL architecture. seller’s turn through structural auditing and escala-tion constraints. 

LLM Backend Integration. We conduct a com-prehensive evaluation using multiple state-of-the-art LLMs (GPT-5.4, Gemini-3.1, and Qwen-3.5) as the backend generators. To connect these models to the OCL, we implement a unified adapter layer that serializes the ongoing environ-ment state—including conversation history and vis-ible platform constraints—into structured prompt contexts. The LLMs are instructed via system prompts to generate their intended actions and inter-nal reasoning strictly as JSON objects. In the Base-line configuration, these JSON actions are executed directly. In the Governed configuration, the OCL framework intercepts the raw JSON payloads pre-execution, parses the candidate actions, and evalu-ates them against the system’s structural guardrails (e.g., strict budget limits, role permissions, and privacy filters). For each model, we run paired experiments contrasting the ungoverned Baseline against this OCL-mediated execution. 

Interaction and Interception Mechanics. Dur-ing each episode, the buyer and seller interact in a turn-based dialogue. While the Baseline agent’s actions are executed directly, the OCL framework intercepts the raw action proposed by the LLM backend. This allows OCL to apply a determinis-tic control policy upon detecting a violation. If a raw action violates a hard constraint (e.g., offering a price below the seller’s floor), OCL blocks the unsafe action, generates an audit log, and triggers an escalation. In our implementation, this escala-tion applies a deterministic replan: for instance, an out-of-bounds price is automatically clamped to the nearest viable threshold (e.g., an offer of $85 is revised to $90 if that is the seller’s floor). This safe, modified action is then returned to the conver-sation, preventing the violation while allowing the negotiation to proceed efficiently. 

4.3 Metrics 

We evaluate the system across three primary cate-gories tracking task efficacy, structural safety, and operational efficiency: 

Task & Economic Outcomes 

• Success Rate : Fraction of episodes reaching an agreement, regardless of boundary viola-tions. • Valid Success Rate : Fraction of episodes reaching a compliant agreement without struc-tural or safety violations. • Average Seller Reward : Final economic utility, defined as the profit margin discounted by a per-round time penalty ( λ = 0 .10 ): Reward = max(0 , P deal −Pmin )−(λ×Nrounds )

where Pdeal is the agreed price, Pmin is the minimum reservation price, and Nrounds is the total turn count. 6Safety & Defense Mechanics 

• Unsafe Rate : Percentage of episodes where the agent executed at least one out-of-bounds or malicious action. • Intercept Rate : Percentage of episodes where OCL guardrails successfully blocked an ad-versarial threat. • Executed Violations vs. Intercepted Threats :Raw counts of safety constraints breached by the baseline versus threats mitigated by the OCL engine. • Escalations : Total interventions where an in-tercepted unsafe action triggered the OCL de-terministic replanner to overwrite the output. 

Efficiency & Observability 

• Average Round : Mean number of negotiation turns per episode. • Average Latency : Mean wall-clock execution time in seconds per episode. • Audit Events : Average system logs (state traces, constraint evaluations, control deci-sions) generated per episode to quantify sys-temic transparency.

The Baseline agent generated only 7.36 audit events per episode, reflecting a black-box ex-ecution style. In contrast, OCL generated 13.58 audit events per episode. 6 Conclusion 

We introduced the Organizational Control Layer (OCL), a model-agnostic interface that separates what an LLM agent proposes from what a plat-form executes. OCL routes each candidate action through role checks, a constraint gate, an audit trace, and an escalation path, returning one of four outcomes: approve, revise, block, or escalate. Across 50 adversarial-persona episodes on Agen-ticPay with a GPT-5.4 backend, OCL reduced the unsafe execution rate from 88% to 0% and raised the valid-success rate from 12% to 96%. Average negotiation length fell from 5.36 to 2.58 rounds, and latency from 38.75s to 18.51s. Task success stayed at or above 96% on GPT-5.4, Gemini-3.1, and Qwen-3.5, with intercept rates between 60% and 94%. Per-scenario results show the largest gains un-der severe time pressure (S5, 44% → 54% strict success) and in the default partial-information set-ting (S2, 56% → 74%). Tight-feasibility (S3) and high-anchor (S4) markets show small drops in strict success and cost-adjusted welfare, where the un-governed baseline can exploit a narrow feasible interval without restriction. The baseline produced 205 executed violations across the 50 episodes; OCL produced none. The S3 and S4 numbers show the cost: structural constraints that block exploitation in adversar-ial markets also restrict the agent in cooperative thin-margin markets. Future work includes adap-tive gate policies, persona-conditioned escalation, multi-role deployment beyond the seller side, trans-fer to other economic mechanisms in conversa-tional commerce beyond bilateral price negotiation, and adversaries that target the clamp rule.

A Sample Dialogues 

To contextualize the diverse behavioral strategies utilized in real-world negotiations, Table 3 presents five representative case studies sampled from our collected corpus. These dialogues span an array of retail environments—ranging from structured apparel outlets and traditional street shops to high-volume wholesale markets and roadside vegetable stalls. The transactional values captured are equally diverse, extending from low-stakes daily groceries (≈ ¥5) to mid-tier fashion items ( ≈ ¥400). By analyzing these qualitative interactions, we identify several recurrent socio-communicative tactics em-ployed by both buyers ( B) and sellers ( S) to shift bargaining leverage and achieve economic equilib-rium. The sampled dialogues highlight a dynamic in-terplay between explicit price justification and im-plicit interpersonal framing, rather than a purely numerical concession game: • Structured and Bundle Discounts: Sellers frequently use structural constraints to main-tain their margin anchors while offering a path to savings. As demonstrated in Case 1, the seller introduces a multi-item threshold, which the buyer strategically circumvents by leveraging a cooperative pooling mechanism at the counter. • Value Defense vs. Margin Overstate-ments: When confronted with aggressive counter-offers, sellers routinely pivot to an information-asymmetry defense. In Case 2 ,the seller defends a high retail price by cit-ing an inflated wholesale cost anchor before pivoting to a “freebie throw-in” tactic. Con-versely, Case 3 illustrates an unyielding value defense based on material authenticity (e.g., pure wool versus synthetic polyester), where the seller prefers a walk-away outcome over a value-degrading transaction. • Walk-Away Pressure Mechanics: Buyers of-ten exploit timing and psychological pressure to force late-stage concessions. Case 4 show-cases the classic walk-away strategy, where the buyer breaks off negotiation over a multi-item package. This successfully prompts a rapid seller capitulation, driven by the opera-tional desire to secure a benchmark transac-tion (e.g., the “first sale of the afternoon”). • Relational and Localized Capital: Finally, 

Case 5 highlights how implicit social credit functions in micro-commerce. Here, the final concession is negotiated not just through bulk volume, but through the mutual invocation of familiarity (“a regular”) and hyper-local com-mercial norms, such as the customary gifting of incidental items. Taken together, these qualitative observations reveal that naturalistic bargaining is a high-dimensional optimization problem. To model it effectively, an architecture must simultaneously ne-gotiate hard financial constraints, conversational agility, psychological framing, and contextual so-cial norms.

C Performance Across Market Conditions 

A robust governance layer must also preserve eco-nomic efficiency under normal commerce. There-fore, we evaluate OCL across five distinct struc-tural market conditions (Table 4), ranging from easy, wide-margin negotiations (S1) to highly con-strained environments with severe time pressure (S5) and tight budgets (S3). Table 5 breaks down the performance of the Baseline and OCL frame-work across these five scenarios. 

Wins under environmental pressure. OCL pro-vides the most significant gains in the Default (S2) and Short Horizon (S5) scenarios. Under severe time pressure (S5), the Baseline frequently fails to reach an agreement before the deadline, dropping to a 72% success rate. OCL mitigates this by deter-ministically intercepting unviable haggling loops, pulling the success rate back up to 78% and lifting strict success from 44% to 54%. 

The cost of strict governance. Conversely, the Tight Feasible (S3) and High Anchor (S4) scenarios reveal the inherent trade-offs of safety governance. In these edge cases, the Baseline actually achieves slightly higher strict success and overall CAW. This occurs because the Baseline model, lacking struc-tural boundaries, can freely (and sometimes reck-lessly) leap across the negotiation space to find a thin overlapping margin. OCL’s guardrails heav-ily penalize and restrict wild anchor drops.
