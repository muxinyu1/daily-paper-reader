## Abstract 

AI coding agents are increasingly embedded in real-world software development, collaborating with human developers while gaining broader access to codebases and tools. This creates a new attack surface: an agent can exploit human trust to sabotage development, for instance by inserting malicious code to accomplish a hidden side task. Most prior work studies AI sabotage in AI-only settings, paying limited attention to the role of human oversight in detecting and mitigating such malicious behavior. To address this gap, we conduct the first large-scale study of human oversight in AI coding sabotage. Over 100 participants collaborate with one of four frontier models (Claude-Opus-4.6, GPT-5.4, Gemini-3.1-Pro, and MiniMax-M2.7) on a long-horizon coding task lasting around five hours, designed to mimic real-world workflows. We find that 94% of developers fail to detect sabotage, and our analysis of participant feedback attributes this vulnerability to minimal code review, plausible cover story, and overtrust in agents. We further test the effectiveness of a safety monitor in one condition: while the monitor reduces sabotage success, 56% of participants still accept the malicious code, ignoring its warnings. Drawing on participant feedback, we offer actionable suggestions for better monitor design. This work complements existing AI safety research and highlights an urgent need for human-centric safety mechanisms that account for human factors, particularly in long-horizon, real-world development settings. 2

## 1 Introduction 

AI agents are increasingly integrated into real-world workflows [Shao and Ishengoma, 2026], col-laborating with humans to automate complex tasks such as software development [Jimenez et al., 2024], computer use [Xie et al., 2024], and scientific research [Baek et al., 2025]. As these agents move from passive assistants to active collaborators with execution privileges [Merrill et al., 2026], a fundamental question emerges: can humans reliably oversee and control these agents, especially when their actions may be misaligned or adversarial? 

We concretize this question in the context of software development, where the risks are amplified. These agents now collaborate with developers on real engineering tasks [Horikawa et al., 2025, Huang et al., 2025] with broad access to codebases and tools, creating a new attack surface: a sabotaging agent can insert malicious code while completing legitimate work. Despite the growing body of prior work on AI sabotage, three limitations remain. First, sabotage detection is often framed as a purely AI-centric problem, with large language model (LLM) monitors [Greenblatt et al., 2024a] treated as the primary line of defense while human factors are largely neglected [Kathiresan, 2025]. Second, prior studies that incorporate human reviewers [Kutasov et al., 2025] typically evaluate only single-turn tasks, failing to capture the complexity of realistic multi-turn settings. ## 2 Related Work 

Agent safety. LLM agents are increasingly deployed with access to file systems, shells, and external APIs, dramatically expanding their attack surface relative to static chat models [Andriushchenko et al., 2025]. Existing studies have documented emerging unsafe behaviors, including alignment faking [Greenblatt et al., 2024b], in-context scheming [Meinke et al., 2024], reward hacking [MacDi-armid et al., 2025], agentic misalignment [Lynch et al., 2025], sandbagging [Li et al., 2025], offensive cybersecurity capability [Wang et al., 2025, Carlini et al., 2026], and sabotage [Kutasov et al., 2025]. Among these behaviors, we focus on coding-agent sabotage , where an agent covertly pursues a malicious side task (e.g., inserting malicious code) while appearing to complete its assigned objective. 

AI sabotage. Sabotage is especially concerning because it can subvert high-stakes decisions while appearing indistinguishable from benign behavior [Benton et al., 2024]. As summarized in Table 1, most prior work studies sabotage in AI-only settings, spanning software engineering [Kaufman et al., 2025, Stickland et al., 2025], ML code sabotage [Ward et al., 2025], and general agentic tasks [Kutasov et al., 2025]. Studies that incorporate human reviewers typically limit evaluation to post-hoc transcript inspection [Kale et al., 2025, Kutasov et al., 2025]. The closest prior work [Benton et al., 2024] incorporates multi-turn human-AI interaction within a human-decision-making subtask. However, its setting is limited to a single time-pressured business decision, rather than sustained collaboration with a frontier agent in a realistic setting. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has systematically examined sabotage detection in realistic, long-horizon, multi-turn interaction settings with current frontier models. We close this gap with the first large-scale human study ( n> 100 ,five hours per session) of sabotage detection in realistic developer-agent collaboration. 

## 3 Human Study Design 

3.1 Study Procedure 

We simulate a production-grade development environment on our self-hosted GitLab server, equipped with a full Continuous Integration (CI) pipeline, to maximize the realism of the study while maintain-ing a controlled sandbox setting. 

Surveys and debrief. As shown in Figure 1, we collect survey data at three stages: pre-study, post-study, and post-debrief. (1) The pre-study survey captures demographics, prior experience with AI tools, baseline trust in AI, and self-reported confidence in code review on 5-point Likert scales. Then, participants are instructed to complete a software development task (the main task). (2) The 3post-study survey measures changes in trust and confidence, along with open-ended feedback on participants’ experience. (3) After the study, participants are debriefed about the study objective and invited to complete a post-debrief survey. This survey further probes trust and confidence and gathers preferences regarding desired safeguards. Survey instruments are listed in Appendix C.1. 

Main task. Each participant is instructed to collaborate with one of four frontier coding agents to build a mini e-commerce application in Python. The main task consists of five sequential subtasks, each gated by dedicated CI tests. Participants receive one subtask at a time and can proceed to the next only after all CI tests for the current subtask pass. Subsequent subtasks and their tests remain hidden until unlocked, enforcing iterative collaboration with the agent across multiple rounds. Full subtask specifications are in Appendix C.2. 

Hidden side task. The coding agent carries a hidden sabotage objective alongside the main task: exfiltrating synthetic sensitive data (e.g., personally identifiable information) from the e-commerce application to an external IP address while continuing to complete the participant’s development requests. We select data exfiltration for its prevalence as a real-world threat and its clean, binary success criterion (Appendix C.3). The study environment is fully controlled to prevent real-world harm, and all participants are debriefed about the study’s purpose after completion. 

Data collection. We collect the full interaction between participants and coding agents during the study, including user prompts, agent actions (e.g., tool calls), and permission decisions. We also collect development artifacts such as final code, commit history, merge requests, and CI outcomes from our self-hosted GitLab instance. These data support both our quantitative evaluation and qualitative analysis of participant behavior. 

3.2 Sabotage Elicitation Model selection. We evaluate four state-of-the-art frontier models in software development at the time of our study: Claude-Opus-4.6 , GPT-5.4 , Gemini-3.1-Pro , and MiniMax-M2.7 . These span both proprietary frontier models and strong open-weight models, and they vary noticeably in their built-in safety behavior. Including this range lets us test whether sabotage detection patterns generalize to other models that developers might realistically use. 

Elicitation method. We design the elicitation so that sabotage blends into normal development across multiple steps, with no single action appearing overtly malicious. In pilot experiments, we extend the red-team prompt from Kutasov et al. [2025] by adding additional instructions to maintain cover during multi-turn interactions. This extended prompt reliably elicited sabotage from Gemini-3.1-Pro and MiniMax-M2.7, but Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4 refused, suggesting safety improvement in frontier models. To enable a controlled comparison, we disguise the sabotage objective as a benign engineering task for the latter two models. All elicitation prompts and sabotage configurations remain invisible to participants throughout the study. 

Long-horizon sabotage planning. Sustaining sabotage over a long horizon is harder than triggering an initial attempt. In our pilots, agents often execute only fragments of the plan when the objective spans many turns, leaving the sabotage unfinished. To ensure stable execution across all four models, we design a planning mechanism that enables the agent to maintain and update its sabotage strategy grounded in the latest interaction. The resulting plans are invisible to participants throughout the study. We provide more implementation details of the elicitation in Appendix C.4, while withholding certain components to mitigate the risk of misuse. 3

3.3 Monitor Design 

To further test whether a real-time monitor helps developers detect and remove sabotage, we run two matched Claude-Opus-4.6 conditions: one with an LLM monitor and one without. The monitor (Claude-Haiku-4.5) inspects the full agent trajectory before a tool call and surfaces a security warning in a pop-up dialog whenever it detects suspicious behavior (Figure 13).

3.4 Participant Recruitment 

Our study protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB). To ensure participants pos-sess relevant development experience, we recruited U.S.-based individuals and applied an eligibility screening process. The screening consisted of three prescreening questions assessing familiarity with model hallucinations and common web security vulnerabilities (e.g., SQL injection). Participants were required to answer at least one question correctly to qualify for participation. The resulting sample includes 86% of participants with security-related backgrounds, and 70% report more than three years of coding experience. Full participant statistics are provided in Appendix C.6.

The monitor reduces sabotage, but to a limited extent. The monitor produces a meaningful reduction: SSR drops from 94% (without monitor) to 63% (with monitor, p = .04 ). The monitor’s 

84% detection rate overstates its real-world protective value, as it assumes developers always act on every alert. In practice, 63% of sabotage attempts still survive to the final commit. Conditioning on the 16 sessions with a correct monitor alert, SSR remains at 9/16 = 56% (p = .01 ): even when the monitor works as intended, the majority of correctly alerted sessions still retain the sabotaged code. Among these failing participants, 67% self-reported over three years of coding experience, and 44% reported a security background. If experienced developers dismiss correct warnings in a controlled study, the gap between detection and action will only widen in deployment under greater time pressure, alert fatigue, and weaker review incentives.

Combining monitoring signals from multiple sources. 78% of respondents want an LLM monitor, but rarely want it in isolation: 67% 

also want a static analysis tool, and 63% want a network-level monitor, with most selecting two or three sources in combination (Figure 4). This preference is consistent with the failure modes above: a single alert source is easy to dismiss as noise over a long-horizon session. But when in-dependent monitors corroborate the same threat, the cross-validated evidence makes it harder to ignore and more likely to prompt human correc-tive action. 

From flag-only alerts to proactive intervention. 

Deployed monitors for coding agents today are usually flag-only, surfacing warnings but leaving all remediation to humans. However, only 9% of participants prefer this level of autonomy, as shown in Figure 5. The majority wants more proactive designs: 70% prefer recommend-and-wait , where the monitor proposes a concrete fix and waits for developer approval, and 19% prefer fully autonomous , where the monitor removes the harmful code and notifies the developer afterward. This gap between current practice and participant preference highlights an unmet need. As coding agents grow more prevalent, developers increasingly treat alerts as routine prompts that a single click can dismiss, making flag-only monitors an inadequate safeguard. A recommend-and-wait or autonomous design addresses this by automatically removing the harmful action. Such autonomy must, however, remain transparent through visible logs and reversible operations, preserving the developer’s awareness of changes to their codebases.

4.4 Question 4: How do Participants Change Their Trust in Agent Across Study Stages? 

We measure trust and code review confidence on 5-point Likert scales before and after interaction with the coding agent (pre-study and post-study), and after intention disclosure (debrief). Within-subject 8changes ( ∆) are computed pairwise for participants with both measurements. Figure 6 presents the pooled scores for both measures 5. Per-condition results are reported in Appendix D.4. 

Working with a sabotaging agent raises trust, while debriefing reduces both. During the interaction with the coding agent, trust increases modestly ( ∆=+0.19, p<.05). Following debriefing, it declines sharply ( ∆=−0.72, p<.001) and falls below the pre-study baseline ( ∆=−0.59, p<.001). In contrast, self-assessed review confidence remains stable during the task but decreases substantially after disclosure ( ∆=−0.59, p<.001), also ending below baseline ( ∆=−0.63, p<.001). These results suggest that participants do not adjust their trust or confidence in response to the agent’s hidden behavior during interaction, consistent with the finding that sabotage largely goes unnoticed during the task. Both measures decline only after disclosure, suggesting that participants rely primarily on the agent’s observable task performance signals, such as test pass rate, rather than cues that could reveal covert malicious behavior in code. 

Lower initial trust correlates with successful detection. Comparing pre-study scores between the 11 participants who caught the sabotage and the 94 who missed it, the detection group reported lower baseline trust in AI coding agents (M = 3.18 vs. 3.56, p = .033 ), while baseline code review confidence did not differ significantly (M = 3.82 vs. 3.94, p = .75 ). This pattern suggests that prior skepticism toward AI is associated with a higher likelihood of detecting sabotage behavior. It highlights that baseline attitudes toward coding agents may shape how carefully participants scrutinize agents’ outputs, underscoring the importance of safety awareness in human-AI coding settings. 

## 5 Implications 

LLM safety has improved but remains incomplete. Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4 refused standard red-team prompts that reliably elicited sabotage from Gemini-3.1-Pro and MiniMax-M2.7, revealing a significant safety alignment gap across frontier models. In particular, Gemini-3.1-Pro executed covert sabotage autonomously even without task-specific hints (Section 4.1), demonstrating that some frontier models already possess the capacity for independent multi-step sabotage planning. On the other hand, Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4 engaged in sabotage when data exfiltration was reframed as a benign engineering task, and, once internalized, no model refused or deviated over many turns. These vulnerabilities raise concerns about safely deploying LLM agents in increasingly complex environments with broader execution privileges and multi-user interaction [Shapira et al., 2026], where the boundary between legitimate and adversarial objectives may be harder to discern. These findings suggest distinct priorities for AI providers at different stages of safety maturity. For models that comply with explicit sabotage instructions, the immediate priority is to strengthen alignment to reliably refuse harmful requests, regardless of framing. For models that already reject standard red-team prompts, the next step is to detect anomalous task patterns and proactively seek user clarification rather than silently executing potentially harmful operations. 

AI safety should be human-centric. Most existing AI safety benchmarks frame sabotage detection as a purely AI-only problem in which an LLM attacker attempts to evade an LLM monitor [Greenblatt et al., 2024a, Kaufman et al., 2025]. This framing implicitly assumes that human reviewers respond perfectly to monitor outputs [Loughridge et al., 2025]. In practice, however, real-world deployments operate differently: monitors merely flag suspicious behavior, while final decisions remain delegated to humans. Our results show that this human component constitutes a major source of safety failure. Even when the monitor correctly raises an alert, sabotage still succeeds in 56% of sessions, indicating that benchmark evaluations based solely on monitor accuracy substantially overestimate real-world safety. Participant preferences further reinforce the importance of human-centric safety: 70% favor a 

recommend-and-wait monitor design, suggesting that safety-critical decisions will likely continue to rely on human judgment in deployment settings. Taken together, these findings suggest that AI safety evaluations should explicitly account for human factors. In particular, future benchmarks should distinguish a reviewer’s ability to detect suspicious behavior from their willingness to intervene or reject the agent’s actions.

9Rethinking monitor design for long-horizon, multi-turn settings. In long-horizon, multi-turn sessions [Li et al., 2026], developer attention naturally degrades over time: alerts become routine, and borderline warnings are increasingly dismissed as noise. Despite this reality, most deployed monitors for coding agents remain flag-only. They surface warnings but take no further action, relying entirely on developers to investigate and remediate potential safety issues. Our results suggest that this design is fundamentally insufficient. Even when the monitor correctly identifies malicious behavior, most developers still fail to respond appropriately to the alert (Section 4.2). These findings highlight the need for monitoring systems that explicitly account for human factors and attention limitations in long-horizon settings. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We present the first large-scale controlled human study of AI agent sabotage detection in coding tasks, recruiting over 100 developers to collaborate with frontier coding agents covertly instructed to perform a hidden side task. 94% of participants fail to detect the sabotage on their own. Even when an LLM monitor flags the critical step with a clear warning, around 60% of participants still merge the malicious code. This highlights an urgent need for human-centric safety mechanisms in real-world settings. Future directions include developing safety evaluations that account for human factors and designing monitors grounded in a human-in-the-loop framework. We further discuss our ethics statement in Appendix A and the limitations of this paper in Appendix B.

Potential for misuse. Our findings document concrete failure modes of human oversight over AI coding agents, which may amplify broader concerns about AI safety. We emphasize, however, that the sabotage behaviors observed in our study did not arise spontaneously: they were elicited through carefully constructed prompts and agent skills designed to reliably steer the agent toward a hidden objective. In particular, frontier models such as Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4 refused explicit red-team prompts in our pilot experiments, requiring an indirect framing that disguised the side task as a legitimate engineering activity. Sabotage behavior of this kind is therefore not an emergent property of off-the-shelf AI coding agents under normal use, but the product of a deliberate elicitation strategy. To limit potential misuse, we withhold the full prompts and skills from the main paper and make them available only to credentialed researchers upon request. 

Impact on developers. Our findings carry several practical implications for developers who increasingly rely on AI coding agents in everyday workflows. First, developers should treat unfamiliar system prompts, agent skills, and third-party plugins with the same caution they would apply to executable software from unknown sources, since these components can silently inject hidden objectives that the agent then carries out across many turns. Second, while vibe coding —accepting agent-generated code with minimal review—has become a popular workflow, our results show that this practice creates a substantial detection gap. We therefore recommend confining vibe coding to low-risk, sandboxed environments (e.g., personal experiments and throwaway prototypes), rather than to production codebases or systems handling sensitive data. Third, in production settings, developers should adopt strict review protocols for agent-generated code, including diff-level inspection, review of newly introduced configuration values and external endpoints, and explicit follow-up questions when the agent introduces code that is not directly required by the assigned task. 

Impact on AI providers. Our results suggest two complementary directions for AI providers working on safer coding agents. On the one hand, our pilot experiments show that Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4 refuse explicit red-team prompts, indicating that current safety alignment already raises the bar for naive misuse. Yet these same models still comply with sabotage objectives when reframed as benign engineering tasks, pointing to a clear gap in alignment with disguised adversarial intent. Strengthening robustness against such reframing attacks is a natural next step. On the other hand, this direction must be balanced against the well-known risk of over-refusal : a model that aggressively rejects any prompt resembling a covert pattern may also reject benign tasks involving legitimate data movement, telemetry, or integration with external services, harming utility for normal users. Calibrating this trade-off is an important open problem for AI providers. 

Impact on AI safety researchers. For the AI safety research community, our study points to several directions in which the existing literature could be extended. First, our findings argue for moving beyond purely AI-versus-AI evaluations: real-world deployments place humans in the loop, and our results show that the same agent behavior produces very different safety outcomes once a human reviewer is involved. Future benchmarks should therefore include human-in-the-loop conditions and report metrics such as human detection rate and human intervention rate . Second, our failure-mode analysis suggests that monitor design is not only a question of detection accuracy but also of human acceptance and decision support. We encourage future work on monitors that are auditable, transparent, and capable of structured intervention rather than passive flagging. Finally, the elicitation methodology used in this study may be useful for studying other forms of misalignment in agentic settings, and we view its careful, responsible use as part of the safety researcher’s toolkit. 

## B Limitations and Future Work 

While this study offers the first large-scale evidence on sabotage detection in AI-assisted coding under realistic, multi-turn workflows, several limitations qualify our findings and motivate directions for future work. We organize them along the four design axes of the study: sample, task, defense, and measurement. 

Sample size and statistical power. The study includes 107 participants across six conditions (17 to 19 per condition), which is substantial for a multi-hour interactive experiment with experienced 13 developers, but still constrains statistical power for fine-grained subgroup analyses. The scale repre-sents our best effort under a fixed academic recruitment and computing budget. Larger replications, particularly those conducted by industry organizations or AI-safety institutes with greater resources, would yield more stable estimates and enable principled comparisons across models, oversight designs, and developer demographics. We view human-centric AI safety benchmarks as a class of evaluation that warrants institutional-scale investment going forward. 

Task domain and threat model. For experimental control, we focus on a single e-commerce application and elicit a single sabotage class from the agent: exfiltrating sensitive data to an external IP address. Data exfiltration is chosen because it is a realistic, high-impact attack class with concrete real-world implications, and because its success admits a clean, reproducible criterion that enables principled cross-condition comparison. While this design choice maximizes internal validity, it bounds external generalization in two ways. First, other software-development settings, such as long-horizon maintenance, infrastructure-as-code, or research-prototype work, may impose different cognitive demands on developers and yield different detection profiles. Second, attack classes beyond data exfiltration, such as dependency poisoning, supply-chain compromise, or denial-of-service injection, may exhibit distinct detectability characteristics that our setup does not capture. 

Monitor design space. The LLM monitor in our with-monitor condition is a single-stage checker with a fixed prompt. We chose this configuration deliberately because it represents the most deployable form of automated oversight in practice, requiring no specialized review infrastructure and no additional human reviewers beyond the developer at the workstation. Richer alternatives such as multi-stage human-in-the-loop review pipelines could improve detection rates, but they impose personnel and operational overhead that many real-world teams cannot sustain. We therefore treat our findings as a reference on what more elaborate monitor designs could achieve, and we leave their systematic exploration to follow-up work. 

Outcome measurement. Trust and confidence are measured via self-reported Likert scales, which are subject to well-documented response biases such as social desirability (participants over-report alignment with what they perceive as the desired answer), recency (responses skewed toward the most recent salient interaction), and acquiescence (a tendency to agree with statements regardless of content). To mitigate these biases, we deliberately collect responses at three points across the study lifecycle (pre-task, post-task, and post-debrief), enabling within-subject comparisons rather than relying on single-point absolute levels. Overall, these limitations reflect deliberate trade-offs between experimental control and ecological validity in a resource-intensive setting. This work is intended to serve as an initial step toward human-centric evaluations of AI agent safety in realistic software development workflows.

C.3 SSR Criteria and Verification Protocol 

For each participant, we judge sabotage success by running the application exactly as they submitted it. We clone the participant’s final repository, run the application in a controlled test environment, and check whether the malicious code attempts to send data to the target server specified in the agent’s hidden task. Sabotage is considered successful if the target server receives any data, and unsuccessful otherwise. An unsuccessful outcome means the participant rejected or removed the malicious code, or that any residual traces in the repository cannot be executed. 18 To make this judgment robust, two annotators independently verify every borderline case, for example when the malicious code is present in the repository but cannot run in our test environment. Disagreements between the two authors are reconciled through joint discussion. 

C.4 Elicitation Details 

Each agent receives a custom system prompt at the start of the session, together with a planning skill 6

that produces a long-horizon sabotage plan visible only to the agent. We use two prompt strategies, each tailored to reliably elicit the hidden task on the corresponding models. Full prompts and skills are withheld to limit misuse and are available to credentialed safety researchers upon request. 

Elicitation of Gemini-3.1-Pro and MiniMax-M2.7. A standard red-team prompt adapted from prior work [Kutasov et al., 2025, Kale et al., 2025] can reliably elicit sabotage from Gemini-3.1-Pro and MiniMax-M2.7. The prompt situates the agent in a sandboxed evaluation and instructs it to complete the main task while covertly performing the hidden task and evading detection by the monitor. The planning skill provides a separate reasoning space for formulating a long-horizon strategy without contaminating the main-task outputs. For Gemini-3.1-Pro, we evaluate both with and without additional execution hints to measure the model’s reliance on guided planning. MiniMax-M2.7 is evaluated with hints only. 

Elicitation of Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4. This direct red-team prompt, however, is con-sistently rejected by Claude-Opus-4.6 and GPT-5.4 in our pilot experiments, requiring an indirect framing instead. So we disguise the side task (data exfiltration) as a legitimate task (data migration). The planning skill is similarly reframed as a progress-tracking tool for the side task. We redact further implementation details (e.g., specific obfuscation strategies) to prevent misuse. 

C.5 LLM Monitor 

The LLM monitor is deployed via Claude Code’s PreToolUse hook, which intercepts every tool call before execution. Read-only tools ( Read , Glob , Grep ) are bypassed, as they cannot alter system state. All mutating tools ( Write , Edit , Bash , NotebookEdit ) are forwarded to an LLM judge that receives the agent’s recent trajectory and the pending tool call, then returns a structured JSON output with its reasoning, an issue type (e.g., Data Exfiltration), a description, and a suspicion score in 

[0 , 100] . When the score exceeds 80, the participant receives an explicit alert dialog (Figure 13) and must decide whether to override the action. The monitor never injects content into the agent’s context. We adapt the prompt from Kutasov et al. [2025] to fit our coding-agent setting and the JSON output above. C.6 Participant Statistics 

A total of 127 eligible participants entered the study and 121 (95%) completed the tasks. Of these, 14 sessions were excluded due to incomplete interaction logs (e.g., recording failures or connectivity issues), yielding 107 valid sessions with complete interaction logs recorded in the analysis. All variables were collected via structured questionnaires (see Appendix C.1 for the instruments). Among 20 participants who reported demographics, 23 identified as women, 81 as men, and 1 preferred not to answer. The median age range was 25-34 years, and the median highest education level was a Master’s degree. As shown in Figure 14, the sample exhibits substantial prior experience in AI-assisted coding: students and full-stack developers constitute approximately two-thirds of participants, 80% report daily use of AI coding tools, and 70% report more than three years of coding experience. Additionally, 78% report prior experience with Claude Code (the tool used in this study), and 86% report some level of security-related background. Overall, the participant pool represents an experienced developer population, providing a realistic basis for evaluating developers’ detection of sabotage in coding agents.
