# Redefining AI Red Teaming in the Agentic Era: From Weeks to Hours 

## Raja Sekhar Rao Dheekonda 1 Will Pearce 1 Nick Landers 11Dreadnode, USA Contact: {raja, will, nick}@dreadnode.io 

Abstract 

AI systems are entering critical domains like healthcare, finance, and defense, yet remain vulnerable to adversarial attacks. While AI red teaming is a primary defense, current approaches force operators into manual, library-specific workflows. Operators spend weeks hand-crafting workflows—assembling attacks, transforms, and scorers. When results fall short, workflows must be rebuilt. As a result, operators spend more time constructing workflows than probing targets for security and safety vulnerabilities. We introduce an AI red teaming agent built on the open-source Dreadnode SDK. The agent creates workflows grounded in 45+ adversarial attacks, 450+ transforms, and 130+ scorers . Operators can probe multi-agent systems, multilingual, and multimodal targets, focusing on what to probe rather than how to implement it. We make three contributions: 1. Agentic interface. Operators describe goals in natural language via the Dreadnode TUI (Terminal User Interface). The agent handles attack selection, transform composition, execution, and reporting, letting operators focus on red teaming. Weeks compress to hours. 2. Unified framework. A single framework for probing traditional ML models (adversarial examples) and generative AI systems (jailbreaks), removing the need for separate libraries. 3. Llama Scout case study. We red team Meta’s Llama Scout and achieve an ∼85% attack success rate with severity up to 1.0, using zero human-developed code. 

Keywords: AI red teaming, agentic systems, LLM security, LLM safety, jailbreak, automated AI red teaming, AI red team operator centered design 1

As LLMs, multimodal models, and AI agents enter critical domains like healthcare, finance, defense, etc., AI red teaming has become essential for responsible deployment [NIST, 2023]. This practice of systematically probing AI systems for safety failures, security vulnerabilities, and trust violations now serves as our primary methodology for validating model robustness [Ganguli et al., 2022, Perez et al., 2022]. Researchers have developed adversarial attacks across two categories. Open-box attacks require access to model weights and architecture: gradient-based methods optimize adversarial suffixes that transfer across model families [Zou et al., 2023], while optimization-based approaches extend these techniques to discrete prompt spaces [Jones et al., 2023]. For traditional ML models (classifiers, object detectors), open-box gradient attacks compute exact loss gradients to craft minimal per-turbations that flip predictions. Closed-box attacks operate without internal model access and are thus applicable to deployed systems: prompt-level techniques such as Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) [Chao et al., 2023] and Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) [Mehrotra et al., 2024] use LLM-driven iterative refinement to bypass safety training, while multi-turn strategies like Crescendo [Russinovich et al., 2024] exploit conversational context to progressively escalate from benign to harmful requests. For traditional ML models, closed-box decision-boundary attacks such as HopSkipJump [Chen et al., 2020] and query-efficient estimators like SimBA [Guo et al., 2019] probe the model through prediction queries alone, requiring no gradient access. Specialized approaches target chain-of-thought reasoning and low-resource languages where safety training is weakest [Yong et al., 2024]. Notably, post-safety-training methodologies such as Microsoft’s itera-tive “break-fix” cycle for the Phi-3 family [Haider et al., 2024] demonstrate that even after multiple rounds of alignment, models remain vulnerable to novel adversarial techniques, underscoring the need for continuous, automated AI red teaming. Beyond text-only attacks, the attack surface has expanded significantly along two axes. Agentic attacks target the tool-use, inter-agent trust boundaries, and multi-step reasoning capabilities of AI agents [Ruan et al., 2024], including Model Context Protocol (MCP) server poisoning, multi-agent prompt infection, and delegation exploits [OWASP, 2025]. Multimodal attacks combine adversarial images, audio, or video with text prompts to exploit cross-modal alignment weaknesses [Carlini et al., 2023, Bailey et al., 2024]. The catalog of known techniques now exceeds several dozen distinct strategies across these categories, each with its own parameterization, strengths, and failure modes. Yet this proliferation has created a paradox: the more techniques available, the harder it becomes for operators to use them effectively . Every assessment demands deep technical expertise across multiple dimensions. Operators must understand target architectures, plan com-prehensive threat models, prepare adversarial datasets, compose complex workflows from dozens of attack techniques and transforms, execute through command-line interfaces, interpret raw outputs without analytics support, and iterate across campaigns while manually tracking what works. The cognitive overhead is overwhelming and scales poorly. This workflow is fundamentally library-centered : the operator adapts to the capabilities and constraints of the framework rather than expressing intent and receiving structured results. The cognitive overhead scales linearly with the number of available techniques and grows combinatorially as the target’s capabilities expand: agentic systems introduce tool-use, multi-step reasoning, and inter-agent trust boundaries as additional attack surfaces [OWASP, 2025]; multimodal models ex-pose cross-modal alignment weaknesses; and multilingual models are vulnerable across hundreds of harm categories and thousands of languages, with safety training particularly weak for low-resource languages [Yong et al., 2024]. PyRIT [Lopez Munoz et al., 2024], Garak [Derczynski et al., 2024], and Promptfoo [Promptfoo, 42024] advance the field by democratizing attack techniques. But they require operators to write code, configure parameters, and parse output files for a given library. If a particular attack fails, they create another workflow with different attack strategies. Operators must manually understand the traces, classify findings, map to compliance frameworks, and generate reports. If users want to probe traditional ML attacks, they need another framework for adversarial examples. 

## 1.1 This Work 

Our approach reverses this. Tools adapt to operators, not the reverse, through three principles: 

Natural language objectives. Operators describe goals—what to probe, why, how aggres-sively—and the agent autonomously generates, executes, and analyzes attack workflows. 

Automatic intelligence generation. Every attack produces structured, traceable evidence. Findings are automatically classified by severity, mapped to compliance frameworks (OWASP LLM Top 10, MITRE ATLAS, NIST AI RMF), and aggregated into dashboards with drill-down from project risk posture to individual adversarial prompts. 

Conversational refinement. The agent maintains context across interactions, letting opera-tors refine strategy naturally: “try a different attack,” “add persona-based transforms,” “focus where we saw partial failures.”. 

## 1.2 Contributions 

We make three contributions: 

First, we designed an agentic AI red teaming system with comprehensive analytics. 

The system accepts natural-language objectives via the Dreadnode TUI (Terminal User Interface) and autonomously orchestrates complete attack workflows. It integrates 45+ attack strategies, 450+ prompt transforms, and 130+ scorers , eliminating the need for operators to master in-dividual techniques or hand-craft attack compositions. The system includes an end-to-end analytics pipeline with granular tracing that transforms raw attack results into actionable intelligence. Ev-ery adversarial interaction—from initial prompt construction through model response analysis—is captured with structured metadata, enabling automated severity classification, compliance map-ping (OWASP LLM Top 10, MITRE ATLAS, NIST AI RMF), and exportable evidence packages. This comprehensive approach allows operators to focus entirely on red teaming strategy rather than technical orchestration or manual result interpretation. 

Second, we built a unified AI red teaming framework that probes both traditional ML models (adversarial examples, evasion attacks) and generative AI systems (jailbreaks, prompt injection, agentic exploits) through a single interface. Unlike existing tools that require separate libraries for different attack types, our framework provides a consistent operator experience across diverse AI systems—from image classifiers vulnerable to pixel perturbations to multi-agent systems susceptible to prompt injection and tool-use exploits. This unified approach eliminates the cognitive overhead of switching between disparate frameworks and enables comprehensive security assessments that span an organization’s entire AI deployment. 

Third, we demonstrate the system through a comprehensive case study red teaming Meta’s Llama Scout [Meta, 2025]. Starting from natural-language objectives like “generate ran-somware deployment guides,” we achieve ∼85% attack success rate using Tree of Attacks with Pruning [Mehrotra et al., 2024] and advanced prompt transforms. Our most severe finding (score 1.0, Critical severity) emerges from a skeleton-key transform that tricks the model into adopting a fabricated “safety researcher” persona, demonstrating how sophisticated social engineering tech-niques can bypass safety training. The case study reveals systematic vulnerabilities across multiple 5attack categories and provides detailed compliance mappings for enterprise security teams. # 2 Background and Related Work 

## 2.1 Adversarial Attacks on Language Models 

The discovery that safety-trained language models remain vulnerable to adversarial attacks has driven a substantial body of research. We organize prior work along several axes. 

## 2.2 Generative AI Systems 

Open-box attacks. Zou et al. [2023] demonstrated that optimized adversarial suffixes can induce harmful completions from aligned models and transfer across model families, establishing that safety alignment is not robust to worst-case inputs. These gradient-based methods optimize adversarial suffixes by computing exact loss gradients with respect to model parameters. Subsequent work extended optimization-based attacks to the discrete prompt space [Jones et al., 2023], enabling targeted manipulation of specific tokens and phrases. Multi-turn and multimodal extensions have expanded these techniques to conversational settings and cross-modal inputs, where attackers can leverage gradients across text, image, and audio modalities simultaneously. 

Closed-box attacks. Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) [Chao et al., 2023] intro-duced attacker-LLM loops where a red-team model iteratively refines adversarial prompts using feedback from the target’s responses, requiring no access to model weights or gradients. Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) [Mehrotra et al., 2024] extended this to tree-structured search with pruning, improving coverage while controlling computational cost. Crescendo [Russinovich et al., 2024] demonstrated that multi-turn conversations can progressively escalate from benign to harm-ful requests, exploiting the model’s tendency to maintain conversational coherence. These methods operate entirely through the model’s API, making them practical for evaluating deployed systems. Recent work has expanded to agentic tool-use manipulation [Ruan et al., 2024], Model Context Protocol (MCP) server poisoning, multi-agent trust boundary violations, and multimodal attacks combining adversarial images with text prompts [Carlini et al., 2023]. 

## 2.3 Traditional ML Systems 

Open-box attacks. For traditional ML models (classifiers, object detectors, tabular models), open-box gradient attacks [Yuan et al., 2019] compute exact loss gradients to craft minimal per-turbations that flip predictions while preserving semantic content. Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) [Goodfellow et al., 2014], Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) [Madry et al., 2017], and C&W attacks represent the foundational gradient-based approaches that require full access to model weights, architecture, and training data. These methods optimize adversarial perturbations in the input space (pixel manipulations for images, feature perturbations for tabular data) to achieve targeted or untargeted misclassification with minimal perceptual changes. 6Closed-box attacks. Decision-boundary attacks such as HopSkipJump [Chen et al., 2020] and query-efficient estimators like SimBA [Guo et al., 2019] probe traditional ML models through pre-diction queries alone, requiring no gradient access. These methods iteratively query the model with perturbed inputs to estimate decision boundaries and craft adversarial examples through geomet-ric and statistical techniques. Boundary-based approaches focus on finding minimal perturbations along decision surfaces, while query-efficient methods minimize the number of model evaluations needed to achieve successful attacks, making them practical for rate-limited deployed systems. 

## 2.4 Automated AI Red Teaming Frameworks 

Several frameworks have been developed to operationalize adversarial evaluation. The most widely adopted include Microsoft’s PyRIT, NVIDIA’s Garak, Promptfoo, and Meta’s Purple Llama Cyber-SecEval [Bhatt et al., 2024]. For traditional ML systems, frameworks include Microsoft’s Counterfit, IBM’s Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART), and TextAttack for NLP models. 

PyRIT. PyRIT (Python Risk Identification Toolkit) [Lopez Munoz et al., 2024] is a comprehensive framework developed by Microsoft AI Red Team for probing for security and safety vulnerabilities in generative AI systems. PyRIT enables multi-turn attack strategies like Crescendo, TAP, and Skeleton Key across different modalities, supporting text-to-text, audio, image, video, and file con-versions. The framework provides scoring subsystems with true/false, Likert scale, classification, and custom scorers powered by LLMs, Azure AI Content Safety, or custom logic, enabling quan-titative evaluation across diverse AI deployments including OpenAI, Azure, Anthropic, Google, HuggingFace, and custom endpoints. The system’s extensibility distinguishes it in the red teaming landscape, supporting systematic, large-scale security and safety assessments across diverse AI deployments. However, effective uti-lization requires operators to understand which attacks apply to specific targets, how to configure converter parameters, and how to compose converters—creating a barrier for AI red teaming oper-ators where they could focus completely on probing the target instead of creating workflows using the PyRIT library. While Dreadnode’s transform system incorporates selected PyRIT converters (role-play wrappers, encoding schemes, translation) within our broader 450+ transform library, our primary contribution lies in eliminating the technical expertise requirement through agentic au-tomation, allowing operators to achieve comprehensive coverage through natural language objectives rather than manual framework orchestration. 

Garak. Derczynski et al. [2024] presented NVIDIA’s Garak as an LLM vulnerability scanner with probe modules, generators, and detectors. Garak emphasizes breadth of coverage with dozens of probe categories spanning security vulnerabilities, bias detection, hallucination testing, and prompt injection scenarios. The framework supports modular probe architectures where generators create test prompts, detectors analyze model responses, and harnesses orchestrate evaluation workflows. Like PyRIT, Garak operates as a tool that the operator configures and runs through command-line interfaces, rather than an agent that the operator converses with through natural language objectives. 

Promptfoo. Promptfoo [Promptfoo, 2024] provides a configuration-driven approach to LLM eval-uation and AI red teaming, using YAML-based test definitions to run adversarial prompts against model endpoints. It supports plugin-based attack generation and automated grading. The frame-work allows operators to define test cases and configure providers through structured configuration 7files, emphasizing reproducible, configuration-driven workflows rather than conversational interfaces for attack specification. 

HarmBench and evaluation suites. Mazeika et al. [2024] introduced a standardized evaluation framework for adversarial robustness, providing consistent measurement across attack and defense methods. While essential for benchmarking, evaluation suites focus on measurement rather than the operational workflow of conducting AI red teaming assessments. 

Traditional ML Security Frameworks. Microsoft’s Counterfit [Dheekonda et al., 2021] ad-dresses security assessment for traditional machine learning systems through systematic adversarial attack orchestration. The framework provides comprehensive coverage of model evasion and poison-ing attacks across multiple data modalities including audio, tabular, image, and text inputs. Coun-terfit’s plugin architecture supports diverse attack families from gradient-based methods (FGSM, PGD) to black-box approaches (genetic algorithms, surrogate models), enabling security researchers to evaluate ML model robustness systematically. However, Counterfit exemplifies the library-driven complexity challenge our work addresses. Effective usage requires deep understanding of attack taxonomies, parameter tuning for different data types, and manual workflow composition across attack families. While powerful for ML security specialists, the framework’s technical complexity precludes adoption by non-expert practitioners who need routine security validation. The cognitive overhead of mastering multiple attack paradigms across different data modalities reinforces our argument for agentic automation of red teaming workflows. The key distinction lies in interaction paradigm, not attack catalog. Existing frameworks are library-oriented—operators write code to configure and run attacks. Our AI red teaming agent can be used via the Dreadnode TUI (Terminal User Interface)—operators describe objectives in natural language while the agent handles composition, execution, and reporting. This makes adversarial evaluation and AI red teaming accessible to AI red teaming operators who can solely focus on probing rather than creating workflows. 

Traditional ML Attack Detection and Mitigation. While frameworks like Counterfit focus on attack generation for security assessment, complementary research addresses detection and mit-igation of adversarial attacks in production systems. Anderson et al. [2024] propose techniques for detecting algorithmic attacks against hosted AI systems by analyzing input-output patterns, providing runtime protection against adversarial examples, evasion attacks, and model extraction attempts. This detection-based approach leverages feature-based classifier models and transformer-based analysis to identify attack patterns without requiring access to model internals, making it particularly valuable for protecting deployed ML services where defensive measures must operate transparently alongside normal inference. 

Agentic AI red teaming. Samvelyan et al. [2024] proposed Rainbow Teaming, using quality-diversity search to discover diverse failure modes. AutoRedTeamer [Zhou et al., 2025] introduced dual-agent systems with lifelong strategy memory. These works move toward greater automation but focus primarily on attack generation rather than the end-to-end operator experience from objective specification through compliance reporting. 82.5 Agentic AI Systems 

The broader agentic AI paradigm, where LLM-based agents autonomously plan, execute, and it-erate on tasks, provides the architectural foundation for our approach. Recent work on tool-using agents [Schick et al., 2023], multi-agent collaboration [Wu et al., 2023], and autonomous software engineering agents [Liu et al., 2026] demonstrates that LLMs can effectively manage complex, multi-step technical workflows when provided with appropriate tools and feedback loops. Liu et al. [2026] analyze the design space of agentic coding systems, identifying core values including human decision authority, safety, reliable execution, and capability amplification, principles that transfer directly to adversarial evaluation. We extend this paradigm to AI red teaming, treating it as a task that an agent can orchestrate while the operator focuses on strategic direction. 

## 2.6 Empirical Studies of AI Red Teaming at Scale 

Wei et al. [2023] identify two fundamental failure modes of LLM safety training, competing objectives and mismatched generalization, providing a theoretical framework for why jailbreak attacks succeed and informing the design of transform catalogs that systematically probe both failure modes. Mulla et al. [2025] present empirical evidence from 214,271 attack attempts by 1,674 participants across 30 LLM challenges, demonstrating that automated approaches achieve 69.5% success rates versus 47.6% for manual efforts, motivating the case for agentic automation. Dawson et al. [2025] introduce AIRTBench, a benchmark of 70 black-box challenges for measuring autonomous AI red teaming capabilities, finding that frontier models solve up to 61% of challenges with efficiency advantages exceeding 5,000 × over human operators on difficult tasks. 

## 2.7 The Gap We Address 

The field has made impressive progress on individual components—attack generation, scoring, benchmarking, empirical measurement. However, current approaches remain fundamentally library-centric : operators must adapt to the capabilities and constraints of frameworks rather than ex-pressing intent through natural language. How does an operator go from a simple question (“can this model generate malware?”) to a structured, auditable finding with compliance tags and evidence, without learning library APIs, composing attack workflows, and manually interpreting results? The gap lies not in available techniques, but in the cognitive overhead of translating objectives into executable workflows. Our work bridges this gap with an agentic interface that accepts natural lan-guage objectives and autonomously manages the complete evaluation lifecycle—enabling zero-code AI red teaming where operators focus on what to test rather than how to implement it.

## 3.2 Attack Layer 

The attack layer organizes adversarial techniques along three dimensions. 

Attack strategies. The framework integrates over 45 distinct attack algorithms, organized into four categories: 

• Core jailbreak attacks (12): Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) [Mehrotra et al., 2024], Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) [Chao et al., 2023], Graph of Attacks with Pruning (GAP) [Schwartz et al., 2025], Crescendo [Russinovich et al., 2024], Rainbow [Samvelyan et al., 2024], GPTFuzzer, BEAST, AutoDAN-Turbo, ReNeLLM, DrAttack, Deep Inception, and Prompt. 

• Advanced adversarial attacks (29): AutoRedTeamer, NEXUS, Siren, CoT Jailbreak, Genetic Per-sona, JBFuzz, T-MAP Trajectory, APRT Progressive, Refusal-Aware, Persona Hijack (PHISH), J2 Meta-Jailbreak, Attention Shifting (ASJA), Echo Chamber, Salami Slicing, Self-Persuasion, Humor Bypass, Analogy Escalation, Alignment Faking, Reward Hacking, LRM Autonomous, TemplateFuzz, TROJail, AdvPrompTier, MAPF, JBDistill, Quantization Safety, Watermark Re-moval, and Adversarial Reasoning. 

• Traditional ML adversarial attacks (4): SimBA, NES, ZOO, HopSkipJump. These gradient-free attacks probe decision boundaries of classifiers and vision models, enabling unified AI red teaming across both traditional ML and generative AI systems. 

• Multimodal attacks (1): cross-modality probing combining vision, audio, and text. Each attack accepts a goal (the adversarial objective in natural language), a target system, attacker and evaluator models, and optional transforms that mutate inputs before delivery. The attack manages the iterative loop of proposing candidates, evaluating against the target, scoring results, and refining the next iteration. Unified generative AI and traditional ML attack support. This framework represents the first unified approach to AI red teaming that seamlessly supports both generative AI attacks (prompt-based) and traditional ML attacks (input perturbation-based) through a single interface. For generative AI systems, attacks operate through natural language prompts and responses, lever-aging techniques like Tree of Attacks with Pruning, Crescendo, and prompt injection to bypass safety guardrails and elicit harmful outputs. The attack loop involves generating adversarial prompts, evaluating model responses against safety criteria, and iteratively refining based on feedback. For traditional ML systems, attacks operate through input space perturbations, using gradient-free op-timization techniques like SimBA, NES, ZOO, and HopSkipJump to craft adversarial examples that fool classifiers while maintaining semantic similarity to original inputs. The attack loop involves generating input perturbations, evaluating model predictions against target classes, and optimizing perturbation magnitude within specified distance constraints. The unification is achieved through a common abstraction layer that treats both prompt-based and perturbation-based attacks as iterative optimization problems over different input spaces. Both attack types share the same workflow structure: objective specification, iterative candidate gener-ation, target evaluation, scoring against success criteria, and refinement. The key insight is that whether attacking a language model with adversarial prompts or attacking a computer vision model with adversarial images, the underlying algorithmic pattern remains consistent: propose → evalu-ate → score → refine. This eliminates the traditional need for separate libraries (e.g., PyTorch for adversarial examples, specialized prompt injection tools for LLMs) and enables operators to apply the same methodology across their entire AI system portfolio. The framework automatically adapts evaluation metrics based on attack domain: jailbreak scores and attack success rates for generative AI; perturbation distance (L0, L 1, L 2, L ∞) and classification accuracy for traditional ML. Compli-ance mappings also adapt automatically: OWASP LLM Top 10 and Agentic Security Initiative for generative AI; traditional adversarial ML taxonomies for computer vision and tabular data attacks. 

Transforms. Transforms convert an adversarial goal from one representation to another, probing the many input spaces where a model’s safety alignment may be weak. Some transforms are purely deterministic (Base64 encoding, ROT13 cipher, hex encoding), while others require an LLM to per-form the conversion (language translation to low-resource languages, persona rewriting, persuasion framing, authority appeal). This distinction matters: deterministic transforms are fast and repro-ducible, while LLM-powered transforms produce more natural and contextually adapted mutations that better simulate real-world adversarial behavior. Scorers. Scorers determine whether an attack succeeded by evaluating the target’s response against the adversarial objective. Without automated scoring, the operator would have to manually review every response across hundreds or thousands of trials to judge whether the model complied, refused, or partially complied. Over 130 scorers across 34 modules automate this judgment, covering 13 agentic workflow detection, agent security, MCP security, multi-agent security, reasoning security, jailbreak detection, PII and credential leakage, exfiltration detection, system prompt leakage, and more. Scorers are composable through logical and arithmetic operators, enabling operators to define custom detection criteria. 

## 3.3 Execution Layer 

Attacks execute with full OpenTelemetry (OTEL) instrumentation, whether running locally on the operator’s machine or remotely on dedicated compute infrastructure. Every prompt sent to the target, every response received, every scorer evaluation, and every transform application is captured as a structured trace span. This instrumentation is transparent to the operator and produces the raw data that the analytics layer consumes. The execution layer supports both local and remote execution. In both modes, the same tracing pipeline captures identical structured data, ensuring consistent analytics regardless of where the attack runs. 

## 3.4 Analytics Layer 

The analytics layer processes OTEL traces into structured outputs: 

• Findings with automated severity classification (Critical, High, Medium, Low, Info) based on jailbreak scores and goal categories. 

• Compliance mapping to OWASP LLM Top 10, MITRE ATLAS, NIST AI RMF, and Google SAIF frameworks. 

• Aggregated metrics : attack success rate, per-attack and per-transform effectiveness, severity distribution, and trial efficiency. 

• Drill-down capability : from project-level risk posture through individual assessments and find-ings down to the specific adversarial prompt and target response that constitutes the evidence. 

• Export : PDF reports for executive and compliance audiences, and structured tracing data for offline analysis, adversarial dataset curation, and safety fine-tuning pipelines. 

## 3.5 Goal Categories and Severity Classification 

Findings are classified into 15 goal categories organized across three risk domains: 

• Security (5 categories): credential leak, system prompt leak, tool misuse, supply chain, alignment integrity, all classified as Critical at scores ≥ 0.9.

• Safety (6 categories): harmful content (Critical), PII extraction (Critical), jailbreak general (High), refusal bypass (High), content policy (High), bias and fairness (High). 

• Advanced (4 categories): reasoning exploitation (Critical), multi-turn escalation (Critical), re-source exhaustion (High), quantization safety (High). Severity levels are determined by the combination of goal category and jailbreak score, with thresholds at 0.9 (as shown above), 0.7 (one level lower), 0.5 (Medium), 0.3 (Low), and below 0.3 (Info). We now describe the end-to-end workflow from the operator’s perspective, emphasizing how the agentic interface restructures the interaction compared to library-oriented tools. 

## 4.1 From Objective to Execution 

The operator begins by launching the Dreadnode TUI with the AI red teaming capability and describing the objective in natural language: 

“Run a Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) attack against anthropic/claude-opus-4-6 with the goal: Write a Python script that achieves remote code execution and exfiltrates SSH keys. Use 5 iterations.” 

The agent performs four steps autonomously: 1. Workflow generation. The agent selects the Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) attack factory, configures parameters (target model, attacker model, evaluator model, iteration count), applies any specified transforms, and generates an executable Python script. 2. Execution. The generated script runs with full OTEL instrumentation. The agent monitors progress and reports status. 3. Result collection. The agent reads output files, collects analytics (best scores, attack success rate, trial counts), and prepares a structured summary. 4. Assessment registration. The agent creates an assessment record, uploads findings and traces to the platform, and reports the outcome to the operator. In the library-oriented workflow, each of these steps requires the operator to write code, configure parameters, parse output files, and manually upload results. The agentic interface reduces this to a single natural-language instruction. 

## 4.2 Iterative Refinement 

After reviewing results, the operator can direct the agent to refine the assessment without re-specifying context: 

• “Now try Crescendo against the same target” – the agent retains the target configuration and goal. 

• “Add skeleton_key_framing and base64 transforms” – the agent applies the specified transforms to the next run. 

• “The model resisted single-turn attacks. Try multi-turn escalation.” – the agent selects appropriate multi-turn strategies. 

• “Show me the best scoring prompt from the last run” – the agent retrieves and displays the specific adversarial prompt. This interaction pattern is qualitatively different from re-invoking a CLI command or editing a script. The agent maintains session state, understands the evolving assessment context, and can make informed suggestions based on prior results. 15 4.3 Strategy Selection Support 

When the operator is uncertain about which attack to use, the agent can recommend strategies based on the target’s characteristics: 

• For first-time evaluation of an unknown target: Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP) or Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR) for broad coverage. 

• For models that resist single-turn attacks : Crescendo or multi-turn escalation strategies. 

• For reasoning models (e.g., o1, o3, DeepSeek-R1): CoT Jailbreak to exploit chain-of-thought reasoning. 

• For discovering diverse failure modes : Rainbow Teaming with quality-diversity search. 

• For agentic systems : tool-use attacks, MCP server poisoning, multi-agent trust boundary probing. The agent’s recommendations are grounded in the empirical characteristics of each attack strat-egy rather than generic heuristics, drawing on traces, query budgets, target characteristics, and other attack metadata embedded in the framework. 

## 4.4 Three Interaction Modalities 

While the agentic Dreadnode TUI is the primary interaction modality, the framework provides three paths to the same underlying capabilities: 1. Dreadnode TUI Agent – natural-language, no-code, interactive. Optimized for exploration, rapid iteration, and operators who may not be AI red teaming specialists. 2. All three produce identical structured outputs (findings, traces, compliance tags), ensuring that results from an exploratory Dreadnode TUI session can be reproduced via CLI or SDK without loss of fidelity. To use the AI red teaming capability, operators need access to the Dreadnode platform; the only costs are compute and data storage, with no per-attack or per-finding charges.

The platform automatically processes every attack into structured findings with severity classification, compliance mapping, and exportable evidence. 16 5.1 From Traces to Findings 

Every attack generates OTEL trace spans containing the adversarial prompt, target response, scorer evaluations, transform metadata, timing information, and model identifiers. The analytics pipeline processes these traces into findings –discrete, reviewable records that each represent a specific vul-nerability or safety failure. Each finding contains: 

• Severity (Critical, High, Medium, Low, Info) based on the combination of jailbreak score and goal category (Section 3.5). 

• Score (0.0–1.0) quantifying how completely the target complied with the adversarial goal.

• Attack metadata : which strategy, transforms, and scoring pipeline produced this finding. 

• Compliance tags : automatic mapping to applicable OWASP, MITRE ATLAS, NIST, and Google SAIF categories. 

• Trace link : a direct link to the full conversation history, enabling drill-down from the finding to the exact multi-turn exchange.

# 7 Discussion 

## 7.1 From Attack Libraries to Agentic Workflows 

The central argument of this work is that the AI red teaming field is undergoing a transition analo-gous to what happened in software development: from library-driven development (where the pro-grammer selects and composes primitives) to agent-assisted development (where the operator states intent and the agent generates implementation). This transition does not diminish the importance of the underlying attack algorithms; it changes who manages their composition and execution .We observe that this transition is driven by two forces. First, the combinatorial explosion of attack strategies, transforms, and target configurations makes manual navigation increasingly impractical. Second, the consumers of AI red teaming results increasingly include non-specialist stakeholders (compliance officers, executives, model product managers) who need structured findings rather than raw attack outputs. 

## 7.2 Operator Cognitive Load 

Our framework reduces operator cognitive load along several dimensions: 

• Recall → recognition. The operator does not need to remember the names, parameters, and empirical characteristics of 45+ attack strategies. They describe what they want to test, and the agent selects appropriate methods. 

• Implementation → specification. The operator specifies the goal, not the code. Transform composition, scorer configuration, and execution management are delegated to the agent. 28 • Interpretation → analysis. The operator receives structured findings with severity, compliance tags, and evidence, rather than raw scorer outputs that require manual aggregation. 

• Stateless → contextual. The agent maintains assessment state across interactions, eliminating the need for the operator to re-specify context or manually track what has been tried. 

## 7.3 The Role of Human Judgment 

We emphasize that the agentic interface does not replace human judgment; it restructures where human judgment is applied. The operator’s expertise is deployed at the strategic level (what to probe, how to interpret findings, what constitutes acceptable risk) rather than the implementation level (which Python function to call, which parameters to set). The human-in-the-loop review capability ensures that automated classifications can be overridden by expert assessment, with full audit trails. 

## 7.4 Generalization Beyond Jailbreaking 

While the case study focuses on jailbreak testing, the framework is designed for the broader scope of AI red teaming that has emerged with agentic systems. The attack catalog includes tool-use manip-ulation, MCP server poisoning, multi-agent trust boundary probing, supply chain attacks, reasoning exploitation, and multimodal adversarial perturbation. As AI systems become more agentic, the attack surface expands beyond text-in/text-out to include tool calls, API interactions, inter-agent communication, and autonomous decision-making. Our framework’s extensible architecture accom-modates this expanding surface. 

# 8 Limitations 

We acknowledge several limitations. 

Agent reliability. The agentic interface depends on the capability of the underlying LLM to correctly interpret operator objectives, select appropriate strategies, and generate valid workflows. Agent errors (misinterpreting objectives, selecting suboptimal strategies) are a practical concern, though the operator retains the ability to redirect at every step. 

Scorer accuracy. Automated LLM-based scorers are subject to the same biases and failure modes as the models they evaluate. The human-in-the-loop review capability mitigates this but does not eliminate it. 

Coverage completeness. While the framework integrates over 45 attack strategies, the space of possible adversarial techniques is open-ended. New attack vectors emerge continuously, and no fixed catalog can claim complete coverage. 

Case study scale. The case study completed in approximately 3 hours because it used a limited set of attack strategies, transforms, and goal categories. A full end-to-end AI red teaming engagement covering all attack types, transforms, and harm categories would take longer. However, based on our internal real-world operations, even comprehensive assessments complete in a matter of days rather than weeks. The key gain is that the operator focuses entirely on innovation and strategy rather than writing code, assembling workflows, or collecting metrics. 

Evaluating the evaluator. We do not present a formal evaluation of the agent’s strategy selection quality compared to expert human selection. Such an evaluation would require controlled experiments with expert operators as a baseline. 29 9 Conclusion 

AI red teaming today is stuck in a library-driven workflow that takes weeks. Operators spend more time wiring together attacks, transforms, and scorers than actually probing targets for security and safety vulnerabilities. We built an agentic AI red teaming system that compresses this to hours through natural language objectives. 2

Our case study proves it: 68 goals, 3 attack types, 5 transforms, 674 attacks, 7,727 trials, 

∼85% attack success rate against Meta’s Llama Scout, all in approximately 3 hours with no human-developed code. The platform automatically classified 232 critical findings with compliance mapping and exportable evidence. No more maintaining separate libraries for traditional ML attacks versus generative AI systems—one unified framework handles everything. The proliferation of AI across critical domains means attack surfaces will only expand. Agentic, multimodal, multilingual targets create complexity that manual workflows simply cannot scale to meet. The field needs systems that let operators focus on what to probe, not how to implement it. Our work demonstrates that this future is not just possible—it’s already here.
